# Write the Behavior Plan in Observable "Looks Like" Terms With a Measurable Passing Bar

## Pattern

Effective behavior plans convert a broad goal ("be respectful," "stay on task") into three concrete components:

1. **An observable definition** — the target behavior is described as what a watching Guide would literally see ("looks like" language: body position, location, actions, words), so any staff member can judge whether the standard was met in a given moment.
2. **A taught replacement behavior** — the plan names what the student should do instead of the target behavior, not only what must stop.
3. **A fixed duration and measurable passing bar** — the plan runs for a defined window with a numeric success criterion (e.g., 12 of 15 days met; 5 consecutive days with a restart on a miss), so the student, Guides, and parents all know exactly when the plan is passed.

The observable definition makes the plan enforceable consistently across Guides; the numeric bar makes progress and completion a fact rather than a judgment call, and builds in tolerance for imperfect days rather than demanding perfection.

## Evidence

*1- Target areas defined as literal observables*

> "Laser focused learning looks like sitting or standing at his computer, headphones on or close by, eyes on screen, actively engaged in learning despite frustrations. Brave in my spot looks like sitting with the group during launch, not running away or leaving a situation when he is upset... Accepting feedback from any guide looks like making eye contact, repeating what they said (if applicable), and following the directions of that guide the first time asked."

Plan duration: 3 weeks; passing bar: 12/15 days met.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is treating the consequence system as the plan — issuing cards repeatedly for the same behavior, month after month, with no recorded observable target, replacement behavior, or passing bar. Where this occurs in the corpus, the documented behavior does not change.

*Identical infractions recur across months with only cards recorded*

One student's tracker records approximately twenty yellow/red cards across Sessions 3–5 for the same cluster of behaviors — e.g., "Screaming at computer and refusing to take break during core skills" (1/13), "Screamed out of frustration in core skills and refused to take break, lost privilege of going on field trip" (3/10), "throwing his headset for being upset" (5/29) — with no plan, replacement behavior, or passing criterion recorded at any point. A second student at the same campus escalated from repeated yellows to three red cards in one week to a strike ("Student received three red cards in one week for the same behaviors"), and the same behaviors continue in the record after the strike.

Note: the tracker documents only consequences, so undocumented coaching may have occurred; what the record shows is consequence repetition without a documented plan, alongside behavior persistence.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Translate → Replace → Measure → Bound process when drafting any behavior plan.

### 1. Translate the goal into "looks like" language

For each target area, write what a Guide would literally observe when the student is meeting the standard — position, location, actions, words. Test: could a substitute Guide who has never met the student judge a given moment against this definition?

### 2. Name the replacement, not just the prohibition

State what the student should do instead, specifically enough to practice (a phrase to say, a tool to pick up, a place to go).

### 3. Set a numeric passing bar

Choose a duration (2–3 weeks in the observed plans) and a days-met threshold that tolerates imperfection (12/15, 8/10) — or, for higher-stakes removals, a consecutive-days criterion with an explicit restart rule.

### 4. Bound and share the plan

Give the plan a start date, an end condition, and a tracker the student can see, so passing the plan is an achievement the student can work toward rather than an open-ended probation.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The student has been told repeatedly what to stop doing, and consequences are being applied."

The standard is:

> "Any Guide could observe the student for a day and score it against the plan, and the student knows exactly what passing looks like and when it happens."
