# Turn an Emotional Trigger Into a Reset-and-Return Routine

## Pattern

The Guide helps the student identify what triggers dysregulation, recognize the student’s early response, and choose a concrete routine for returning to productive action.

The purpose is not to eliminate frustration, disappointment, stress, or anxiety. The purpose is to prevent the emotion from automatically determining what happens next.

A complete regulation routine includes:

1. **Trigger**
   - The event that begins the emotional response.

2. **Signal**
   - What the student notices in thoughts, feelings, body, attention, or behavior.

3. **Reset**
   - A brief action that reduces escalation.

4. **Return**
   - The exact way the student re-enters the task.

5. **Review**
   - Evidence the student and Guide will use to determine whether the routine worked.

Validation alone is not enough.

A breathing exercise or break is also incomplete when there is no agreed path back to the challenge.

Related: This is the 1:1-meeting route into the same structure BL-074 installs at plan level. When the routine graduates from conversation to a written behavior plan, BL-074 defines the stronger practice.

## Evidence

*1- The student identifies the wrong-answer trigger and its effect on attention*

The Guide asks about strategies for improving reading accuracy:

> **Guide:** “Your reading accuracy is a little lower. What are some strategies that we can use to make sure we're accurate?”

The student identifies the emotional trigger:

> **Student:** “When you get one wrong, don't, like, be discouraged, because that's what happens to me.”

The student then describes the behavioral consequence:

> **Student:** “When I get one wrong, like, if I got the first one right, I'm like, yes. Then if I get the second one wrong, I, like, zone out.”

The Guide reflects the experience:

> **Guide:** “I get it, because it gets frustrating, and you kind of, like, just tune out.”

The conversation identifies the intended return behavior:

> **Guide:** “We got it wrong. We just keep going.”

This aligns with the pattern because the student connects a specific trigger—one incorrect response—to discouragement and disengagement, then names continuing rather than zoning out as the replacement response.

*2- The student links frustration to pace and chooses a slower, calmer execution condition*

The student explains:

> **Student:** “I actually need to go slower than what I need to do and then I get frustrated.”

The student identifies a possible response:

> **Student:** “So maybe I should do it slower.”

The Guide adds an environmental support:

> **Guide:** “Yeah, I think having the right environment, the right space... might be a great space for you to work out of because it's very zen. It's very relaxed, right?”

This aligns with the pattern because the conversation connects frustration to rushing and identifies two regulation supports:

- reducing pace;
- and using a calmer workspace.

Both supports are designed to help the student continue the task rather than escape it.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is moving from emotional and physical distress directly to compliance.

*Multiple possible triggers are raised, but no reset-and-return routine is established*

The student reports:

> **Student:** “It's just like I was feeling so sick.”

The student is uncertain whether the barrier is motivation or distraction:

> **Student:** “I don't know if it's like I'm not feeling motivated or I'm getting distracted. I don't know.”

The student also reports a technology problem:

> **Student:** “It's either it doesn't work or it does work, but it's super slow.”

The Guide responds:

> **Guide:** “It's for everybody, but other people are still working.”

The Guide then shifts to compliance:

> **Guide:** “What I need you to do is I need you to get on board with what everyone else is doing.”

The student presents several possible triggers:

- physical discomfort;
- low motivation;
- distraction;
- and system slowness.

The exchange does not establish a reset routine or a supported way to return to work. Comparison with peers replaces regulation coaching.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use **Trigger → Signal → Reset → Return → Review**.

### 1. Trigger

Ask:

> “What happened immediately before you shut down, rushed, argued, or stopped working?”

Keep the trigger observable and specific.

### 2. Signal

Ask:

> “What do you notice first when that happens?”

Possible signals include:

- zoning out;
- speeding up;
- rereading without understanding;
- feeling hot or tense;
- wanting to close the application;
- becoming argumentative;
- or telling oneself, “I cannot do this.”

### 3. Reset

Have the student choose one brief response:

- take three slow breaths;
- stand and stretch;
- drink water;
- move to a quieter space;
- slow the task pace;
- write down the frustrating thought;
- or ask for one precise form of help.

### 4. Return

Define the re-entry action:

> “After the reset, what is the first small action that puts you back into the work?”

Examples include:

- reread the question once;
- attempt the next item;
- show the Guide the error;
- work for another five minutes;
- or restart from the last successful step.

### 5. Review

Ask afterward:

> “Did the routine help you return, or did it only delay the task?”

Keep the parts that worked and revise the parts that did not.
