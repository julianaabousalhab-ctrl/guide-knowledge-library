# Move Regulation Upstream — Teach Signal Recognition and Pre-Select the Tools Before the Moment

## Pattern

Regulation support works when it is installed before the escalation, not invoked during it. The recurring structure across guides and campuses has three parts:

1. **Signal recognition** — the student is taught to notice their own early frustration indicators (body signs, feelings words) and to ask for help at that point, before the behavior becomes unsafe.
2. **A pre-agreed tool menu** — the student and Guide agree in advance on a small set of concrete regulation options (breathing, sand timer, calm corner, squeeze ball, a hug, a bounded break), so that in the hot moment the student is choosing among known tools rather than receiving a novel instruction.
3. **A daily preview** — a short morning check-in identifies feelings and likely hard moments for the day and rehearses which strategy the student will use, so the plan is loaded before it is needed.

The mechanism the corpus shows: in-the-moment directives to a dysregulated student ("take a break," "use your strategies") are frequently refused, while tools the student selected in advance — and can choose among — get used.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is running regulation reactively: managing each episode as it comes with quick in-the-moment interventions and directives, without teaching the signals or pre-agreeing the tools — until an escalation forces a crisis response.

*1- Reactive management holds until it doesn't*

> "Over the past several days we've noticed behaviors that have been unsafe, including kicking, pushing, and punching other students, staff, and school belongings. Until now, we've been able to manage these behaviors with quick interventions and brief conversations at pickup. Unfortunately, today his behavior escalated to a level of unsafety that cannot be permitted at school."

The Behavior Intervention Plan with signal recognition and tool menus (Evidence 1) was created only after this escalation produced a strike and suspension.

Source artifact:[Parent Comms](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1ZJW_l3cvEf4Uxz7vhYbcXIdG4KvQRH8XfL_WXIiqwoY/edit?usp=drive_link)

*2- In-the-moment directives to use strategies are refused*

A student's card record shows regulation invoked as a live directive rather than a pre-loaded plan, and refused repeatedly: "Refusing to use strategies to calm down" (5/8), "Refusing to use emotional regulation strategies and disrespecting guide" (5/12, two guides same day), "Not using his strategies" (5/18).

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Name → Menu → Preview → Prompt process.

### 1. Name the signals with the student

In a calm moment, help the student list their own early frustration indicators in their words ("mad face," "stomping feet," a hurting belly). The student's job at school becomes noticing the signal and asking for help at that point — that request is a win, not an interruption.

### 2. Build the menu together

Agree on 3–5 concrete tools, spanning in-place options (breathing, squeeze ball, timer) and bounded out-of-place options (calm corner, supervised room, max minutes stated). Post the menu visibly.

### 3. Preview daily

Run a 2–3 minute morning check-in: how are you feeling, what part of today might be hard, which tool will you reach for first. Load the plan before the day tests it.

### 4. Prompt at the signal, offer the menu

When early signs appear, don't issue a directive — offer the pre-agreed choices ("choice board time — which one?"). Choosing preserves the student's agency at exactly the moment dysregulation threatens it.

The coaching standard is not:

> "When the student melts down, Guides step in quickly and tell them how to calm down."

The standard is:

> "The student can name their own warning signs, the tools were agreed before they were needed, and the Guide's move at the first sign is to offer a choice, not give an order."
