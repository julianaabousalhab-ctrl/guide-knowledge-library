# After an Incident, Change the Conditions — Not Only the Student

## Pattern

When an incident closes with only a student-level consequence, the conditions that allowed it remain in place. The stronger incident responses in the corpus pair the individual consequence with a concrete change to supervision, environment, vendors, or rules — something that would have made the incident less likely regardless of which student was involved. Observed forms:

- **Supervision structure**: changing where adults are positioned during the risky window.
- **Physical environment**: securing, monitoring, or removing the hazard that the incident revealed.
- **Third-party systems**: changing what vendors or external providers supply.
- **Temporary scoped rules**: campus-visible restrictions with an owner, instructions for any staff member who observes a violation, and an expiry date — so the environmental control is enforceable by everyone and doesn't silently become permanent.

The test: if the same students were in the same situation tomorrow, what besides their memory of the consequence is different?

## Evidence

*1- A supervision change is part of the incident response*

After one student scratched another at lunch (guides were at a separate table):

> "Max received a red card and parents were contacted. Max has been coached to not be next to Hayden. Guides will be sitting at the same table as students during lunch from now on."

The structural change was also communicated to the parent as part of the action taken.

*2- The hazard revealed by the injury gets a monitoring commitment*

After a water bottle fell from a high cubby shelf and cut a student's head:

> "We will continue to monitor the cubby area to ensure items stored on higher shelves are secure. Carson will follow up with mom on the same day for updates on Jesse's condition."

Source artifact:[Alpha Incident Report](https://docs.google.com/document/d/14KQLF5v-YfWEIApKuAPauYAnW43wcMvjoR_A5IC0Lj4/edit?usp=drive_link)

*3- The vendor system is changed after an allergy scare*

After a student with a peanut allergy showed a possible reaction on a day peanuts were in the vendor lunch:

> "Following this incident, I asked Chris Ahrens to contact Sifted to ask that they not include peanuts in any further meals moving forward."

The companion report records the completed action: "Sifted requested to remove peanuts from their meals provided to NGA, communication is sent to parents."

Source artifacts:[Alpha Incident Report](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1s18HHxHNHrn6HUMuCLaoebcfR_mi99jGryys_FIJYxI/edit?usp=drive_link)

*4- A campus-level mechanism for temporary, scoped environmental rules*

One campus operates a standing "Daily Overview" tracker whose columns are Name, Umbrella, Expiry Date, Time of Day, Assignment/Rule, and Instructions — turning incident responses into enforceable, time-bounded environmental controls visible to all staff, e.g.:

> "Student is not allowed to play 'Tag' or 'Sharks and Minnows' or any other touch contact games today. | If observed, instruct the student they need to play something else and let Des know." — after he pushed a peer during a game.

> "Student will receive assigned solo seating during Core Skills, Check Chart, Workshops, and Van time. | If the student is observed sitting too closely with another student during learning blocks..., please instruct them to move to solo seating and notify Zijin." — after repeated boundary-crossing despite reminders.

Every rule carries an expiry date and instructions any observing staff member can execute.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is responding to a known risky behavior with warnings and conversations only — leaving the activity, access, and supervision unchanged — until the risk lands as an injury.

*Verbal warnings only, then a serious injury*

Two students had been practicing MMA chokeholds; after one lost consciousness, fell, and injured his ankle, the report acknowledges the prior response had been telling them to stop:

> "Both boys involved should receive a strike. We have had to tell them (especially Justin) not to be doing dangerous MMA moves at school."

The prior interventions were verbal; nothing in the record changed the students' unsupervised access to the side-wing space or established a monitored rule with staff-wide instructions before the injury forced the escalation.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Consequence → Conditions → Assign → Expire process when closing any incident.

### 1. Issue the student-level response

Card, strike, coaching, repair — per the behavioral model. This BrainLift adds to that step; it does not replace it.

### 2. Ask the conditions question

Before the report is closed: what about supervision, space, materials, vendors, or rules made this possible — and what single change would have prevented it? Write the change into the report's action section, not just the consequence.

### 3. Assign the change with staff-wide instructions

An environmental rule only works if every adult can enforce it. Specify the rule, the "if observed, do X and tell Y" instruction, and the owner — and put it where all staff see it.

### 4. Give it an expiry

Scope temporary restrictions with a review or expiry date, so controls are deliberately renewed or retired rather than accumulating indefinitely or fading silently.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The student was consequenced and warned not to do it again."

The standard is:

> "If the same situation arose tomorrow, something about the environment, supervision, or rules — enforceable by any staff member — would already be different."
