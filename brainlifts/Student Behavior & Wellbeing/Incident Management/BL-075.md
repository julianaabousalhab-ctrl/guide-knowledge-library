# Separate the Students and Take Each Account Individually, Then Corroborate With Uninvolved Witnesses

## Pattern

When an incident involves multiple students, involved students' first accounts are predictably incomplete: each reports the others' contributions and omits their own, minimizes to protect a friend, or justifies their action with claims no one else observed. The Guides producing reliable incident records use a consistent protocol:

1. **Separate** the involved students before questioning, so accounts cannot converge.
2. **Take each account individually**, capturing specific words.
3. **Corroborate with uninvolved witnesses**, questioned individually, before accepting any involved student's version of contested facts.
4. **Test consistency** — re-ask when details matter (especially injuries), and document changes between tellings rather than smoothing them over.

The reconstructed story is built from the overlap of independent accounts, not from the most confident single account. Where accounts conflict with witness evidence, the record says so explicitly.

## Evidence

*1- A justification claim is checked against individually questioned witnesses*

Max said he scratched Hayden "due to Hayden calling Sofia Stone names"; Hayden denied it. The Guide did not adjudicate on the involved students' words:

> "Whitney asked both Ariella and Sofia what they witnessed at lunch individually; Both shared they saw Max scratch Hayden, but did not hear any name calling or rude remarks from Hayden toward anyone. Guides also did not hear any name calling."

The parent communication then accurately reported both the claim and the witness result.

Source artifact:[Alpha Incident Report](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1ahUm1mpvN8GXAGPiJdBRQWseltBUHBwqISPkvfZmz0M/edit?usp=drive_link)

*2- Separation exposes what self-reports omit, and the eyewitness closes the gap*

In a four-student swing incident, the report documents the mechanism directly:

> "When students were separated and asked questions, they all reported how the other students were involved, but left out their own contributions. Between these self reported stories and the eye witness, the story became consistent/clear."

Source artifact:[Alpha Incident Report](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1OTijG0uIOUbcafbInqto2r8VI5dekQXF9t25pG5s7KI/edit?usp=drive_link)

*3- The other involved student is removed before the account is taken*

> "I asked what was wrong, he was holding his leg sitting on the ground cross legged. I had Justin leave the area and asked Zoe to explain exactly what happened."

Separate accounts were then collected from Zoe, Justin, and an uninvolved witness (Luca), which agreed on the core facts — including Justin's own statement against interest ("He actually did say we shouldn't do it again I pressured him into doing it").

Source artifact:[Alpha Incident Report](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1-LDom514iNoVIufowEIYAZ-DRJJpJrBo-g5CpurhwF4/edit?usp=drive_link)

*4- Re-asking surfaces inconsistencies, which are documented and disclosed*

> "Julian changed his story from the first time I asked him to the second time. Originally, he said that he hit his head on a pole on the playground, but the second time he said he hit it on one of the stepping stones on the playground. Additionally, when initially asked, he did not mention losing consciousness, but did bring it up half an hour later."

The Guide gave the parent the honest epistemic state rather than a cleaned-up story: "when I first asked him what happened, he didn't mention that... So I'm not 100% sure."

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is accepting the first, socially motivated account — especially a victim's minimizing account offered to protect a peer — without individual probing, and building the day's response on it.

*A minimizing account is accepted by multiple adults; intent surfaces only on later, deeper questioning*

Amelia reported that Luca "'accidentally' nicked her with a pencil," and repeated the same version to a second Guide at lunch and to the shuttle driver — the report itself records the motive: "From Amelia's parents' perspective, Amelia didn't want Luca to get in trouble." The account was carried through the day as given. Only after her parent messaged that night ("Apparently she had the Scramble Square that Luca wanted, and instead of telling Amelia, he used a pencil to hurt her hand") and "upon further discussion with Joshu Lezmi and Allyson Dudich," did Amelia state the action was intentional — at which point the incident was re-classified and a Strike issued.

Each adult asked casually and received the same protective story; no individual, probing account was taken until the parent prompted one.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Separate → Collect → Corroborate → Re-ask process.

### 1. Separate before questioning

Move involved students apart before anyone gives an account. If one student is injured or distressed, have another Guide hold the second student elsewhere.

### 2. Collect individual accounts in the student's words

Ask each student to explain exactly what happened; record specific phrases. Expect each account to be accurate about others and thin about self — that is data, not an obstacle.

### 3. Corroborate with the uninvolved

Identify students or staff who saw the event but weren't part of it; question them individually. Treat contested claims (provocation, intent, "it was an accident") as unresolved until checked against witnesses.

### 4. Re-ask where it matters, and document drift

For injuries and high-stakes claims, ask again after a gap. Record changes between tellings verbatim, and pass the uncertainty on honestly — to leadership and to parents — rather than presenting a single reconciled story.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The Guide asked the students what happened and wrote down the story."

The standard is:

> "Every involved student was heard separately, contested claims were checked against uninvolved witnesses, and the written record distinguishes what is corroborated from what is claimed."
