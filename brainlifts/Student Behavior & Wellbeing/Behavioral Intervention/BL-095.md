# Calibrate Consequences Across Guides — Same Behavior, Same Response

## Pattern

Students experience the behavioral model as fair only if the same behavior earns the same consequence regardless of which Guide is watching. Where standards drift between Guides, students track the inconsistency precisely — and it corrodes both behavior and culture: students test boundaries, perceive cards as personal, and lose pride in the level. The converged practice:

1. **Calibrate as a guide team** — review the behavioral standards together and align on what specific behaviors earn, so "the same behaviors have the same consequences across guide groups."
2. **Make the rule objective where drift is worst** — convert judgment calls into defined rulings ("if you remind someone and they have to be reminded again, it's an immediate yellow card. This is not subjective").
3. **Set it early and hold it** — establish the rules and consequences at the start of the year and keep them stable, rather than negotiating case by case.

This complements BL-077 (the student classifies their own behavior at the posted standard): self-classification only works if the standard means the same thing at every Guide's hands.

## Evidence

*1- A student names the drift; the guide team recalibrates*

> "He stated a recurring frustration that yellow-card standards feel inconsistent from guide to guide (he got more yellows than oth[ers])..." → "Reviewed behavioral standards with the guide team to ensure the same behaviors have the same consequences across guide groups."

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is letting each Guide run their own threshold — so the consequence a student receives depends on who saw the behavior, and students experience discipline as arbitrary.

*Inconsistent discipline named as a driver of a student's worst year*

> "Beau faced a lot of disciplinary action (yellows, reds, strikes) due to tantrums, an inability to emotionally regulate, and inconsistent behavior discipline from guides. He did not take feedback well and would often blow up after receiving a card (once earning a second one on the same day due to his reaction about the first)."

The guide's fix names the missing element: "Beau needs a very clear system from S1-S5 to hold the behavioral standard for him; his guide must discuss this with him at the beginning of the year."

The David Nolan record (Evidence 1) shows the cultural cost even for a well-regulated student: perceived guide-to-guide inconsistency fed a drop in "his sense of community and pride in the group's culture" that "never recovered."

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Define → Calibrate → Publish → Audit process.

### 1. Define behaviors, not vibes

For the highest-friction behaviors (ignoring feedback, physical contact, disruption), write the ruling in observable terms with the consequence attached — the "repeat rule" format: condition → consequence, no discretion clause.

### 2. Calibrate the guide team

Walk the guide team through the defined rulings with real recent cases: would each of us have issued the same card? Resolve disagreements in the meeting, not in front of students.

### 3. Publish once, early

Set the standards at the start of the year (with students, per BL-077's posted-standard mechanism) and hold them stable across sessions; changes go through a deliberate process, not per-incident drift.

### 4. Audit the distribution

Periodically review cards by guide and by student. A student collecting multiples of what peers receive for similar behavior is either a genuine outlier needing a plan (BL-073) — or the calibration signal David Nolan raised.

The coaching standard is not:

> "Each Guide enforces the behavioral model as they judge best."

The standard is:

> "Any Guide watching the same behavior would issue the same response — and students, asked privately, would agree the cards don't depend on who's watching."
