# Walk the Student to the Posted Standard and Let Them Classify Their Own Behavior

## Pattern

Instead of announcing the consequence as a verdict, the Guide brings the student to the posted behavioral expectations (the yellow/red card chart, the community standards) and asks the student to classify their own behavior against it — and, where appropriate, to propose the consequence. Recurring features:

- The student **names the violation and the matching consequence themselves** ("he decided for himself a yellow card was an appropriate penalty"), which converts the consequence from something done to them into something they endorse.
- Ownership is **noticed and sometimes rewarded**: when a student self-classifies honestly and immediately, Guides have reduced the secondary cost while keeping the primary consequence — making honesty cheaper than denial without making the behavior free.
- Students sometimes **propose consequences calibrated to the harm**, tighter than the default (a fine of one Alpha per dishonestly attempted XP).

The mechanism: the standard, not the Guide, becomes the authority; the argument shifts from "Guide vs. student" to "behavior vs. posted rule," which lowers defiance and builds the student's ability to self-police.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is delivering the card as a pronounced verdict in the heat of the moment, with no step where the student measures their own behavior against the standard. In the corpus, verdict-delivery to an already-escalated student repeatedly produces a defiance spiral in which the announcement itself triggers the next card.

*The announcement becomes the escalation*

> "Disrespctfully saying 'I don't care!' when being told he got the 1st yellow card." → same day: "Disrespectfully yelling 'I don't care!' after being told he got a 2nd yellow card. He was then told he got a red card and said the same thing."

The same loop appears with another student at the same campus: "yelling 'I don't care!' when receiving a yellow card, and talking/interru[pting]..." — three yellows to red, three reds in a week to strike, with no recorded self-assessment step at any rung.

Note: these tracker rows record the card deliveries, not every conversation around them; what the record shows is verdict-style delivery co-occurring with immediate escalation, in contrast to the ownership outcomes where self-classification is documented.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Pause → Chart → Classify → Price process.

### 1. Pause before pronouncing

When the behavior warrants a card, resist announcing it immediately unless safety requires. The goal of the next two minutes is the student's own classification, not your announcement.

### 2. Go to the chart together

Physically bring the student to the posted expectations. Ask them to find where their behavior sits: "Look at the chart — what was that, and what does it earn?"

### 3. Let the student classify — and accept honest answers

If the student names the violation and consequence accurately, that is the consequence — even if they classify it more severely than you would have. If they minimize, walk through the definitions rather than overruling by authority.

### 4. Price honesty below denial

Keep the primary consequence, but visibly reduce a secondary cost (tokens, fines) when the student owns it immediately — and say why, so the whole level learns that ownership is the cheaper path.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The Guide told the student which card they earned and why."

The standard is:

> "The student, standing at the posted standard, named what they did and what it earns — and left owning the consequence rather than fighting the verdict."
