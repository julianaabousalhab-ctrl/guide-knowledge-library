# Communicate the Student’s Trajectory

## Pattern

Show how the student’s performance or behavior changed over time, rather than reporting only the final status.

Strong parent-facing progress communication includes:

1. Earlier state: Where the student started or what was happening initially.
2. Change or intervention: What shifted, or what support influenced the change.
3. Current direction: What the student is now doing more consistently.

This pattern is about explaining movement already observed. It is distinct from setting a measurable checkpoint, which defines how future progress will be evaluated.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide reports a session-wide pattern, identifies the condition behind the weaker weeks, and clarifies the student’s current direction.*

>“[Student] had a wonderful Session 1, earning 100% in Core Skills for six out of the eight weeks. On the weeks she didnʼt meet her minimums, we discovered that a lack of sleep impacted her focus and attention during Core Skills time.”

The parent can see both the overall success rate and the pattern separating successful weeks from unsuccessful ones.

Source artifact: [Session Snashot](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1VTubRJLENKro30phfpa-eDtmJGPEbOG7w5-6abeC9bk/edit?usp=drive_link)

*2- The Guide contrasts the student’s early difficulty with the improvement that followed more frequent support.*

>“At the beginning of the session, I was not able to help [Student] accurately track her XP, which made it difficult for her to know what needed to be completed each day. As we began having more frequent check-ins to review her tracker, [Student] responded well to feedback and finished strong — hitting all of her minimums over the last three weeks of the session.”

The comment shows a clear sequence: inaccurate tracking, increased check-ins, and three weeks of meeting minimums.

Source artifact: [Session Snashot](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1xT_pEg6Vlm3AsHKK7auuDdyUr7IJu59SZt8S469yDzc/edit?usp=drive_link)

*3- The Guide explains why the full-session average understates the student’s recent performance and identifies the point when the trend changed.*

>“[Student]ʼs XP averages do not fully reflect his progress this session. His averages are brought down from a slow start first few weeks because at first I was not able to help him accuratley track how much he needed to accomplish. [Student] recieved feedback extremely well midway through the session and proceeded to track has data properly as well as hit his 2x goals the last few weeks of the session.”

The parent receives a more accurate interpretation of the cumulative data because the Guide distinguishes the early weeks from the student’s recent performance.

## Anti-Pattern

*Using broad progress language without showing the starting point, change, or time period.*

>“[Student] has surpassed her minimum daily XP in every subject and has been consistent in properly tracking her data. She is at or above grade level in every subject other than Writing, where she is still growing. Overall, she is making clear strides academically and building strong learning habits.”

“Making clear strides” suggests progress, but the comment does not show what changed, when it changed, or how current performance compares with an earlier point.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Then → Change → Now check before submitting a progress comment:

 - Then: What was happening earlier?
 - Change: What intervention, realization, or condition shifted?
 - Now: What is the student doing differently, and for how long?

Use this frame:

>“At the beginning of [time period], [observable starting point]. After [intervention or change], [Student] began [new behavior] and has now [current result over a defined period].”

Replace vague phrases such as “showing growth” or “making progress” with a comparison the parent can verify.
