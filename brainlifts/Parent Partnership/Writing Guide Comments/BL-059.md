# Use Evidence Unique to the Student

## Pattern

Ground every parent-facing Guide Comment in evidence that could belong only to that student. Strong evidence includes an exact student quote, a specific interaction, a named interest or motivator, or an observable behavior connected to the Guide’s interpretation.

The comment should pass the **Parent Test**: if the student’s name were removed, the parent should still recognize their child. Generic praise such as “hard-working,” “positive,” or “a joy” is not enough without proof.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide uses the student’s own words, a precise interest, and the observed impact of the coaching approach.*

>“I think you set high standards for me, but Iʼm also not worried youʼre going to make me feel bad about myself when I fail. I appreciate that.” [Student] and I have built a strong connection through our shared love of sports — especially his passion for the New Orleans Saints. Using a sports mindset has helped [Student] hold himself accountable and set high goals, which has translated into him hitting his minimums and excelling in workshops. As he put it, “I donʼt know you well enough yet to say you inspire me as much as my parents do.”

Source artifact: [Session Snapshot](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1RLVtk4TIYjM9v1cD5DU2BJH0BCORR5JboyOJrjfevfQ/edit?usp=drive_link)

*2- The Guide combines a direct quote with a specific student response to a real school system.*

>“[Student] shows a strong love of school and thrives most in workshops, where her creativity and drive shine. She said, ‘I love this school because we can do fun stuff, but we still learn a lot,’ a reflection of her appreciation for Alphaʼs unique balance of rigor and freedom. While the new tracking system has been confusing at times, [Student] advocates strongly for herself and offers thoughtful, solution-oriented feedback.”

Source artifact: [Session Snapshot](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1c5ZwDYT6KeMrXOSBcqHzQcvFSqjU6FVwNl1gcK1TzZA/edit?usp=drive_link)

*3- The Guide names several individual interests and connects them to both motivation and a specific growth context.*

>“In a Limitless Meeting, [Student] shared, ‘I feel like Alpha is fun and it helps me focus better.’ Iʼve gotten to know his favotire things - from Percy Jackson books to sushi to soccer and FIFA - and these insights help me connect his learning to what excites him most. I have not yet fully helped [Student] internalize what it looks like to hold both himself and his peers to high standards during core skills—especially when working near close friends.”

## Anti-Pattern

*Claiming deep knowledge of the student without showing the parent the evidence.*

>“I have worked really hard to get to know [Student] this year and to create a safe, supportive, welcoming, and predictable space for her within my Guide Group. She has already set high standards for herself, not necessarily because of anything I've said but because that's who she is. However, because of the relationship we've built, I believe she is willing to push herself and try new things because she knows she has support and understanding behind her. I understand [Student] very well, and we regularly work together to come up with strategies to help her get through harder moments when needed. She is a joy, and I am so thankful to be her Guide.”

The comment says the Guide understands the student, but it does not include a student quote, named interest, specific difficult moment, or concrete strategy. Much of the wording could apply to many students.

Source artifact: [Session Snapshot](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1Ef-WGwms5UdGNqgeOYsghBs-OqH-Bl_6beKkKyuIS40/edit?usp=drive_link)

## Reusable Coaching Move

Run the Name-Swap Test before submitting the comment:

Temporarily remove the student’s name.

1. Ask: “Could this describe another student?”
2. When the answer is yes, add at least one piece of unique proof:
3.  An exact student quote
   - A named interest, fear, or motivator
   - A specific observed moment or recurring behavior
   - A concrete example of how the Guide used that knowledge

Then explain why the evidence matters. Do not merely list an interest; connect it to the student’s motivation, progress, or next support.

## Confidence: Medium — 3 instances + 1 anti-pattern case, single snapshot corpus; no parent-response data to confirm impact.
