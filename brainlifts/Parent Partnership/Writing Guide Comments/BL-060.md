# Take Guide Ownership of the Gap

## Pattern

When a student is not yet meeting a commitment, describe the gap as a shared outcome that the Guide has not yet unlocked.

Strong parent-facing comments identify the support, structure, strategy, or motivational lever the Guide has not yet provided effectively. This communicates accountability without blaming the student or lowering expectations.

This pattern is about who owns the gap, not the order of the comment. The Guide should be able to say: “Here is what I have not yet built, discovered, or delivered—and how I will adjust.” The standards explicitly require Guides to own outcomes and avoid language that presents the challenge as a student flaw.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide acknowledges that the current approach emphasized outcomes without providing enough supportive structure.*

>“I have not yet shown [Student] how pairing high standards with high support can elevate her confidence and learning experience. My focus has been more on outcomes than on modeling how feedback and structure lead to growth with less frustration.”

This makes the Guide’s approach—not the student’s personality—the focus of the diagnosis.

*2- The Guide owns both the missing motivational lever and the ineffective use of an existing tracking strategy.*

>“I have not yet helped [Student] balance his time across all subjects in the mornings. While he is ahead in half of his subjects, I need to find a motivation that helps him stay consistently focused. In recent weeks we created a tracker for him to see exactly what needs to be accomplished each day, but I have not helped him use it effectively yet.”

The Guide does not simply say that the student fails to track. The comment acknowledges that the Guide has not yet made the system effective for this student.

*3- The Guide directly owns insufficient academic support rather than assigning the Writing gap entirely to the student.*

>“In writing, I did not provide enough support to guide [Student] through retaking his writing exams. As we move into Session 2, I will strengthen our academic tracking so [Student] has clear visibility into what needs to be completed to make steady progress in writing.”

The language gives the parent a clear account of what the Guide did not deliver and what will change.

## Anti-Pattern

*Explaining the gap primarily through the student’s motivation, priorities, or willingness to apply themselves.*

>“[Student] is a very bright student who is performing at grade level in nearly all areas, and he is actually ahead of grade level in math. His strong ability in this subject highlights just how capable he is academically. When he applies himself and focuses, he has the skills to excel across the board.
The challenge for [Student] is engagement — his schoolwork is often not high on his list of priorities, and he becomes easily distracted.”

Phrases such as “when he applies himself” and “not high on his list of priorities” place the explanation inside the student. The comment mentions trying different supports, but it does not identify which Guide strategy has failed or what specific lever remains undiscovered.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Run the Ownership Rewrite before submitting a comment.

When a sentence begins with:
  - “The student does not…”
  - “The student refuses…”
  - “The student is unmotivated…”
  - “When the student applies themselves…”
  - “The student just needs to…”

Ask:

>“What structure, strategy, support, relationship lever, or motivation have I not yet made effective?”

Rewrite the diagnosis using Guide-owned language:

>“I have not yet built a structure that helps [Student] sustain focus across the full block.”

Then name the adjustment the parent should expect:

>“In Session 2, I will test a daily visual tracker and review it with [Student] at the end of each morning.”

## Confidence: Medium — 3 instances + 1 anti-pattern case, single snapshot corpus; requirement is also normative per the standards doc.
