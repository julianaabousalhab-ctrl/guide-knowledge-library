# Prioritize One High-Leverage Growth Area

## Pattern

Select the single growth area most likely to improve the student’s overall outcome, then keep the diagnosis and next step centered on that priority.

A Guide Comment may acknowledge several strengths or contextual factors, but it should not give every subject, concern, and possible intervention equal weight. Parents should finish the comment knowing:

  - The most important gap
  - Why that gap matters
  - The focused action the Guide will take

This pattern concerns prioritization, not comment order or Guide ownership. The standard requires comments to be “dense, not long,” with every word earning its place.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide acknowledges success across five subjects, then centers the comment on the one remaining academic gap: Writing.*

>“I have helped [Student] excel academically, with Math, Reading, Language, Vocabulary, and Math Fluency all at 2X. These are clear wins that show her focus, consistency, and ability to thrive with high standards. I have not yet helped her bring the same drive to Writing, where procrastination and delayed starts hold her back. The next step is to strengthen her writing routine — planning ahead and finishing early — so she matches her success in other subjects.”

The strengths provide context, but the parent is left with one clear growth area and one connected response.

Caveat: "procrastination and delayed starts hold her back" locates part of the explanation inside the student — under BL-060's Ownership Rewrite, a stronger phrasing would be "I have not yet built a writing routine that gets her started early." The example is retained here because its prioritization move (one growth area, one response) is the behavior this BrainLift evaluates.

*2- The Guide uses broader academic performance as context while keeping Reading as the clear priority.*

>“[Student] has consistently hit all of her minimums, falling just a few points shy in reading. At the beginning of the year, reading was a real challenge, but once we placed her in the proper grade level she was able to reach her XP marks without issue. She tracks her data carefully and with no problems, which has allowed her to stay on pace academically. My action plan is to keep building [Student]’s confidence in reading now that she is properly leveled so her growth accelerates throughout the year.”

The comment does not introduce unrelated academic concerns. Every detail supports the parent’s understanding of the Reading priority.

*3- The Guide identifies Reading as the priority and keeps each proposed strategy tied to comprehension.*

>“I have not yet provided [Student] with the guidance needed to find full success in reading this session. Moving forward, I will focus on helping her understand that higher-grade-level content requires increased discipline and focus. It will be important to coach [Student] in re-reading directions and passages to ensure she fully understands what is being asked. Strengthening her ability to use context clues will also be essential for improving her reading comprehension and overall confidence.”

Although the Guide names multiple reading strategies, they all serve one coherent growth area rather than competing priorities.

## Anti-Pattern

*Turning the comment into an inventory of subjects, exceptions, transitions, strengths, and possible incentives without identifying the most important growth area.*

>“[Student] is a highly motivated and consistent learner who makes excellent use of his Timeback Learning sessions. For every subject that L1 focused on during session 1, Math, Reading, Language, and Math Fluency, [Student] has learned 2x. The two blank boxes, Writing and Vocabulary, are subjects that we will be focusing on in later sessions. He has the occassional day where he does not get his minimums for a subject or two--usually due to misunderstandings about how XP is measured (by units, not time) or small oversights, such as forgetting to bring his computer home or the added challenge of a new app. In the last few weeks, [Student] has leveled up from Lalilo to MobyMax for Language which would account for the few days he has just missed his minimum XP goals. He has told me he believes his Reading app, Amplify, is too easy. Because Amplify covers language skills and reading, we have transitioned [Student] to ClearFluency, which focuses solely on reading fluency, to strengthen his literacy skills. This was to remedy his view of the Reading app not being challenging enough and to increase his Reading fluency level. Overall, he consistently strives to meet his daily minimum goals. His persistence, strong time management, and ability to stay focused highlight his growth and reliability as a learner. Looking forward to next session, I would like to give [Student] more specific motivational models for reaching his goals more consistently, such as providing incentives like a group lunch.”

The comment contains valuable information, but several topics compete for attention: subject availability, XP measurement, forgotten materials, a Language transition, Reading placement, literacy fluency, daily minimums, and incentives. The parent cannot easily identify the highest-leverage growth priority.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the One-Gap Filter before submitting the comment:

1. List every concern you could mention.
2. Ask: “Which one, if improved, would create the greatest change in this commitment?”
3. Make that issue the comment’s primary growth area.
4. Keep only evidence and strategies that directly explain or address it.
5. Move unrelated details to another section or omit them.

Use this sentence frame:

>“Although [Student] is succeeding in [brief strength], the highest-leverage growth area is [one priority]. I will address it by [one coherent Guide response].”

A parent should be able to name the comment’s priority after one read.
