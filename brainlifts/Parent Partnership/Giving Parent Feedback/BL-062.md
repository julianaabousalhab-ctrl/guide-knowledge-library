# Describe Observable Behavior, Not Character

## Pattern

Describe what the student did, said, completed, or repeatedly demonstrated rather than assigning a character label, motive, or fixed trait.

Strong parent-facing feedback uses details a Guide could see, hear, count, or verify. This allows the Guide to communicate concerns honestly while preserving the student’s dignity and giving the parent information they can trust.

This pattern is distinct from using evidence unique to the student. BL-059 focuses on personalization; BL-062 focuses on neutral, non-judgmental framing. The standards require observable behaviors and warn against opinions, vague adjectives, and language that treats a gap as a student flaw.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide describes the tracking problem, the intervention, and the student’s measurable response without labeling the student as careless or unmotivated.*

>“At the beginning of the session, I was not able to help [Student] accurately track her XP, which made it difficult for her to know what needed to be completed each day. As we began having more frequent check-ins to review her tracker, [Student] responded well to feedback and finished strong — hitting all of her minimums over the last three weeks of the session.”

The comment reports the observable difficulty, the change in support, and the three-week result. It does not describe the student as disorganized or irresponsible.

*2- The Guide supports the description of leadership with specific actions and accomplishments.*

>“She was the first to complete her Back-to-School Bingo chart, took on a leadership role with her boat crew in the Fast and the Floatiest workshop, and earned one of the top scores in public speaking by staying calm under pressure. In the community, she is active in Town Hall and even ran for Student Council, just missing the cut but already planning to run again.”

Instead of relying only on a label such as “natural leader,” the comment shows the parent the actions that support that conclusion.

Source artifact: [Session Snapshot](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1PrTVhIYnYprBqVAhq_21mX_NM3RhlNnNU3I3c700NKg/edit?usp=drive_link)

*3- The Guide communicates an academic gap through verifiable performance, app access, and testing information rather than judging the student’s effort or ability.*

>“Although [Student] has achieved 100% in coreskills each week, I have not successfully provided enough time to work in each app. [Student] increased her output in Vocabulary to cover her Language XP when she was missing app content due to testing delays or placement testing. In writing, [Student] has been working on completing her grade 3 Language test which will unlock her writing app.”

The parent receives concrete information about performance, allocation of work, and app access without the student being characterized as weak, avoidant, or unfocused.

## Anti-Pattern

*Using character judgments or assumed motives to explain observable behavior.*

>“[Student] is brilliant and shows that he can excel academically when he chooses to lock in. He sometimes makes excuses to avoid work, like saying his tooth hurts or that he doesnʼt like the school water, but Iʼve seen how powerful the right motivator can be — when boba is on the line, [Student] shows impressive focus and productivity.”

The phrases “when he chooses to lock in” and “makes excuses to avoid work” infer intention. The same concern could be communicated more credibly by reporting the frequency, conditions, and visible behavior—for example, opening unrelated tabs, talking during Core Skills, or completing more work when a short-term incentive is established.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Camera Test before submitting parent feedback:

>“Could a camera record what I wrote?”

Replace judgments with three pieces of information:

1. Observable action: What did the student do or say?
2. Context and frequency: When and how often did it happen?
3. Impact: How did it affect progress or participation?

Replace:

> “[Student] is lazy and makes excuses.”

With:

> “On three mornings this week, [Student] opened unrelated tabs during Core Skills and completed fewer than half of the planned tasks.”

A parent should receive evidence they can understand—not a verdict about who their child is.
