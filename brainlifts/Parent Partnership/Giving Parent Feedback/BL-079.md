# Deliver the Consequence With the Stakes, the Plan, and the Belief in One Message

## Pattern

When a serious behavioral consequence (strike, suspension) must be communicated to the student's family, the strong messages in the corpus never deliver the verdict alone. They carry five elements in a single communication:

1. **The facts and the consequence**, stated plainly — what happened, what was issued.
2. **The stakes, named without euphemism** — where the student now sits in the behavioral model and what the next escalation would be ("He now has 2 strikes left — if he were to reach a third, expulsion would be the next step").
3. **The forward plan, specific and already underway** — not "we'll address it" but the named work-off plan, behavior intervention plan, or support structure, with its content.
4. **Evidence-based belief** — genuine positives from the record (ownership shown, growth observed that same day), so the belief reads as observation rather than consolation.
5. **The partnership ask** — what the school needs from home, and an invitation to talk.

The mechanism: parents receiving only a verdict must choose between defending their child and accepting the school's judgment. Parents receiving verdict + stakes + plan + belief are being recruited into a project that is already moving — the observed conversations end in alignment, even after initial pushback.

## Evidence

*1- Strike letter with work-off plan and evidence-based positive framing*

> "During the Financial Feud Test 2 Pass, guides observed Max and Hayden communicating to relay answers to each other during the assessment. ... we addressed it through our 'working' Strike process. The positive side of this is that Max was receptive to the conversation and open to feedback. We're using this as a growth opportunity to build his confidence in asking for help and communicating proactively when he doesn't understand something. In Session 4, he'll work off the Strike by practicing stronger communication with guides and taking ownership of getting support when needed."

The parallel letter to the second family carries the same structure with a student-specific plan: "he'll work off the Strike through a leadership-focused plan during sports workshops, intentionally coaching and supporting peers rather than simply giving answers. ... We really appreciate Hayden's willingness to take ownership and grow from the experience."

He now has 2 strikes left — if he were to reach a third, expulsion would be the next step. ... The full plan (signal recognition, intervention choices, sticker system, consequences) is included in the same message, closing with: "If he cannot follow it safely, we may need to talk about other school options, but I believe in him and want to work together with you to help him thrive."

*3- In-person consequence delivery with the plan attached survives strong pushback*

After a strike for a shoving injury, the family meeting covered "the incident that happened, and the plan moving forward for Luca" — the report specifies the plan's content: "An individualized behavioral plan will be created for Luca, and will be implemented upon his return to school on 9/10/25. Luca will be required to make a public apology to Benjamin, and adhere to his improvement plan." The parents pushed back hard ("for a 6 year old who misses his parents, spending a day home from school is more of a reward than a punishment"), yet:

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is letting the communication thin out precisely when severity rises — issuing consequences while the family experiences silence between them, so support and plan arrive only after the relationship has been damaged.

*The school's own apology documents the cost*

The second strike notice for the same student opens with a repair:

> "First, I want to apologize. Patricio shared with me that you've felt communication has been too low with such a high-priority situation, and I want to make sure that not only is Angel supported, but that you feel supported as a family in our community."

The corrective content that follows is exactly the missing plan-and-support half of the pattern: counselor outreach ("David Cooney, our counselor, will be reaching out... to set up a meeting with you and to share resources"), and named plan adjustments ("Shorter sprints of learning time with frequent breaks... Motivational rewards tied to his passions (including new Messi stickers)"). The verdict-and-stakes half had been delivered throughout; the family's experience of under-communication arose in the gap where plan, resources, and partnership should have been.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Verdict → Stakes → Plan → Belief → Ask checklist before sending any consequence communication.

### 1. State the verdict on facts

What was observed, what consequence was issued, and the immediate logistics (suspension dates, return date).

### 2. Name the stakes without euphemism

Locate the student in the model explicitly — strikes remaining, what the next escalation is. Parents should never later say they didn't understand how serious the situation was.

### 3. Attach the plan, already in motion

Describe the specific work-off plan, intervention plan, or added supports — with content, not labels. If a plan doesn't exist yet, the message isn't ready to send.

### 4. Ground the belief in evidence

Include a genuine, specific positive from the record: ownership shown, receptiveness to feedback, growth observed the same day. Skip generic reassurance — the specificity is what makes it credible.

### 5. Make the ask and open the channel

State what home can reinforce, and invite the conversation. As severity rises, increase communication frequency deliberately — a family in a high-stakes situation should hear from the school between consequences, not only at them.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The parents were informed of the strike and its consequences."

The standard is:

> "The family left the message knowing exactly where their child stands, what the school is already doing about it, why there is real reason for hope, and what to do next — and heard from us again before the next consequence, not at it."
