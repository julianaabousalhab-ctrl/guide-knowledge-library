# Explain Context, Then State the Guide Action

## Pattern

When testing delays, late app access, placement changes, absences, or scheduling issues affect a student’s reported results, briefly explain the relevant context and then state what the Guide will do in response.

Context should help the parent interpret the data accurately—not excuse the outcome or replace a next step. Strong feedback answers two questions:

1. What circumstance affected the result?
2. What will the Guide do now?

This pattern is distinct from prioritizing one growth area. It focuses on converting necessary background information into a clear, Guide-owned response. The standards call for honest explanations of what is and is not working, followed by a concrete go-forward plan.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide explains how testing and assignment delays affected the reported averages, then sets an exact academic target.*

>“[Student] earned an average of 113 XP per day, just under our expectation of 125. This number does not account for standardized testing or limitless meeting credits, which I believe would put her over, if added.
[Student] faced several delays in writing tests being graded or lessons assigned, which is why she is just below our expectation of 25 XP per day in that subject. Going into next session, I will ensure she earns 3 more XP in both math and reading for a total of 25 XP in each.”

The context clarifies how the parent should interpret the data, while the final sentence makes the Guide’s response measurable.

*2- The Guide explains late course access and then changes the student’s learning environment to address the remaining gaps.*

>“[Student] was not rostered in a Language course until Week 5 of Session 1 but made up his XP through consistent effort in Vocabulary. He is also rostered in Science, where he averages 26 XP per day — a strong indicator of his work ethic once heʼs in a rhythm. My focus next session is to help [Student] maintain that same consistency across all subjects now that his full schedule is set. A coaching point for [Student] will be putting him in a proper spot to focus during his reading and writing sections.”

The Guide does not stop after explaining the roster issue. The comment identifies the next condition the Guide will change.

*3- The Guide explains why some subject averages are incomplete, then names two concrete monitoring actions.*

>“In terms of daily XP, [Student] exceeded our expectations of 125 XP by earning an average of 138. He did not have writing and language for the entire session, so he did not have the opportunity hot those expected averages. [Student] overindexed slightly in reading and vocab, so I will coach him to spread his work across all apps to hit his minimums before going over in any single subject. I have not yet shown [Student] the benefit of daily tracking, and will check in with him at the end of each morning in S2 to compare his recorded points to Timeback.”

The parent receives both an explanation of the data limitation and a specific picture of how the Guide will respond.

## Anti-Pattern

*Providing several contextual explanations without stating what the Guide will change.*

>“[Student] has triple the minimum XP in Math and completed Math Fluency, often making up that XP in Math. He had weeks without a Writing subject while waiting for tests to be administered, which has caused his Writing to be slightly below the daily minimum. He just passed an 8th-grade Reading test, which now allows him to access more Reading lessons to help him hit his averages.”

The information helps explain the numbers, but the comment ends without identifying the Guide’s next action, monitoring plan, or target. The parent knows why the result occurred but not what support to expect.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Context-to-Action Check before submitting parent feedback:

1. Name the circumstance: What testing, placement, access, attendance, or scheduling issue affected the result?
2. Explain the impact: Which specific metric or behavior did it affect?
3. State the Guide action: What will the Guide change, monitor, or provide next?
4. Set a checkpoint: When possible, include a target or review point.

Use this frame:

>“Because [relevant circumstance], [Student]’s [specific result] was affected. In [timeframe], I will [Guide action], with success measured by [target or observable outcome].”

Context earns its place only when it helps the parent interpret the result and understand what happens next.
