# 6.2.1 Student Ownership — Scope Log

## In

- **BL-045:** Create a reliable student agenda-submission pipeline and assign a student owner to every topic.
- **BL-046:** Give students bounded leadership roles with genuine authority, not ceremonial titles.
- **BL-047:** Transfer implementation and recurring operations to student-owned roles.

These were included because they describe distinct Guide actions across the full ownership cycle: students originate the work, lead the meeting process, and carry decisions into implementation.

## Out

- **Student speaking percentage as a pattern:** Useful as a diagnostic, but it is an outcome measure rather than a Guide behavior.
- **Having a student mayor or president:** Not included on its own because a title may be ceremonial. It was incorporated into BL-046 only when the role carried real authority.
- **Student-generated agendas as a percentage target:** Not included separately because the actionable Guide behavior is the submission and ownership pipeline in BL-045.
- **Recurring student jobs as a separate pattern:** Folded into BL-047 because the important distinction is whether students own implementation, not simply whether jobs exist.
- **Student choice of meeting format or room setup:** Observed, but not supported strongly enough as a distinct ownership pattern without overlapping facilitation structures.

## Ambiguous

- In younger groups, Guides frequently supplied prompts while students appeared to lead.
- Some transcripts labeled all child speech as one combined student speaker, making individual ownership difficult to verify.
- Several recordings appeared to be duplicate or near-duplicate versions of the same meeting.
- A student title did not always indicate control over agenda order, speakers, discussion, or voting.

## Handling

- Counted leadership as genuine only when the student controlled a consequential process step.
- Treated brief prompts as scaffolding when the Guide returned the task to the student.
- Treated repeated adult direction or adult-authored outcomes as ceremonial leadership or takeover.
- Consolidated duplicate recordings so the same meeting did not count as multiple evidence examples.

# 6.2.2 Decision Making — Scope Log

## In

- **BL-048:** Turn discussion into one clear decision at a time, with explicit options, eligibility, and vote limits.
- **BL-049:** Require BothSides, feasibility, and standards impact before opening the vote.
- **BL-050:** Use a pre-agreed tie protocol that keeps authority with students.
- **BL-051:** Maintain a visible decision record and start each recurring item from the last adopted rule.

These were included because they cover four distinct parts of student decision-making: framing the vote, testing the proposal, resolving no-majority outcomes, and preserving continuity after the decision.

## Out

- **Use majority vote:** Not included as a separate pattern because it was already a common baseline expectation and did not by itself ensure decision quality.
- **Use anonymous voting:** Not included because anonymity was helpful in some contexts but unnecessary or counterproductive in others.
- **Always reach a decision:** Excluded because tabling a proposal or retaining the current rule can be the strongest student-owned outcome when no majority exists.
- **Revisit previous decisions:** Incorporated into BL-051 rather than treated separately.
- **Assign implementation owners after voting:** Mapped to BL-047 under Student Ownership to avoid duplicating the same operational behavior.
- **Celebrate ambitious decisions:** Observed inconsistently and not supported as a distinct pattern with sufficient contrasting evidence.

## Ambiguous

- A Guide sometimes supplied feasibility information students could not reasonably know.
- In other cases, the same type of intervention became a substantive argument for the Guide’s preferred result.
- Some votes contained several nested questions, making it unclear whether the meeting had one decision or multiple decisions.
- Tie procedures varied across campuses and sometimes included adult selection, random selection, re-voting, or retaining the current rule.
- Transcription errors occasionally made vote counts or option wording unclear.

## Handling

- Treated Guide-provided constraints as positive when they were factual, necessary, neutral, and followed by a return of authority to students.
- Treated adult advocacy or adult selection of the outcome as an anti-pattern.
- Separated nested decisions into principle, model, implementation, and review when evaluating clarity.
- Accepted multiple tie protocols only when they preserved student authority and were known before the tie.
- Did not rely on unclear numerical vote counts unless the decision process was independently understandable from the transcript.

# 6.2.3 Student Voice — Scope Log

## In

- **BL-052:** Require concerns, feedback, and nominations to include a concrete observation and its impact.
- **BL-053:** Use a transparent turn-distribution system and actively recover unheard voices.
- **BL-054:** Teach respectful dissent as a repeatable peer-to-peer move, then require students to retry unproductive phrasing.

These were included because they address three different conditions for meaningful student voice: evidence quality, access to the floor, and the ability to disagree productively.

## Out

- **More than two or three students should speak:** Not included as a pattern because it is an outcome measure, not a Guide behavior.
- **Students should speak more than Guides:** Useful as a quality indicator, but it overlaps Student Ownership and Guide Facilitation.
- **Students should articulate well-reasoned opinions:** Folded into BL-052 and BL-054 rather than treated as a separate pattern.
- **Students should move from personal preference to community impact:** Incorporated into BL-052 and BL-049.
- **Students should give direct feedback:** Included inside BL-052 and BL-054 to avoid creating an overlapping feedback pattern.
- **Every student must speak:** Excluded because equitable access does not require forced public participation.

## Ambiguous

- Speaker diarization often combined several students into one speaker label.
- In some excerpts, it was unclear whether respectful phrasing was a retry by the original student or a model supplied by another student.
- A Guide invitation to a quiet student could represent voice recovery or pressured participation.
- Repeated agreement statements sometimes reflected authentic consensus and sometimes reflected shallow participation.
- Some personal examples were relevant evidence, while others remained preference without community impact.

## Handling

- Used generic anonymized speaker labels when individual identity or role was uncertain.
- Claimed a successful retry only when the transcript clearly showed the same student restating the idea.
- Otherwise described the evidence as modeling or redirecting respectful language.
- Treated voice recovery as an invitation with permission to pass, not a requirement to manufacture an opinion.
- Counted student contributions as meaningful when they added evidence, a concern, a question, an alternative, or a standards connection.

# 6.2.4 Guide Facilitation — Scope Log

## In

- **BL-055:** Clarify and return the thinking to students instead of supplying the answer.
- **BL-056:** Intervene firmly on process—safety, respect, access, and clarity—then immediately hand leadership back.
- **BL-057:** Use an explicit scaffold-and-fade progression matched to student readiness.

These were included because they distinguish three separate Guide responsibilities: making student thinking understandable, protecting the meeting process, and developing students toward increasing independence.

## Out

- **Use a circle, timer, talking object, or gavel:** Not included as independent patterns because these are tools. Their value depends on how they are used.
- **Use neutral paraphrasing:** Incorporated into BL-055.
- **Protect psychological safety:** Incorporated into BL-056 through respect, access, and emotional-safety guardrails.
- **Keep Guide speaking below a fixed percentage:** Not included because speaking percentage is diagnostic and varies by developmental readiness.
- **Guides should remain silent:** Excluded because silence can allow unsafe, disrespectful, inaccessible, or unclear processes to continue.
- **Model expectations at the start:** Incorporated into BL-057 when modeling is part of a deliberate progression rather than a permanent adult role.

## Ambiguous

- Some Guide questions sounded open but introduced a preferred solution.
- Necessary safety or feasibility information sometimes resembled adult control.
- Younger students often required repeated procedural prompts.
- It was not always clear whether repeated prompting represented appropriate scaffolding or failure to fade.
- Speaker labels sometimes made it uncertain whether a statement came from a Guide, student facilitator, or another adult.

## Handling

- Classified a question as neutral only when students could reasonably answer it in multiple ways.
- Classified an intervention as process protection when it addressed safety, respect, access, or clarity and was followed by an explicit return of the floor.
- Classified it as takeover when the Guide supplied the solution, argued for the outcome, or retained control afterward.
- Evaluated scaffolding by whether support became lighter across the interaction or across later meetings.
- Used generic role labels when speaker identity was not supported clearly by the transcript.
