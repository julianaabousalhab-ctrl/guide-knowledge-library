# Give Student Leaders a Defined Area of Real Authority

## Pattern

Assign students clearly bounded leadership roles that include genuine authority over meaningful parts of the Limitless Launch.

A student leader should do more than stand at the front, hold a gavel, read a poster, or announce a result. The role should include specific responsibilities the student can exercise without waiting for continuous Guide approval, such as:

- opening or closing the meeting
- introducing agenda items
- selecting or calling on speakers
- enforcing agreed discussion norms
- sequencing discussion
- checking whether opposing views have been heard
- stating the decision question
- conducting and counting a vote
- announcing the outcome

The role is bounded because the student knows what they own and what remains a Guide responsibility. The Guide retains safety, legality, accessibility, factual constraints, and true non-negotiables. Inside the assigned boundary, the student has real authority.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide transferred the symbol of leadership and the student immediately exercised meeting authority.*

> [Guide]: “The PLayers' Parliament is now Now, in session. I place the gavel in your hand, sir.”

> [Student Leader]: “Alrighty, so uh... The first issue is joystick. If this was your issue, then come stand up, please.”

> [Student Leader]: “Okay, heads down, eyes closed.”

The student did not merely receive the gavel. The student introduced the issue, brought the topic owner forward, managed the discussion, and initiated the decision process.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1FMDzwUhn4duBjRTJmmR80hepYfzkFQumRrmbubBX7Jg/edit?usp=sharing)

*2- A younger student was given authority over a defined section of the meeting.*

> [Guide]: “[Student Leader], can you please run this portion of our meeting? Where would you like to start?”

> [Guide]: “Can you assign somebody?”

> [Student Leader]: “Does anybody want to read the poster?”

> [Student Leader]: “Do we need to discuss anything?”

> [Student Leader]: “Are you ready to go?”

The boundary was clear: the student owned one meeting section. Within that section, the student selected the sequence, delegated a task, checked for discussion, and moved the community toward a vote.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1tk1g9pfceAIAwJzM49EvLOAz3i3f_L3G5ULAke-UhrM/edit?usp=sharing)

*3- The student leader established norms, enforced them, selected agenda items, and ran the vote.*

> [Student Leader]: “if you have the... Squidward. Yeah, Fluffy Squidward. Then you can talk. But if you don't, then you can't talk.”

> [Student Leader]: “[Bangs gavel] Here ye! Here ye! Player's Parliament is now in session!”
>
> [Student Leader]: “Hey! You have to hold the Squidward and come up here to talk.”

> [Student Leader]: “Okay. So I guess we're just going to put it up to a vote. So heads down and eyes closed.”

The student had authority over the operating rules of the meeting, not just its appearance. The student corrected a norm violation and advanced the group into a vote without requiring the Guide to retake control. 

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1mUNsJl8ygzb4v_OsN8ItzfqBTufKODYbCDDHnjgIDeI/edit?usp=sharing)

*4- The Guide explicitly stepped away, and students managed discussion and decision-making.*

> [Guide]: “And I'm stepping away from the conversation. It's now student-led.”

> [Student Facilitator]: “Does anybody agree or disagree with the placement of Town Hall slips?”

> [Student Facilitator]: “Alright, so let's take a vote. If you think it should be in that room, raise your hand.”

> [Student Facilitator]: “It has to be a majority vote. One, two, three. Four, good, approved.”

The transfer of authority was observable: students invited disagreement, conducted the vote, counted it, and announced that the proposal had been approved.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/170-iRxxWHjVcSC_l2o9S0of9My2G-F438L6Ljk1oejc/edit?usp=drive_link)

## Anti-Pattern

A Guide gives a student a leadership title but retains the consequential parts of the role.

The student may be called “mayor,” hold the gavel, call on a few speakers, or announce the result, while the Guide still:

- chooses the sequence
- explains the topic
- proposes the options
- decides when discussion is complete
- frames the vote
- counts the vote
- interprets the result
- redirects every procedural move

This creates ceremonial leadership. The student appears to lead but remains dependent on the Guide for every meaningful decision.

*1- The mayor received a title, but the Guide continued to define the agenda and frame the solution space before handing the floor back.*

> [Guide]: “So we have been leading it a little bit, but we're trying to make it to where it's student-led, and [Guide] and I say as little as possible.”

> [Guide]: “As the mayor you can also lead the agenda. Okay? So I right here on the agenda. Can you start here?”

> [Guide]: “So we are going to pick a reward for when everybody in our grade van Closes their time ring.”

> [Guide]: “Mayor, you're taking over from here. Okay?”

The Guide named the goal of student leadership but still introduced the substance, explained the topic, and suggested the decision context before transferring only the remaining discussion to the mayor.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1JXtwykfAGYuEryoYNYGpOD4pWb7isjYIHJHCrJZbPMM/edit?usp=drive_link)

*2- The mayor was brought in only after the vote to announce the result and use the gavel.*

> [Facilitator]: “All right, Mayor, come on over.”

> [Facilitator]: “I'll let you announce the results.”

> [Facilitator]: “And then you may hit the gavel.”

> [Facilitator]: “What's the result, Mayor?”

> [Student Mayor]: “The job. We are going to do the job at the end of the day.”

The student received the visible final moment of leadership, but not the authority to frame or conduct the decision. Announcing an adult-managed result is ceremonial participation rather than genuine ownership.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1OjWYRT42_LWCTCa6cRc5UGTUlo4dl5SWSfapnzBzw88/edit?tab=t.0)

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Leadership Authority Card** before the Launch.

Give the student leader a short card with two sections:

### You Own

- opening and closing your assigned section
- introducing the topic
- calling on speakers
- enforcing the talking protocol
- asking for an opposing view
- deciding when the group is ready to vote
- stating the vote options
- counting or assigning someone to count
- announcing the outcome

### The Guide Owns

- physical and emotional safety
- legal or schoolwide non-negotiables
- accessibility support
- correcting material factual errors
- clarifying constraints students could not reasonably know
- stopping conduct that violates community standards

Use an explicit handoff:

> “For this section, you own the speakers, discussion flow, and vote. I will step in only for safety, accessibility, or a non-negotiable constraint.”

During the section, the Guide should redirect questions back to the student leader:

> “That is a process question for the student leader.”

> “Ask the mayor whether the group is ready to vote.”

> “The student leader owns the speaker order.”

After the meeting, debrief privately rather than coaching continuously in front of the group:

- What authority did you use well?
- Where did you hesitate?
- Did every student understand your directions?
- Did you hear an opposing view?
- What will you run independently next time?

A leadership role is genuine when the student makes consequential process decisions inside a known boundary and the Guide does not reclaim those decisions merely because the student leads differently from an adult.
