# Move Community Work From Adult Reminders to Student-Owned Roles

## Pattern

Transfer the implementation of Townhall decisions and recurring community operations to named student-owned roles.

Student ownership should continue after discussion and voting. When a decision creates ongoing work—such as running a raffle, preparing a newsletter, restocking a vending machine, welcoming families, presenting at Test to Pass, or completing community jobs—the Guide helps students define:

- who owns the operation
- what the role must accomplish
- when the responsibility occurs
- what materials or access the student needs
- who serves as backup
- how the community will know the task was completed

The Guide retains responsibility for safety, purchasing authority, transportation, legal requirements, and other adult-only constraints. The student owns the repeatable work that can authentically be transferred.

## Evidence

*1- Students maintained recurring jobs that supported the community rather than leaving the work to the Guide.*

> [Student Leader]: “Previously why we wanted to do jobs is because one person had a job and we didn't think that was fair. So this is why this is on our agenda forever.”

> [Guide]: “Forever but also because it's supposed to help right who does it help?”

> [Student Leader]: “It helps our community helps our community”

> [Guide]: “Okay, great. So what do we have for jobs right now?”

> [Student Leader]: “[Student A] is newsletter, [Student B] is inside cleanup and [Student C] is door shutter.”

The jobs were treated as standing community infrastructure rather than temporary participation. Students could name the current owners and understood that the roles existed to support the community.

> [Guide]: “But we have, instead of the adults taking it, this is your school, this is your life skills learning, not ours. We've done the facilitating, but we are not the deliverers of this information.”

> [Guide]: “So, we have people, we have two people, Oh, two people, one breeder. The Guide kept the adult role as facilitator while transferring welcoming, emceeing, and transitions to students. These were necessary operational responsibilities, not symbolic titles.

## Anti-Pattern

A Townhall produces a decision, event, or recurring expectation without assigning an owner, backup, trigger, or completion check.

In this anti-pattern, students may vote for something, but implementation remains dependent on the Guide remembering, organizing, and completing the work. When the adult is absent or does not prompt the next step, the decision stalls.

*1- Students voted for an event but stopped planning it because no implementation owner or next step had been established.*

> [Guide]: “Valentine's Day party, you added this, you voted for it, and then you stopped talking about it, this will be your gentle nudge as to is this happening tomorrow or is it not happening tomorrow, with a reminder that this was your vote, not a school-offered thing, so you will need to figure out what's going on with that.”

The vote created an intention but not an implementation plan. The Guide had to revive the decision immediately before the event because ownership had not been assigned.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1sqF9LHTJZJPqU7oxVgngjlbJFlPXNaiKkykHVOzpJy8/edit?usp=sharing)

*2- A recurring operation stopped when the adult who normally initiated it was absent.*

> [Guide]: “I was not here on Friday because I was sick and usually I am the one who starts the raffle.”

> [Student]: “Um, we have lots of unused tickets and people worked hard for those tickets.”

The raffle appeared to be a community system, but its execution depended on one adult. The absence exposed that students did not yet own the trigger or operating routine.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use an **Owner–Backup–Trigger–Proof** closeout before leaving any operational topic.

After the vote, ask the student facilitator to complete four statements:

1. **Owner:** “The student responsible for this is ___.”
2. **Backup:** “If that student is absent, ___ will do it.”
3. **Trigger:** “This happens every ___ / when ___ occurs.”
4. **Proof:** “We will know it happened because ___.”

Record the result in a visible operations board:

| Operation | Student Owner | Backup | Trigger | Completion Proof |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Weekly raffle | Student A | Student B | Friday before Closing | Winners recorded and tickets reset |
| Newsletter | Student C | Student D | Wednesday after Workshops | Draft submitted |
| Vending restock | Student E | Student F | Monday | Inventory updated |
| Test to Pass welcome | Student G | Student H | Families arrive | Welcome and transitions completed |

Before accepting the role, ask the student owner:

> “Can you explain what you are responsible for, when you will do it, and what support you need?”

During implementation, the Guide should avoid becoming the default reminder. Use the visible role system instead:

> “Who owns this operation?”

> “What does the role card say happens next?”

> “Is the owner here, or does the backup take over?”

At the next Townhall, review the operation briefly:

- Did it happen?
- Did the owner have enough authority and access?
- Did the backup system work?
- Should the role continue, rotate, or be redesigned?

A decision becomes student-owned only when students also own the repeatable work required to make it real.
