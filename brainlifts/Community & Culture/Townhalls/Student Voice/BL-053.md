# Use a Visible Speaker Queue and Recover Unheard Voices

## Pattern

Use a transparent turn-distribution system so students understand how to enter the discussion, how speakers are selected, and when their turn will come. Actively notice and recover voices that have not yet been heard.

The system may use raised hands, a talking object, a visible speaker list, name cards, or another predictable queue. Regardless of the format, it should:

- use one clear method for requesting a turn
- give priority to students who have waited longest
- prevent frequent speakers from repeatedly moving ahead of others
- make the student facilitator responsible for speaker order
- allow a student to pass without losing future access
- include a check for students who have not yet spoken
- protect one speaker at a time

The Guide monitors access rather than controlling the discussion. When the student facilitator misses someone, the Guide briefly identifies the process issue and returns authority to the student leader.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide established one visible method for entering the discussion and connected it to equal opportunity.* 

> Guide: “Every person speaks at a time. If you have something to say, these things that are attached to your torso, these appendages called your arms, you put that in the air and that's how we know you have something to say and we will call on you so that everybody has an opportunity to speak.”

> Guide: “If you sit there and you're like, I won't call on you any faster. Just putting it in the air nicely gets you called out.”

Students were told exactly how to request a turn, and louder or more insistent behavior did not move someone ahead in the queue.

*2- The student leader received explicit authority over speaker order and the talking object.*

> Guide: “And your job, my friend, is to help us with who speaks in what order, and you'll be passing out Pete the Cat, okay, to help us keep track.”

> Guide: “Hold on, because I'm no longer in charge of this. It's now [Student Leader]. Raise your hand.”

> Guide: “And [Student Leader] as the town hall mayor will be deciding who speaks in what order by passing out Pete the Cat.”

Speaker order was a defined student responsibility rather than an informal privilege. The talking object made the current speaker and the student facilitator’s authority visible to the community.

*3- The student facilitator used a wait-time rule to prevent newer hands from overtaking students who had already been waiting.*

> Student Facilitator: “So with the, um, Squidward, if you want to talk you're going to come up here. And, um, also if you want the Squidward then you're going to raise the hand... raise your hand. Um, wait until someone picks on you.”

> Student Facilitator: “Also if someone's been raising their hand for, like, over two turns, I guess, then just give it to them because they've been waiting the longest.”

The process included both a clear entry signal and a fairness rule: students waiting longest should receive priority instead of being repeatedly skipped.

*4- The Guide noticed that one student had not participated and deliberately recovered that voice.*

> Guide: “[Student], we haven't heard your voice.”

The student then entered the discussion:

> Student: “I felt a lot about the game kit. So you'd just be playing by yourself, sad, by yourself, cornered.”

The Guide did not supply the student’s position. The Guide simply noticed the participation gap and created access for the student’s own perspective.

## Anti-Pattern

Speaker selection is informal, personality-driven, or based on who is most visible to the facilitator.

In this anti-pattern, students cannot tell when they will be called, students on one side of the room receive preference, recent hands move ahead of students who have waited, and frequent speakers dominate. A student leader may technically control the talking object while lacking a fair system for distributing it.

*Students reported side-based favoritism, newer hands being selected first, and one student waiting approximately 30 minutes.*

> Student A: “if somebody's hand went up on this side, instantly. And, like, even if, like, if somebody was talking over here, and then somebody's hand pushed up over here, they, this side, they would make them stop talking and let the other side go.”

> Student A: “I saw some kids who had their hands up for a while, and then a kid who just put their hand up got called over here.”

> Student B: “The town hall mayor was favoriting people. So I saw someone right next to her have their hand up for maybe 30 minutes without being called.”

The student mayor had a title and controlled turns, but the absence of a transparent queue made access appear arbitrary and unfair.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Queue–Track–Recover** routine.

### 1. Queue

Before discussion begins, the student facilitator states:

> “Raise one hand to enter the speaker queue. I will call on people in the order I see them. Keep your hand raised until I acknowledge you.”

The facilitator acknowledges each student:

> “[Student A], I see you. You are after [Student B].”

A talking object may be used, but the object should represent whose turn it is—not determine the order by being passed randomly.

### 2. Track

Use a visible queue appropriate to the age group:

- write names in order on a small board
- place student name cards in a line
- use numbered tokens
- assign a student queue keeper
- point around the circle in a consistent direction for an initial round

Apply two fairness rules:

1. A student who has waited longest goes next.
2. Before a repeat speaker receives a third turn, invite students who have not spoken.

The student facilitator can say:

> “I have [Student A], then [Student B], then [Student C].”

> “[Student D] has been waiting longer, so they are next.”

### 3. Recover

Before closing discussion or opening a vote, ask:

> “Whose voice have we not heard?”

> “Is there anyone who has not spoken and wants to add a different point?”

> “We have heard several people from this side. Is anyone on the other side of the circle waiting?”

> “You may speak or pass.”

Do not require every student to manufacture an opinion. Recovery means creating a genuine opening, not forcing public participation.

### Guide intervention

When the student facilitator misses someone, the Guide uses the smallest possible process correction:

> “Pause. [Student A] has been waiting longer. Who is next in your queue?”

> “Before another repeat speaker, check for an unheard voice.”

> “I noticed we have heard from one side of the circle more than the other. Please check your queue.”

The Guide should then return the floor immediately.

### Final participation check

Before voting, the student facilitator states:

> “We heard from __ students. We heard support, disagreement, and questions. Is there an unheard perspective that could change how we understand the decision?”

A transparent turn system does not guarantee that every student will speak equally. It guarantees that access is understandable, waiting is respected, dominance is limited, and quiet students receive a meaningful opportunity to enter the discussion.
