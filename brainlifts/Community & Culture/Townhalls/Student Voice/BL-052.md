# Require an Observation and Its Impact

## Pattern

Require concerns, feedback, and nominations to include a concrete observation and an explanation of its impact.

Students should not make judgments about a peer’s personality, intentions, or general character. They should identify something they directly observed, explain when or where it happened, and connect it to a community standard or effect.

For concerns and critical feedback, students answer:

- What did you see or hear?
- When or where did it happen?
- What effect did it have on you, the learning environment, or the community?
- Which community standard was affected?
- What change or support are you requesting?

For nominations and recognition, students answer:

- What did the student do?
- When did it happen?
- Which standard did the action demonstrate?
- How did the action help the student or community?

The Guide protects this evidence threshold without supplying the accusation or nomination on the student’s behalf.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide established specificity and direct address before students delivered peer feedback.* 

> Guide: “specific feedback as to why you believe that this is behavior that is not acceptable in our community. And you talk directly to the person. You're not speaking to me or your peers. You're talking directly to the person.”

Students then named concrete moments:

> Student A: “Like when we're doing core skills or when we're doing work, like you start laughing, you start talking, and sometimes when there's a little, you start laughing when they're doing stuff.”

> Student B: “there's one time when you laughed, and I don't think I think that's very appropriate.”

> Student C: “Like today, when we were doing shoes and juggling, [Guide] kept asking you to juggle. From it at so high that you hit these things above us. And you just kept doing that and not listening to any feedback that anybody gave you.”

> Student D: “Like yesterday, when we were in the room and [Visitor] was telling us about the escape room, you didn't take her seriously and you were laughing.”

The feedback moved from a general concern to identifiable events during Core Skills, juggling, and a presentation. Students described both the behavior and how it affected the seriousness, focus, and feedback culture of the community.

*2- The Guide required the group to identify the exact behavior and its negative community impact before considering a consequence.*

> Student A: “I remember last session when we were reading our stories and giving feedback. When people gave you feedback and you blamed it on other things.”

> Guide: “So [Student B], to clarify, what is the the exact reason the yellow card is being proposed?”

> Student B: “For not receiving the feedback.”

> Guide: “And so the overall question to the group then is, do you feel that [Student C]'s non-receiving of feedback is impacting our community in a negative way?”

The Guide did not allow a consequence to rest on a vague impression. The group identified a previous story-feedback session, named the behavior as not receiving feedback, and examined whether it negatively affected the community.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://drive.google.com/file/d/11FZJXEmawIOOaY7uc_mR9ZcsMXV8nRQv/view?usp=sharing)

*3- The Guide helped a student distinguish the observable behaviors from the broader impact.*

> Student A: “Whenever someone's telling you something, you're not listening. You're not even listening to them. You're like tuning them out when they're trying to give you help.”

> Guide: “What is the specific behaviors in which this was proposed for?”

> Student B: “Because I was being distracted.”

> Guide: “Okay, being distracting, and what else?”

> Student B: “And I've been making a lot of noise.”

> Guide: “You've been making a lot of noise, but what is the other thing that you're not doing right now?”

> Student B: “I'm not paying attention to other people.”

> Guide: “your peers are noting that you've been very disruptive, which is distracting the whole community from being able to focus or do the work in which they've been asked to do, and that you're not receiving feedback.”

The exchange separated the observation—making noise and not attending to feedback—from the impact—preventing the community from focusing and completing its work.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1-2bqOLXwB2TMPA13zVgvgYjcPcz27g8Z/view?usp=sharing)

*4- A nomination was tied to a specific act and then connected explicitly to community impact.*

> Student A: “I would like to nominate [Student B].”

> Speaker: “For what?”

> Student A: “Even though she wasn't wasn't playing with the ball, she picked it up.”

Later, the facilitator restated the nomination:

> Guide: “I think [Student B] should get Bob because she picked up at lunch and took care of our community and upholding community standards.”

The nomination identified a concrete action—picking up a ball the student had not been using—and connected it to caring for the community and upholding its standards.

## Anti-Pattern

Students offer concerns or nominations based on preference, convenience, promises, or unsupported labels rather than observable evidence and impact.

A vague statement such as “they deserve it,” “they are annoying,” “they are nice,” or “I just want them to win” does not provide enough information for meaningful feedback or fair recognition.

*Students initially nominated a peer to finish the meeting quickly and because of a personal promise.*

> Student A: “I'll just nominate [Student B] so we can get to the Christmas party. Yeah, nominate [Student B]. [Student B] gets Yet Yeti, whatever.”

The Guide challenged the basis of the nomination:

> Guide: “Do we vote on somebody just because we want to get to their Christmas party? Or do we actually believe that she has said, I can't do this yet. I am adaptable. I have a positive attitude.”

During the next nomination:

> Student A: “[Student B] because... She really wants it and because of my promise.”

> Guide: “Is that a reason that matches her passage?”

> Student A: “No.”

The nominations were based on reaching the party faster, the nominee’s desire, and a personal promise. None of those reasons demonstrated the award standard or described a qualifying action.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use an **Observation–Context–Impact–Standard–Request** sequence.

### For concerns or corrective feedback

Ask the student to complete:

> “When we were ___, I saw or heard you ___.”

> “The impact was ___.”

> “This affected our standard of ___.”

> “What I am asking you to do next time is ___.”

Example structure:

> “During Core Skills, I heard you continue talking after two peers asked for quiet. It made it harder for people near you to focus. That affected our standard of respecting other people’s learning. Next time, I am asking you to move the conversation to break or another space.”

### For nominations or recognition

Ask the student to complete:

> “I nominate [Student] because during ___, I saw them ___.”

> “That demonstrated ___.”

> “It helped ___ by ___.”

Example structure:

> “I nominate [Student] because during cleanup, they picked up materials they had not used. That demonstrated upholding community standards. It helped the group finish cleanup without waiting for the Guide.”

### Guide prompts for vague statements

When a student says:

> “They were distracting.”

Ask:

> “What did you see or hear them do?”

When a student says:

> “They never listen.”

Ask:

> “What is one specific time when feedback was given and what happened next?”

When a student says:

> “They deserve the award.”

Ask:

> “What action did you observe that matches the award language?”

When a student says:

> “They are nice.”

Ask:

> “What did they do, and how did it help someone?”

When a student proposes a consequence, ask:

> “What evidence are we using?”

> “What was the impact on the community?”

> “Have we described the behavior without judging the person?”

Do not convert the student’s vague claim into a fully formed accusation yourself. Return the thinking:

> “I need the observation and the impact from someone who witnessed it.”

If students cannot provide both, the concern or nomination is not ready for a community decision.

The goal is not to make feedback longer. The goal is to make it fair, verifiable, actionable, and connected to the standards students are responsible for maintaining.
