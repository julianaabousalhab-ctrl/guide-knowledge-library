# Make Students Retry the Message Without Abandoning the Disagreement

## Pattern

Teach respectful disagreement as a repeatable peer-to-peer communication move, then require students to retry statements that dismiss, ridicule, blame, or attack another person or idea.

Respectful dissent does not mean avoiding disagreement or weakening a student’s position. Students should be able to challenge a proposal directly while:

- addressing the peer rather than the Guide
- identifying the idea they disagree with
- giving a reason
- avoiding judgments about the person
- acknowledging any part of the idea they understand
- offering a concern, alternative, or question

The Guide should explicitly teach a small set of reusable sentence frames. When a student uses an unproductive phrase, the Guide pauses the discussion, names the problem with the phrasing, and asks the student to express the same underlying concern again.

The student—not the Guide—should produce the revised statement.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide interrupted a put-down, connected the correction to the community standard, and required the idea to be expressed differently.* 

> Student A: “So you'd just be playing by yourself, sad, by yourself, cornered.”

> Guide: “Is that a kind way to phrase that?”

> Guide: “I understand where you're going, but how could you say that in a way that doesn't feel like you're putting down his idea?”

> Guide: “Because remember, expectation number three is if you disagree with somebody, that's fine, but don't tell somebody they're wrong because they disagree with your opinion. How could you express that in a way that is more aligned with our cultural standards?”

The discussion then returned to the concern in more productive language:

> Student B: “[Student A], I agree with your opinion, but if you're doing game kit, you might accidentally disrupt someone. But I know it's a fun game”

The Guide did not reject the concern about GimKit. The Guide rejected the put-down and required the group to preserve the concern while changing how it was communicated.

*2- The Guide taught reusable disagreement stems before students needed them.*

> Guide: “So remember that if we disagree, we disagree respectfully.”

> Guide: “For example, if someone says something you disagree with, and then you have Pete the Cat, you can say, I respectfully disagree, or I hear you, but this is what I think, right?”

> Guide: “Something like that.”

The Guide provided students with language they could reuse independently during peer discussion instead of relying only on a broad instruction to “be respectful.”

*3- A brief facilitator prompt led the student to complete the disagreement using the expected peer-to-peer frame.*

> Facilitator: “Finish with something.”

> Student: “I respectfully disagree with [Student] because um at lunch we do a lot of music for 20 minutes.”

The facilitator did not construct the student’s reasoning. A minimal prompt activated the familiar disagreement structure, and the student supplied the peer, position, and reason.

*4- Younger students were explicitly shown that disagreement could remain kind.*

> Guide: “Or you could say, I respectfully disagree because...”

> Guide: “Okay, so those are kind ways that we can disagree with someone.”

The Guide framed respectful dissent as a normal and acceptable part of Townhall rather than treating disagreement itself as misbehavior.

## Anti-Pattern

The facilitator corrects only turn-taking or volume while allowing insults, commands, or personal remarks to remain unrepaired.

In this anti-pattern, students may be reminded to raise their hands or wait their turn, but they are not required to restate language that attacks or humiliates a peer. The meeting then moves forward without restoring respectful peer-to-peer communication.

*Students used personal insults and aggressive commands, while the intervention focused on speaking procedure rather than requiring a retry.*

> Student A: “Poopy pants.”

> Student B: “And get out of my way!”

> Student C: “My name is not poopy pants.”

> Facilitator: “If you want to speak, you gotta raise your hand.”

The discussion soon moved into the vote:

> Facilitator: “Time to get your vote.”

The facilitator addressed how students entered the conversation but did not require the insulting or aggressive statements to be repaired. The students therefore received no practice converting the conflict into respectful dissent.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Pause → Name → Frame → Retry → Return** routine.

### 1. Pause

Interrupt the phrasing without ending the student’s contribution:

> “Pause. Keep your concern, but change how you are saying it.”

This communicates that disagreement is welcome even though the current language is not.

### 2. Name

Identify the specific communication problem:

> “That described the person instead of the idea.”

> “That sounded like a put-down.”

> “You told your peer they were wrong, but you have not explained your concern.”

> “That was a command. Turn it into feedback or a request.”

Avoid vague corrections such as “Be nice.” Students need to understand what made the statement unproductive.

### 3. Frame

Offer one short stem without supplying the student’s argument:

> “I respectfully disagree with ___ because ___.”

> “I hear your idea, but my concern is ___.”

> “I agree with ___, and I would change ___ because ___.”

> “When you said ___, I understood it as ___. Is that what you meant?”

> “My alternative is ___ because ___.”

For younger students, keep two stems visible throughout Townhall. Older students can choose from a larger set or speak without a stem once they consistently meet the standard.

### 4. Retry

Require the student to say the complete thought again:

> “Try the same point again using the stem.”

Do not paraphrase it for the student. Do not let “Never mind” become the automatic escape from productive discomfort.

A successful retry should include:

- the peer or proposal being addressed
- the student’s position
- at least one reason, concern, or alternative

### 5. Return

Return immediately to the student-led discussion:

> “Thank you. [Student addressed], would you like to respond?”

> “Who wants to build on or challenge that reasoning?”

This prevents the Guide’s correction from becoming a lecture or shifting the discussion toward the adult.

### Suggested visible protocol

Post this beside the Townhall expectations:

**Disagreement is welcome. Put-downs are retried.**

1. Address the peer.
2. Name the idea.
3. Give a reason.
4. Offer a concern, question, or alternative.
5. Retry if the phrasing attacks the person.

The goal is not polite agreement. The goal is strong disagreement that students can hear, evaluate, and respond to without losing respect for one another.
