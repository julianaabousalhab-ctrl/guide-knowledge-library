# Match the Support to Readiness—and Remove It on Purpose

## Pattern

Use an explicit scaffold-and-fade progression that gives students only the support they currently need and deliberately removes that support as they demonstrate readiness.

The Guide may initially model or prompt parts of the Limitless Launch, including:

- opening the meeting
- reading the agenda
- assigning a reader
- calling on speakers
- asking for disagreement
- restating the vote question
- counting votes
- announcing the result

The support should not remain fixed. Each successful attempt should result in the student owning more of the next meeting.

A strong progression moves through four stages:

1. **Model:** The Guide demonstrates the routine while naming the steps.
2. **Prompt:** The student leads while the Guide provides brief cues.
3. **Observe:** The student leads independently while the Guide watches.
4. **Guardrail only:** The Guide intervenes only for safety, respect, access, or decision clarity.

Readiness should be judged by what the student can reliably do, not only by age or grade. A younger student may independently own one bounded section, while a more experienced student may own the entire agenda and vote process.

The Guide should tell students which stage they are in and what responsibility will be transferred next.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide framed the task, identified the decisions students needed to make, and then explicitly stepped away.* 

> Guide: “Okay, so first topic I will start, and then it's going to be a student-led conversation.”

> Guide: “Okay, so what you all as a group of students need to decide is, one, where should the Town Hall slips go? And two, when should we be allowed to write down ideas for Town Hall?”

> Guide: “And I'm stepping away from the conversation. It's now student-led.”

Students then took over the discussion and vote:

> Student Facilitator: “Does anybody agree or disagree with the placement of Town Hall slips?”

> Student Facilitator: “Alright, so let's take a vote.”

The Guide completed only the setup students still needed: defining the topic and the two decisions. The Guide then made the transfer explicit and allowed students to manage participation and voting.

## Anti-Pattern

The Guide announces an intention to make the meeting student-led but continues supplying the agenda sequence, content, examples, and next moves before allowing the student leader to act.

This creates a nominal handoff rather than a real progression. Students receive a title or a final speaking turn, but the Guide continues performing the cognitive and procedural work they were supposed to learn.

* The Guide stated that adults would speak less, appointed student roles, and then continued to explain the topic and generate possible answers before handing over the floor.*

> Guide: “So we have been leading it a little bit, but we're trying to make it to where it's student-led, and [Guide] and I say as little as possible.”

> Guide: “As the mayor you can also lead the agenda. Okay? So I right here on the agenda. Can you start here?”

The Guide then resumed control of the topic:

> Guide: “So we are going to pick a reward for when everybody in our grade van Closes their time ring.”

> Guide: “This might be a pajama day. This might Be crazy sock day. This might be I bring a stuffy and chill in the common space for core skills one day.”

Only after explaining the topic and supplying examples did the Guide say:

> Guide: “Mayor, you're taking over from here.”

The meeting expressed the goal of fading, but the Guide continued doing work the student mayor could have attempted: reading the agenda, explaining the decision, and eliciting initial proposals. The handoff therefore happened after much of the leadership work was already complete.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Model → Prompt → Watch → Debrief** plan for each student leadership skill.

### 1. Name the skill being transferred

Choose one or two skills rather than transferring the entire meeting at once.

Examples:

- opening the meeting
- introducing a topic
- maintaining the speaker queue
- asking for an opposing view
- framing the vote
- counting and announcing the result

Tell the student:

> “Today you will independently introduce each topic and call on speakers. I will help only if the vote question becomes unclear.”

### 2. Select the current support stage

| Stage | Guide behavior | Student behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Model | Demonstrates and narrates the step | Watches, identifies the sequence, and practices one part |
| Prompt | Gives a brief cue only when needed | Performs the step |
| Watch | Remains silent and records observations | Runs the step independently |
| Guardrail | Intervenes only for safety, respect, access, or clarity | Owns the full process |

Use the least intrusive support that allows the student to succeed.

### 3. Use progressively lighter prompts

Move from explicit to minimal support:

**Full model**

> “First I name the topic. Then I ask the student owner to explain it. Then I create the speaker queue.”

**Direct prompt**

> “What comes after the topic owner explains the proposal?”

**Visual prompt**

Point to a role card that says:

`Topic → Owner → Speakers → BothSides → Vote`

**Nonverbal prompt**

Pause, make eye contact, or indicate the agenda.

**No prompt**

Allow the student to notice and repair the missed step.

Do not repeat the full verbal prompt once a student has already demonstrated the routine.

### 4. Establish a fade target before the meeting

Record one measurable transfer goal:

> “Today the Guide may prompt the student leader up to three times.”

> “Next week the student leader will introduce every topic without a verbal prompt.”

> “After two successful meetings, the Guide will move from prompting to silent observation.”

Without a fade target, temporary support easily becomes permanent adult control.

### 5. Debrief after—not throughout—the meeting

Unless immediate intervention is required, save coaching for the end.

Ask:

- Which parts did you lead without help?
- Where did you need a prompt?
- What did you notice when the discussion became quiet or confusing?
- Which responsibility are you ready to own next time?
- What should the Guide stop doing in the next meeting?

End with one specific transfer:

> “Next time, you will also frame and run the vote. I will only step in if eligibility or vote limits are unclear.”

### Readiness indicators

Fade support when the student can consistently:

- identify the next agenda step
- give directions peers understand
- call on students fairly
- recover an unheard voice
- ask for disagreement or another option
- restate the decision question
- apply the agreed voting protocol
- recover from a minor mistake without the Guide taking over

Increase support temporarily when a student cannot yet access the role because of unfamiliarity, language, reading demands, anxiety, or an inaccessible meeting structure.

The goal is not immediate Guide silence. The goal is support that makes itself unnecessary.
