# Protect the Process Without Taking Back the Meeting

## Pattern

Intervene firmly when safety, respect, participation access, or decision clarity is at risk, then immediately return leadership to the student facilitator.

A student-led Limitless Launch does not require the Guide to remain silent while the process breaks down. The Guide should step in when:

- a physical or emotional safety boundary is crossed
- a student is insulted, dismissed, or personally attacked
- students are speaking over one another or being denied access
- the speaker queue is not being applied fairly
- the student leader’s authority is being bypassed
- the vote question, eligibility, or vote limit is unclear
- students lack a factual or non-negotiable constraint they could not reasonably know

The intervention should address the process rather than decide the substance. It should be brief, specific, and followed by an explicit handback to the student leader.

The Guide protects **how** the community discusses and decides. Students continue to own **what** they think and decide.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide protected the student leader’s authority and returned the question to the student-led discussion.* 

> Guide: “Is there a different solution? I cannot call on you. Only [Student Leader] can call on you.”

The student leader then continued calling on peers, and another student proposed:

> Student: “The people who want to sing sing and the people who don't, don't sing.”

The Guide contributed a clarifying question but refused to replace the student leader as the person controlling the floor. The intervention reinforced the leadership boundary and immediately returned participation authority to the student.

*2- The Guide repaired a confusing tie process, then handed the runoff back to the student leader.*

> Guide: “I know we usually break a tiebreaker with rock, paper, scissors, Before we go to that, do you guys want to try a re-vote with only [Student A] and [Student B]?”

> Students: “Yeah.”

> Guide: “That way we get kind of an even vote?”

> Students: “Yeah.”

> Guide: “Okay [Student Leader], can you run this vote?”

> Student Leader: “Yep.”

> Student Leader: “Who votes for [Student A]?”

The Guide clarified how the tied vote could be narrowed without deciding the winner. Once the group agreed to the runoff, the student leader resumed control of the voting process.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/12gNP8T65CwefQ5TtGFlsIfDMU-n7YKEu51fhLb6a-80/edit?usp=sharing)

*3- The Guide set a firm respect boundary and immediately handed direct peer feedback back to students.*

> Guide: “And you talk directly to the person. You're not speaking to me or your peers. You're talking directly to the person. So, [Student A], since you've had it, go ahead and start us off.”

The student then gave the feedback directly:

> Student A: “Okay, so he has changed it maybe a little bit since I have added this, but it's still a problem. Like when we're doing core skills or when we're doing work, like you start laughing, you start talking”

The Guide defined the communication requirement but did not deliver the feedback for students. The substantive concern remained peer-to-peer.

*4- The Guide corrected the physical meeting structure, defined the task, and visibly stepped out.*

> Guide: “Can you move this way so that we're all sitting in a circle?”

> Guide: “Let's sit in a circle.”

After framing the two decisions students needed to make:

> Guide: “And I'm stepping away from the conversation. It's now student-led.”

Students then managed the discussion and vote:

> Student Facilitator: “Does anybody agree or disagree with the placement of Town Hall slips?”

> Student Facilitator: “Alright, so let's take a vote.”

The Guide intervened to create an accessible circle and clarify the scope of the discussion, then explicitly transferred the floor instead of remaining the central facilitator.

## Anti-Pattern

The Guide notices a legitimate process or standards concern but responds by taking over the substance of the discussion, delivering an extended explanation, or imposing the answer.

In this anti-pattern, the Guide moves beyond protecting safety, respect, access, or clarity. The adult decides what should happen and students are left to agree, comply, or vote on an adult-authored conclusion.

*1- The Guide closed a student proposal through repeated adult explanation rather than clarifying the constraint and returning the floor.*

> Guide: “Yeah, but that's kind of like recess. It's like a little bit like an outside activity. It's very high energy moving around a little bit distracting. So that's not something that we want to do at the same time as lunch. Okay. So that's why lunch and recess are separate.”

Later:

> Guide: “I'm going to reiterate one more time. The reason why we can't follow [Student]'s idea, though it is a great one, and it was his first house. So that's very brave. We want to make sure that we are focusing on refueling and being social and a responsible way during lunchtime and that recess and recess activities happen after.”

The Guide had a legitimate responsibility to communicate a lunchtime constraint. However, the intervention became a repeated substantive argument for rejecting the proposal rather than a concise boundary followed by student exploration of allowable alternatives.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1oShhxGZ0sYgZwBEKlwKPmmtz-aihGAGZ/view?usp=sharing)

*2- The Guide proposed a new rule, received a student objection, and continued advocating for the adult-generated answer.*

> Guide: “Okay, so can we say that, like, if you don't have an opinion to sharing town hall, then you have to put away your things.”

> Student: “No.”

> Guide: “Yeah, because, like, if you're not participating, then you need to put away your stuff.”

The Guide moved from protecting participation to supplying the rule. When a student disagreed, the Guide defended the proposed answer rather than returning the policy question to students.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/191STq4RF5zbHldoZQpffamnxaB-biX4Q4e56ja3zGJw/edit?usp=sharing)

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Stop → State → Hand Back** intervention.

### 1. Stop

Interrupt immediately when the process is unsafe, disrespectful, inaccessible, or unclear.

> “Pause.”

> “Stop for one process correction.”

Do not wait for the issue to escalate merely to preserve the appearance of student leadership.

### 2. State

Name the exact process boundary in one or two sentences.

#### Safety

> “That action is not physically safe. It stops now.”

> “The safety boundary is ___. You may continue discussing options inside that boundary.”

#### Respect

> “That statement addressed the person instead of the idea. Retry it as a concern, question, or alternative.”

#### Access

> “[Student A] has been waiting longer.”

> “One speaker at a time.”

> “Only the student facilitator calls on speakers.”

#### Clarity

> “The vote has stopped. We do not yet have one clear question.”

> “Clarify how many votes each person receives, then restart from zero.”

Avoid explaining the entire issue, arguing one side, or proposing the preferred solution.

### 3. Hand Back

Name the student who regains control and specify the next action:

> “[Student Leader], continue with your speaker queue.”

> “[Student Leader], restate the vote question.”

> “[Student Proposer], offer an option that stays inside the safety boundary.”

> “[Student A], retry your statement respectfully. Then [Student B] may respond.”

> “The process is clear. The decision is yours.”

### The five-second test

After intervening, the Guide should ask internally:

> “Can I return the floor within five seconds?”

Some safety or accessibility situations will require more explanation. Most respect, turn-taking, and vote-clarity corrections should not.

### Process intervention versus content takeover

| Process intervention | Content takeover |
|---|---|
| “One person speaks at a time. Student leader, continue.” | “Here is what I think the group should decide.” |
| “That option is not available because of a safety rule. What alternatives remain?” | “That will not work, so we are doing this instead.” |
| “The vote limit is unclear. Restate it and restart.” | “I will run the vote because this is taking too long.” |
| “Retry that without describing the person.” | “What you should say is…” |
| “Only the student leader calls on speakers.” | The Guide resumes calling on everyone. |

Firm facilitation protects student leadership. It does not compete with it.
