# Clarify the Question, Then Return It to Students

## Pattern

Clarify what students mean, check that the group understands the issue, and then return the thinking to students instead of supplying the answer.

The Guide may help students:

- define an unclear proposal
- distinguish the problem from the proposed solution
- identify the decision the group needs to make
- surface a missing constraint
- restate competing positions accurately
- connect the discussion to community standards

After clarifying, the Guide gives the intellectual work back to the students. Students should generate the reasons, alternatives, trade-offs, and final decision.

Effective clarification sounds like:

- “What do you mean by ___?”
- “Did I understand you correctly?”
- “Is this required or optional?”
- “What decision do you want the group to make?”
- “Who can respond to that concern?”
- “What options do you see?”

It does not sound like a question that quietly replaces the student’s proposal with the Guide’s preferred answer.

## Evidence

*1- When students looked to the Guide for approval, the Guide explicitly returned ownership of the discussion.* 

> Guide: “Don't look at me, it's your culture. This is you, this is up for you to discuss, not me.”

Students then continued examining the proposal:

> Student A: “I think, I think because he said like something about the Grow Towers and it takes us 15 minutes to get the stuff out most of the time.”

The Guide did not signal whether the proposal was acceptable. The Guide reminded students that the culture and the decision belonged to them, and students resumed the reasoning.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1wra5yZVhfVCWVu14EtjIXVfPzUXMPTZgJa0UKphILE8/edit?usp=sharing)

*2- The Guide clarified participation requirements, then asked the student proposer to answer a peer’s standards-based concern.*

> Guide: “Would this be required for everybody to participate? Or would this be a choice actually?”

> Students: “A choice.”

Later:

> Guide: “OK, so [Student A], can you help [Student B] better understand in what way Do you believe that having an origami club would elevate our culture?”

> Student A: “It can help people learn how to do hard things. Because whenever me and [Guide] were doing Origami Bird, we were seeing a hard thing and we still tried to go past it. And it could teach other people the person who's doing origami can teach other people that want to learn origami. They can show them. And if you want to learn origami, you can.”

The Guide clarified whether participation was mandatory, but did not defend the proposal. The student proposer remained responsible for explaining how the club would elevate the community.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1sqF9LHTJZJPqU7oxVgngjlbJFlPXNaiKkykHVOzpJy8/edit?usp=sharing)

*3- The Guide clarified the exact concern, then returned the consequence decision to the community

> Guide: “So [Student A], to clarify, what is the the exact reason the yellow card is being proposed?”

> Student A: “For not receiving the feedback.”

> Guide: “For not receiving the feedback, okay. And so the overall question to the group then is, do you feel that [Student B]'s non-receiving of feedback is impacting our community in a negative way? If the answer is yes in your head, how do you want to proceed?”

The Guide narrowed a potentially vague behavioral concern to the exact issue being considered. The Guide then returned both the impact judgment and the response decision to students.

## Anti-Pattern

The Guide uses clarification as a vehicle for introducing or steering students toward the Guide’s preferred solution.

A leading question may appear student-centered because the student supplies the final words, but the Guide has already narrowed the available thinking. The student’s original proposal is replaced before peers have examined it on its own merits.

*1- The Guide redirected a student’s proposed community job into a fine system.*

> Student: “So I think there should be a new job. A guy back there.”

> Guide: “[Student], instead of having a person monitor that, could you think of maybe something that might happen if you get out of your spot in line?”

> Student: “Like a 10 alpha bill?”

> Guide: “Maybe it's an alpha bill. So [Student], I just want to make sure I have clarity. You're proposing that there's some kind of alpha bill for moving out of your place in line”

The student proposed a new operational role. Rather than clarifying how the role would work or inviting peers to evaluate it, the Guide introduced a different category of solution. The discussion then proceeded as though the student had proposed a fine.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1UKLRsYTcsfYgVqTQAPaiLsQNsBCvn0gGdoVJDo8Ys_s/edit?usp=drive_link)

*2- The Guide authored a rule and continued advocating for it after a student initially rejected it.*

> Guide: “Okay, so can we say that, like, if you don't have an opinion to sharing town hall, then you have to put away your things.”

> Student: “No.”

> Guide: “Yeah, because, like, if you're not participating, then you need to put away your stuff.”

The Guide moved from asking students what distraction looked like to supplying the rule that should govern it. When a student said no, the Guide immediately explained why the proposed rule should be accepted rather than returning the disagreement to the group.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/191STq4RF5zbHldoZQpffamnxaB-biX4Q4e56ja3zGJw/edit?usp=sharing)

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Reflect → Check → Return → Wait** routine.

### 1. Reflect

Restate only what the student has already expressed:

> “What I heard is that the current system causes ___. Your proposed change is ___. Did I get that right?”

Do not add a consequence, solution, or rationale the student did not provide.

### 2. Check

Ask one neutral clarifying question:

> “Who would this apply to?”

> “Would this be required or optional?”

> “What problem would this solve?”

> “What decision do you want the community to make?”

> “What would this look like in practice?”

A neutral question can be answered in several reasonable ways. A leading question points students toward one adult-generated answer.

### 3. Return

Direct the unresolved thinking back to a student:

> “[Student proposer], how would you answer that concern?”

> “Who has another possible solution?”

> “What trade-offs do students see?”

> “Student leader, what question should the group decide?”

> “That is for the community to determine.”

When students look toward the Guide for approval:

> “Don't look at me, it's your culture.”

### 4. Wait

Allow silence after returning the question. Do not rescue the group immediately.

Count silently, invite students to discuss with a peer, or ask the student facilitator to call on someone who has not spoken.

### Test for a leading question

Before speaking, the Guide asks internally:

> “Could students answer this question in more than one valid way?”

If the answer is no, the Guide is probably supplying or steering toward a solution.

Replace:

> “What if we gave a five-alpha fine?”

with:

> “What possible responses could address the problem?”

Replace:

> “Should the student lose the privilege?”

with:

> “What response would be fair, effective, and aligned with our standards?”

Replace:

> “Can we say that students must put their things away?”

with:

> “What expectations could protect participation without removing useful tools?”

The Guide’s clarification should make the students’ thinking easier to understand—not replace it with adult thinking.
