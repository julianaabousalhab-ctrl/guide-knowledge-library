# Resolve Ties With a Protocol Students Already Understand

## Pattern

Adopt a tie-resolution protocol before the next tie occurs, and ensure that the protocol preserves student authority.

A tie should not automatically transfer the decision to the Guide. The community should already know what happens next, such as:

1. confirm that the count is accurate
2. reopen discussion for equal-strength arguments
3. hold a runoff between the tied options
4. if the runoff remains tied, retain the current rule or table the proposal until a majority emerges
5. use a neutral random method only when the tied outcomes are genuinely equivalent, such as deciding who goes first or which equally qualified student receives the first turn

The Guide may remind students of the agreed protocol, but should not become the deciding voter on an ordinary student-governed issue.

## Evidence

*1- The community used an existing tie rule tied to standards and majority support.*

> Student Facilitator: “It is a tied vote of three to three. [Guide], can you remind me what we were doing in the case of tied?”

> Guide: “So if it's a tie, then we typically refer to high standards and say, does one vote raise the standards of the school over another?”

> Guide: “If it is a subjective vote, meaning that this If this standard is just an opinion and not necessarily raising or lowering the standard of the school, then I would say the rule stands as is until a majority rule is voted on.”

> Student Facilitator: “Does anyone else have anything to say we can do a revote to?”

The tie did not give the Guide authority to choose the preferred policy. The existing rule preserved the current standard unless students produced a majority for a change, while still allowing students to continue discussion and re-vote.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1eGvfCjQGIauSBjROxd_fOVcVGci4trJd/view?usp=sharing)

*2- A known tiebreak routine was surfaced, and students chose a runoff limited to the tied candidates.*

> Students: “We have a tie.”

> Student: “Okay, so what do we need to do? Rock, paper, scissors.”

> Guide: “I know we usually break a tiebreaker with rock, paper, scissors, Before we go to that, do you guys want to try a re-vote with only [Student A] and [Student B]?”

> Students: “Yeah.”

> Guide: “That way we get kind of an even vote?”

> Students: “Yeah.”

The community recognized an established tiebreak method, but students retained the authority to select a more decision-relevant runoff between the two tied candidates.

## Anti-Pattern

The tie protocol includes deferring the decision to the Guide, or the Guide overrides a student-proposed runoff and announces the outcome personally.

This weakens student ownership even when students appear to consent. Students may choose to hand the decision to the adult because it is faster, because they do not understand the alternatives, or because the adult is positioned as the default authority.

*1- The established procedure allowed students to hand an ordinary policy decision to the Guide.*

> Guide: “Okay, we are at a tie. Right now, our tie breaker procedure is to either table it for next week or to defer to [Guide]. What would you guys like to do?”

> Student: “[Guide].”

> Guide: “Defer means let [Guide] have a vote.”

> Guide: “And I'm gonna hold to the don't look down agreement that we made last time. So we will do a rotation but it will not include don't look down.”

Although the procedure had been discussed previously, one of its options transferred the substantive decision to the adult. The final policy therefore came from the Guide rather than from a student majority.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/19S-ijp2RMOefdjvFMIqMmqQjYcMn1XJYhlIMUY1XwJY/edit?usp=sharing)

*2- The student mayor proposed another vote, but the Guide took over the decision.*

> Student Mayor: “We should take the vote again.”

> Guide: “OK, I'll break the tie and I'll decide.”

The student leader offered a student-owned resolution, but the Guide replaced it with an adult decision.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1oE8X1hb74GKIn-8-9heVeq9mnuuThDoS_4TYrk9HGIg/edit?usp=drive_link)

## Reusable Coaching Move

Create and adopt a **Tie Protocol Ladder** before the community encounters its next tied vote.

Display the protocol beside the voting expectations:

### Tie Protocol Ladder

1. **Verify**
   - Recount the votes.
   - Confirm that no one voted more than allowed.
   - Confirm that all tied options were clearly understood.

2. **Clarify**
   - Give each tied side the same amount of time.
   - Ask one student from each side to state the strongest reason for that option.
   - Allow one clarifying question per side.

3. **Runoff**
   - Re-vote only on the tied options.
   - Reset all prior votes.
   - Keep the same eligibility and vote limits.

4. **No Majority**
   - For a new rule or policy change, retain the current rule.
   - Table the proposal until the next Townhall or until students bring new evidence.
   - Record that no majority was reached.

5. **Equivalent Outcomes Only**
   - Use rock-paper-scissors, a wheel, or another neutral randomizer only when the options are substantively equivalent, such as:
     - who presents first
     - which equally qualified student takes the first shift
     - who receives an item first when both will receive it

The community should approve the ladder before using it.

At the moment of a tie, the Guide says:

> “We have a tie. What is the next step in the protocol?”

The student facilitator should then run the next step.

The Guide should not say:

> “I will decide.”

Instead, use:

> “There is no student majority yet, so the current rule stays in place and the proposal is tabled.”

A tie is not permission for adult control. It is evidence that the community needs a clearer question, stronger reasoning, a runoff, or more time.

## Confidence: Medium — 2 instances + 2 anti-pattern cases; the scope log notes tie procedures varied across campuses, so the protocol itself is less converged than the principle.
