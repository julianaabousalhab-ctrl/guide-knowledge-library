# Start Recurring Topics From the Current Adopted Rule

## Pattern

Maintain a visible record of Townhall decisions and begin every recurring agenda item by restating the current adopted rule.

The decision record should allow students to see:

- what the community decided
- when the decision was made
- whether the decision is active
- who or what the rule applies to
- how the rule is implemented
- whether the rule has a review date
- what would need to happen for the rule to change

When a recurring item returns to the agenda, the Guide or student facilitator should not reconstruct the rule from memory or begin as though no decision exists. The student facilitator first reads the current rule, checks that the community understands it, and then identifies the precise amendment being proposed.

This protects continuity, reduces unnecessary re-voting, and helps students experience Townhall decisions as real community laws rather than temporary conversations.

## Evidence

*1- The recurring jobs item began from two previously adopted operating rules.*

> Guide: “Next on the agenda is jobs. We previously determined that we would always do jobs at the beginning of the meeting so that we would not miss it. We also previously decided that the newsletter would be on rotation. So the next person to do the newsletter this week is going to be [Student A].”

> Guide: “[Student Leader], can you run the rest of jobs, please?”

The Guide did not reopen whether jobs belonged at the beginning or whether the newsletter rotated. Those adopted rules were treated as active, and the student leader continued from the next implementation step.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/12gNP8T65CwefQ5TtGFlsIfDMU-n7YKEu51fhLb6a-80/edit?usp=sharing)

*2- The facilitator repeatedly applied prior decisions to the current meeting process.*

> Guide: “Just a reminder, we voted two sessions ago, or two weeks ago, that we no longer need to put our heads down or close our eyes for voting.”

> Guide: “It cannot be [Student A] because of a previous vote said we could only have one person read one poster at a time so somebody else has to read this one.”

> Guide: “So we previously voted that anyone who had an Indy Mindy or a Yet Yeti could not get Bob, so that takes [Student A] and [Student B] out of the running, which means we have [Student C] and [Student D].”

Several recurring procedures were carried forward: voting format, poster-reader eligibility, and award eligibility. The meeting operated from the adopted rules instead of inventing new procedures each time.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1wkwAVuXT0uU8PaA4E3jG17SIjpr21I6PNScRogEZdaA/edit?usp=sharing)

*3- The meeting opened by recalling the standing sequence and current voting protocol.*

> Guide: “We had previously decided by vote that we were always going to start our agendas with Indie Mindy, Yet Yeti, and Bob.”

> Guide: “Remember that we previously decided whoever you get to read the indie mini poster cannot read any of the other posters.”

> Guide: “Our new protocol is, can anyone remember what our new protocol is?”

> Student: “Where we keep our hands up and raise our hand.”
  
> Guide: “And do we talk when we're raising our hand?”

> Student: “No, no.”

The facilitator treated previous decisions as the starting conditions for the meeting and asked students to recall the active protocol before using it.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/11UC8cpIRZCMX-Bx4hlBPA7s0iNhkOn8kqgdbplAVg3A/edit?usp=sharing)

## Anti-Pattern

The community has no accessible decision record, so students and Guides rely on memory to determine whether a previous rule still exists.

This produces avoidable confusion, repeated discussion, unnecessary re-voting, and inconsistent enforcement. A recurring topic may be reopened without first identifying the current rule, or an already adopted decision may be treated as though it expired simply because time passed.

*1- Students submitted a new agenda item because they could not tell whether an earlier rule was still active.*

> Student: “So, so when we were doing writing, we got confused if we get paid still or not because it was last year.”

> Student: “So I don't, we, I don't think we understood that it's still a thing because that's why we wrote [a Townhall slip] because we wanted to keep it up.”

> Guide: “Well yeah, let's, let's clarify some expectations or maybe let's just say quick vote, raise your hand if yes, you do want to be paid $10 per check.”

The community could not confirm whether the previous decision remained in force. Instead of reading an active decision record, the group moved toward voting again on a rule that apparently already existed.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/18LfXc8SThh4Du7E66y63uw1C2ezqw8pR-b2IXQTlxf8/edit?usp=drive_link)

*2- A previously decided rule resurfaced inside an unrelated discussion because the current rule was not anchored visibly at the start.*

> Student: “I think we should ban safety sword."

> Student: “And just to tell you, we already banned safety sword.”

> Student: “I know. What are we talking about right now?”

> Guide: “What we're talking about right now is whenever you have newsletter, you also have another job to do it with”

An already decided rule was reintroduced during a different agenda item, and the group lost track of the active decision question. A visible record and a clear “current rule / proposed change” frame would have prevented the drift.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1hxTYt9zmHuaVaNrsGSiZnxxW1GUJkT_Rqdd3tWHoNSo/edit?usp=sharing)

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Current Rule → Proposed Change → New Record** routine for every recurring item.

### 1. Display the current rule

Keep a student-visible decision log with these fields:

| Field | Example |
|---|---|
| Decision ID | TH-2026-014 |
| Topic | Newsletter rotation |
| Current rule | Newsletter rotates weekly |
| Adopted | 2026-03-26 |
| Applies to | All students in the community |
| Owner | Weekly newsletter student |
| Status | Active |
| Review date | End of session |
| Supersedes | Previous volunteer-only system |

### 2. Begin from the record

The student facilitator reads:

> “The current rule is ___. It was adopted on ___. It applies to ___. Today we are not deciding the whole topic again. The proposed change is ___.”

Then ask:

> “Does anyone need clarification on the current rule before we discuss the proposed change?”

### 3. Keep amendments narrow

Write both versions visibly:

**Current rule:**  
`Newsletter rotates weekly.`

**Proposed amendment:**  
`The newsletter student also receives a second recurring job.`

This prevents the group from drifting into already settled questions unless a student explicitly proposes changing them.

### 4. Update the record immediately after the vote

The student recorder adds:

- the winning decision
- the vote count
- the implementation owner
- the effective date
- any review date
- which previous rule the decision replaces

Read the final entry aloud:

> “The new rule is ___. It begins ___. The owner is ___. This replaces ___.”

### 5. Treat silence as continuity, not expiration

Unless the community set an expiration date, an adopted rule remains active until students amend or repeal it through Townhall.

The Guide should not ask:

> “Do we still want this?”

unless a student has submitted a proposal to reconsider it.

Instead, ask:

> “The current rule is still active. What specific change are you proposing?”

A visible decision record turns Townhall from a series of disconnected conversations into a functioning system of student governance.
