# Lock the Decision Before Opening the Vote

## Pattern

Turn discussion into one clearly defined decision at a time. Before voting begins, the Guide or student facilitator makes the decision architecture explicit:

- the exact question being decided
- the available options
- who is eligible to vote or be selected
- how many votes each person receives
- what threshold determines the result
- what the winning result will mean

When a topic contains several connected decisions, separate them and vote in sequence. For example, decide whether to adopt a proposal before deciding its implementation details, or narrow a large field of options before holding a final single-choice vote.

The Guide protects the clarity of the process without choosing the outcome for students.

## Evidence

*1- The facilitator reduced a broad discussion to two explicit options before the vote.*

> Facilitator: “So with the vote today, we will be voting to either keep the plus ones as is, or add additional plus ones from the poster.”

> Facilitator: “If you are in favor of adding More plus ones to the poster, please raise your arm. If you're in favor of keeping the plus ones as is, please raise your arm.”

> Facilitator: “We have a vote. And the vote is for now. We will keep the plus ones as is.”

The facilitator named one decision, presented two mutually exclusive options, and announced the result in the same terms used to frame the vote.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://app.read.ai/analytics/meetings/01K4E5QMNQG3E0JKCKQTQF1RTT?utm_source=Share_CopyLink)

*2- Related parts of a complex proposal were separated into distinct decisions.*

> Facilitator: “The next one was, do we want to show the top three? I guess these two counteract each other. So do you want to show only the top three, or do you want to show everyone's personal best? Let's vote on that.”

> Facilitator: “So the vote is, do you want just show the top three people on their records or do you want to show everyone's personal best score on the leaderboard?”

> Facilitator: “Alright, we will show everybody's personal best score up on the leaderboard, along with a total for the year and the total for the session. Cool. On to the vote for the reward.”

Instead of asking students to approve an entire leaderboard system at once, the facilitator isolated one design choice, resolved it, and only then moved to the separate reward decision.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://app.read.ai/analytics/meetings/01K4X9Z2MKFB1A2RN7BJC9SR11?utm_source=Share_CopyLink)

*3- A large option set was narrowed through a preliminary round followed by a final single-choice vote.*

> Facilitator: “Okay, heads down, I still want to give you five votes.”

> Student: “Do you have to use all the votes?”

> Facilitator: “No.”

> Facilitator: “We'll take the top three.”
>
> Student: “Wait, how many votes do we have?”
>
> Facilitator: “One.”
>
> Facilitator: “All right, so, one singular book, heads down, I suppose, you're choosing between Dog Man, The Unwanted, or City Spies.”

The first round allowed several preferences to surface. The runoff then clearly reset the vote limit to one and reduced the field to three named options.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1syN9opmukCQ1xpWJdhGKmAPyfQyMdBvSE85Hr2LCzVU/edit?tab=t.0)

*4- The facilitator applied previously adopted eligibility rules and clarified the number of votes.*

> Facilitator: “Just a reminder, we voted two sessions ago, or two weeks ago, that we no longer need to put our heads down or close our eyes for voting. [Student Leader], would you like to call the votes?”

> Facilitator: “Well, we have two anemones, so can we vote twice? Okay, [Student Leader], call it again.”

> Facilitator: “So we previously voted that anyone who had an Indy Mindy or a Yet Yeti could not get Bob, so that takes [Student A] and [Student B] out of the running, which means we have [Student C] and [Student D].”

The facilitator made both vote limits and candidate eligibility visible before the final selection was completed.

## Anti-Pattern

The facilitator begins voting before the group has confirmed what is being decided, which options are active, who is eligible, or how many votes each person has.

This produces restarts, contradictory counts, accidental double-voting, and outcomes that students may not trust. The Guide then spends more time repairing the vote than would have been required to clarify it beforehand.

*1- The group began voting without agreeing on the award category or vote limit.*

> Speaker A: “Who votes are we? How many do we vote for?”

> Speaker B: “2.”

> Speaker A: “We get 2, right? Okay, so vote for 2.”
  
> Speaker B: “Weren't we doing yet Yeti?”

> Speaker A: “Oh, I thought we were doing indie. Mindy, are we doing?”

> Speaker B: “Yes, we were doing yet Yeti.”

> Speaker A: “Oh, you guys didn't read the right things. Okay, let's try again Yet Yeti.”

The vote had to restart because the group had not locked the category, nominations, and number of votes before beginning.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1hxTYt9zmHuaVaNrsGSiZnxxW1GUJkT_Rqdd3tWHoNSo/edit?usp=sharing)

*2- The number of available winners and the eligibility rule remained unresolved throughout the discussion.*

> Facilitator: “My question is, if I only have one Timmy, and five people make it into Timeback Terminal, or four, or three, or two, how do we know who gets the tiger?”

> Facilitator: “Are we saying that whoever the top three Tied Back Terminal people are, are the ones who are gonna get the time back to me?”
  
> Facilitator: “I also want to remind you that there are three in Indie Mindies but we only give out one. So should we only give out one Time Back Terminal, Timmy? Or all of the ones that are available?”

The discussion moved among one winner, three winners, sharing, ranking, and random selection without first establishing the single policy question the community needed to decide.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Decision Lock** before every vote.

Write or display these six fields:

| Decision Lock | Entry |
|---|---|
| Question | What exactly are we deciding? |
| Options | What choices may receive a vote? |
| Eligibility | Who may vote, and who or what may be selected? |
| Vote limit | How many choices may each voter make? |
| Threshold | Majority, plurality, unanimous, or runoff? |
| Effect | What happens immediately if an option wins? |

Ask the student facilitator to read the completed lock aloud:

> “We are deciding ___. The options are ___. The people eligible are ___. Each person gets ___ vote(s). The winning option needs ___. If it wins, ___ will happen.”

Then use a comprehension check:

> “Before we vote, can someone who did not frame the question repeat what we are deciding and how many votes each person gets?”

For complex topics, create a decision sequence:

1. **Principle:** Do we want this change?
2. **Model:** Which version do we want?
3. **Implementation:** Who owns it, when does it begin, and how will it work?
4. **Review:** When will the community examine the result?

If confusion appears after voting starts, stop immediately:

> “Pause. The Decision Lock was not clear. No votes will count yet.”

Clarify the fields, reset all hands or ballots, and restart the vote from zero.

A high-quality vote is not simply one in which every student participates. It is one in which every student understands the same question, the same options, the same eligibility rules, and the same limits before expressing a choice.
