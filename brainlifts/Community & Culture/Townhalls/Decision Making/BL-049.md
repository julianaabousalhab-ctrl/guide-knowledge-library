# Test the Motion Before Opening the Vote

## Pattern

Require the community to examine three things before a proposal can move to a vote:

1. **BothSides:** Students articulate a credible case for the proposal and a credible concern, alternative, or opposing position.
2. **Feasibility:** Students identify whether the proposal can actually work within the available time, space, materials, money, technology, safety requirements, and adult-only constraints.
3. **Standards impact:** Students explain whether the proposal raises, maintains, or lowers the community’s standards.

The Guide does not argue one side or steer students toward a preferred result. The Guide protects the quality of the decision by asking neutral questions and delaying the vote until students understand the trade-offs.

A proposal is not ready merely because students like it, agree quickly, or are eager to vote.

## Evidence

*1- Students explored meaningful arguments on both sides before deciding whether voting should be public.*

> Student A: “I mean, public voting is like when you're when we're voting in town hall, we can like we can open our eyes and like see what they're voting.”

> Student B: “I don't really want that because that's kind of like showing who you're voting for and that could spark some arguments.”

> Student B: “if we do eyes up, then if a majority votes for it, you automatically go with them.”

> Student C: “Everybody likes that idea, but one person raises their hand for no. Everybody raises their hand for no because they're popular.”

> Student C: “And then it becomes very, very biased and unfair.”

Students moved beyond personal preference and examined accountability, social pressure, popularity bias, arguments, and hurt feelings. The competing positions were substantive enough to inform the vote.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/18EgKiRWcLS42LYslotl8g0rQhh9oULFfSYtObL39rOM/edit?usp=sharing)

*2- A student challenged a proposal to explain how it would raise the standard before supporting it.*

> Student A: “for me, honestly, to vote on what I want, I need to know like, an example of what we should might add to the plus ones.”

> Student B: “I know you want to add, and I understand your reasoning for wanting to add those, but like, is there any reason? Is there how, how does it raise the bar of our school”

> Student C: “So, like, this is just a quick decision that you made?”

> Student A: “Yeah.”

> Student C: “Okay. I think I'm definitely going to [the opposing option] right now.”

The students did not treat the existence of a proposal as sufficient reason to approve it. They asked for a concrete example and required the proposer to connect the idea to raising the bar.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://app.read.ai/analytics/meetings/01K4E5QMNQG3E0JKCKQTQF1RTT?utm_source=Share_CopyLink)

*3- The community tested availability, cost, and product constraints before deciding how trading cards could work.*

> Student A: “I think that's a pretty good idea. I like Pokemon, but also it might be hard to get Pokemon card packages”

> Facilitator: “The newest sets, like the ones that just came out recently, are very difficult to find.”

> Facilitator: “So something that I want you guys to think about is, is it important to you that it's the new set or do you just want Pokemon cards in general?”

> Facilitator: “there is literally no way for us to tell what is in a pack of Pokemon cards without opening it.”

> Facilitator: “There's no way for us to guarantee you getting specific types or cards unless we opened all the packs”

The facilitator introduced relevant constraints without deciding for students. Students then had to distinguish between new and older sets, random packs and individual cards, and different pricing implications.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1WYLTar5Skr49zPstKxzG4w4njxZlEpaX/view?usp=sharing)

*4- The Guide tested whether a proposed incentive could be measured and implemented without undermining focus.*

> Guide: “For example, what happens if a desk pet becomes distracting or you had 100% and then you lost your 100%?”
  
> Student: “Then it gets taken back.”

> Guide: “How are we going to measure this?”

> Student: “Every 10 minutes.”

> Guide: “Is that going to help us stay focused in our apps if we look at it every 10 minutes?”

> Student: “No.”

The Guide helped students discover an implementation weakness by asking how the proposal would be measured and whether the measurement system supported the intended academic standard.

## Anti-Pattern

The facilitator opens or completes a vote while the discussion is still based on enthusiasm, personal preference, or shallow agreement—and postpones feasibility, earning conditions, or standards analysis until after approval.

This produces decisions that may be popular but are not yet rigorous, executable, or aligned with the culture of excellence.

*1- The group voted for a field trip after little reasoning and deferred the earning model until afterward.*

> Student: “I think yes, because I was the one who put it.”

> Facilitator: “Don't just say yes. You need to have no reason.”

> Facilitator: “Okay, nobody has something.”

> Facilitator: “Raise your hands.”

> Facilitator: “We will work out a way to earn that soon”

> Facilitator: “Last but not least, we're doing the field trip to see Harry Potter at some point, but it has to be earned by the individual students.”

Although the facilitator briefly requested reasoning, the vote proceeded without a developed opposing case, feasibility review, or defined earning condition. A key implementation detail was left until after the proposal passed.

Source artifact: [Townhall](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1nMlr6BcSYdXRFUxN6Z6dlyQbUgLHupq-deW0kmW9BbM/edit?usp=drive_link)

*2- Students approved an incentive while explicitly postponing the operational details.*

> Facilitator: “In a unanimous decision, we are voting on earning Magic the Gathering cards based on performance in game against equal or greater players.”

> Facilitator: “Details of those winnings can be clarified at a later date. We'll come back to that if needed.”

The community approved the incentive without first deciding what would be earned, how much would be earned, how comparable skill would be determined, or how the system would be administered.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Three-Gate Vote Check** before the student facilitator asks for votes.

### Gate 1: BothSides

Ask:

> “Who can make the strongest case for this proposal?”

> “Who can name a real concern, trade-off, or alternative?”

> “Have we heard a different perspective, or only versions of agreement?”

Do not accept a performative opposing side. The concern should be strong enough that a reasonable student could vote differently because of it.

### Gate 2: Feasibility

Ask:

> “What would have to be true for this to work?”

> “What time, materials, money, space, technology, or adult permission would it require?”

> “Who would carry it out?”

> “What could make this fail?”

> “Are we voting on something the community can actually implement?”

A constraint should narrow or improve the proposal, not become an adult veto after the vote.

### Gate 3: Standards Impact

Ask:

> “How does this raise or maintain our community standards?”

> “What behavior will this encourage?”

> “Could this reward something students are already expected to do?”

> “Does this make excellence more likely, or only make something easier or more enjoyable?”

> “What evidence would show that the decision improved the community?”

### Vote-ready statement

The student facilitator completes this statement before opening the vote:

> “We heard the strongest reason for this proposal: ___. We heard the strongest concern or alternative: ___. We know the proposal is feasible because ___. It raises or maintains our standards by ___. The question we are now voting on is ___.”

If any blank cannot be completed, the motion is not ready. The group should clarify it, revise it, table it, or gather missing information.

The Guide’s role is to guard the three gates—not to supply the winning argument or decide which side is correct.
