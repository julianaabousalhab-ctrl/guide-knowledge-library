# Make Mistakes Recoverable and Keep Students in the Task

## Pattern
Design a clear recovery pathway so that an ordinary mistake does not end a student’s participation. When an error occurs, the student should acknowledge it, complete a brief reset, and re-enter the same task quickly.

The recovery routine should be known before the activity begins and simple enough to use without a long interruption. This allows students to experience mistakes as information within the learning process rather than as a final judgment about their ability. The Guide reinforces the pattern by directing students to continue, finish, or rejoin instead of treating the first error as elimination.

This pattern applies to ordinary learning and performance mistakes. Unsafe behavior or deliberate disruption may require a separate accountability response, but that response should not be confused with the learning routine for recoverable errors.

## Evidence

*1— The activity includes an immediate reset-and-rejoin routine*

In a listening challenge, the Guide establishes a nonverbal response to mistakes. A student who makes an error completes a brief action and returns to the same position rather than being removed from the game.

> “So when I see a mistake, I’m tapping their shoulder.”

> “They will go ring the gong and then come right back to their original spot.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the mistake is acknowledged without becoming terminal. The gong makes the error visible, while returning to the original spot preserves participation and gives the student another opportunity to listen successfully.

*2— The Guide directs students to finish after an error*

During a timed movement challenge, the Guide repeatedly prompts students to continue through mistakes and explicitly contrasts finishing with quitting.

> “Keep going, keep going, keep going, finish. Keep going, keep going, finish, finish, finish.”

> “Why is it important to continue even if you make a mistake?”

> “So if you make a mistake, should you quit right away, or should you finish?”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide does not allow an error to erase the remainder of the attempt. Students practice recovering while the task is still underway, making persistence an observable action rather than only a discussion point.

## Anti-Pattern

*Treating a minor performance error as immediate failure or removal*

In a silent physical challenge, the Guide defines several deviations as failing the entire exercise, including talking or ending the movement early. No reset or re-entry pathway is provided.

> “If you talk during it, you are out and uh you you you fail the exercise.”

> “If you uh come up too early, you fail the exercise.”

This weakens the desired pattern because one mistake ends the student’s opportunity to regulate, correct the error, and demonstrate improvement within the task. The consequence may produce compliance, but it does not let students practice recovery.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Notice–Reset–Rejoin** routine:

1. **Notice:** Name or signal the mistake neutrally: “That was the error.”
2. **Reset:** Give the student one brief recovery action, such as taking a breath, returning to the start marker, restating the direction, or ringing a reset signal.
3. **Rejoin:** Direct the student back into the task immediately: “Reset complete—continue.”
4. **Finish:** Encourage completion even when the attempt will not count as perfect.
5. **Reflect:** Afterward, ask, “What helped you recover and stay in the task?”

Before the Launch, state the routine in one sentence:

> “When you make a mistake, you will ___, then immediately rejoin and keep going.”

Avoid using elimination for ordinary mistakes when continued participation is safe. Reserve removal for genuine safety concerns or behavior that prevents others from learning, and provide a separate repair pathway for returning.
