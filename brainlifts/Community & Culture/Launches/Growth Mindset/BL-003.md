# Convert Growth Mindset Language Into a Concrete Next Move

## Pattern
When a student uses Growth Mindset language, immediately connect the phrase to an observable next action. Ask what the student will change, try, inspect, practice, or do differently now.

Statements such as “I can do hard things,” “mistakes help us grow,” and “failure is fuel” can help students regulate their emotions, but the language does not improve performance by itself. The Guide turns the phrase into learning by requiring a decision and a next move that responds to the actual obstacle.

The next move should be specific enough to observe. Examples include changing the order of steps, practicing one smaller section, checking a source of error, adjusting the structure, asking a teammate for targeted input, or testing a different method.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide redirects “mindset” into a plan*

Before students begin an untangling challenge, the Guide asks how they will approach the task. When a student answers with the abstract word “mindset,” the Guide accepts the disposition but requires a strategy.

> “How are you going to untangle? Come up with a plan.”

> “Mindset.”

> “Sure. Have a growth mindset, but come up with a plan.”

The students then begin proposing concrete actions:

> “One chance to go under at a time.”

> “Yes. Let’s do [one student] first and then [another student]…”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide does not treat “growth mindset” as a complete response. The phrase becomes useful only after students identify an executable sequence.

*2- “Mistakes help us grow” is followed by diagnosis and retry*

During a chime activity, the Guide uses Growth Mindset language and then immediately directs the student to inspect the specific source of the error before trying again.

> “That’s okay. Mistakes help us grow. Let’s take a look at that.”

> “I noticed you saw two red ones.”

> “Do you think it’s the one that’s a low note or a high note?”

> “Okay, go ahead and try again.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the affirmation is paired with a concrete learning process: look closely, distinguish between two possibilities, and retry using the new information.

## Anti-Pattern

*Ending with the slogan instead of asking what the student will do*

At the end of a pencil-tower Launch, students are asked to select something they will say when work becomes difficult.

> “Share one thing they’re gonna say to themselves when something is hard today.”

> “When things get harder, I try harder.”

> “Failure is fuel.”

The affirmations may support persistence, but the exchange stops before students name a concrete response to difficulty. “Try harder” does not clarify whether the student will strengthen the base, change the spacing, restart, inspect the failure point, or use another strategy. Without that next move, Growth Mindset remains motivational language rather than an actionable learning process.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Phrase–Decision–Action** prompt:

1. **Phrase:** Let the student name the Growth Mindset statement.
   - “Mistakes help me grow.”
2. **Decision:** Ask what the obstacle is telling them.
   - “What specifically is not working yet?”
3. **Action:** Require one observable next move.
   - “What will you change on this attempt?”
4. **Test:** Let the student act immediately.
   - “Try it and see what happens.”
5. **Check:** Compare the action with the result.
   - “What did that change improve?”

Useful Guide prompts include:

> “What does that phrase tell you to do next?”

> “Show me what ‘try harder’ looks like in this task.”

> “What is one part you can change right now?”

> “What evidence from the mistake will you use on your next move?”

Do not reject positive self-talk. Treat it as the beginning of the response, then coach the student to turn it into a strategy that can be seen, tested, and revised.
