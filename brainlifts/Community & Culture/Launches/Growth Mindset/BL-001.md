# Build a Second Attempt Into the Launch and Require a Strategy Change

## Pattern

Plan at least two attempts at the same underlying challenge. Between attempts, pause students to examine what happened, identify one specific strategy change, and then test that change in the next attempt.

The second attempt should not be a blind repetition. It should give students an immediate opportunity to use evidence from the first attempt, revise their approach, and see whether the revision improves the result. This makes Growth Mindset observable: students do not merely say that they will keep trying; they change what they do when they try again.


## Evidence

 *1-A failed build becomes a revised build*
 
During a pencil-tower challenge, the Guide responds to instability by asking students to identify what they can change. When a student restarts, the Guide again asks for a change and names the improved foundation rather than praising repetition alone.

> “What can we change to make it better?”

> “Okay, starting over, I like it.”

> “What are you gonna change?”

> “I love how you started over and you have a really strong foundation now. Good job adjusting.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the student receives another attempt and the Guide makes strategy revision—not simply persistence—the condition of the retry.

*2 — The Guide narrows the task, diagnoses the error, and asks for another attempt*

During a chime activity, the Guide changes the approach from attempting the whole sequence to practicing a smaller section. After an error, the Guide directs attention to the specific note distinction and then releases the student to retry.

> “So do you want to try the first one to the second row?”

> “That’s okay. Mistakes help us grow. Let’s take a look at that.”

> “Do you think it’s the one that’s a low note or a high note?”

> “Okay, go ahead and try again.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the second attempt uses a changed strategy: reduce the task to a smaller section, inspect the source of the mistake, and retry with that information.

## Anti-Pattern

*Debriefing the failure without preserving an opportunity to test the revision*

In a blindfolded maze challenge, the first attempt ends unsuccessfully. The Guide identifies that more specific directions would likely help, but time expires and students do not receive another attempt in which to test that change.

> “Unfortunately, we do not have time to keep trying, but I do have a few questions for you.”

> “If we were more specific and she said, take two foot steps to the front with your foot a little to the left. Is that more descriptive than go there?”

The reflection identifies a plausible strategy change, but the learning remains verbal. Students leave without evidence that more specific directions would improve their performance. A debrief is not a substitute for a retry when the intended lesson is revision through experience.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use an **Attempt–Revise–Retry** sequence:

1. **Attempt:** Keep the first attempt short enough that time remains for another try.
2. **Notice:** Ask, “What happened?” and “What evidence shows where you got stuck?”
3. **Revise:** Require each student or team to complete the sentence: “On our next attempt, we will change ___ because ___.”
4. **Retry:** Run the same underlying challenge again so students can test the stated change.
5. **Compare:** Ask, “What changed in the result because you changed your strategy?”

Do not accept “try harder,” “focus more,” or “use a Growth Mindset” as the full revision. Prompt for an observable change such as adjusting the base, reducing the task to one section, changing the sequence, assigning roles, or making directions more specific.
