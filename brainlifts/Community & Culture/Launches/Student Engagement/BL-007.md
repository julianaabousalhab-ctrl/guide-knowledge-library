# Engagement is measured by thinking, not movement

## Pattern
Across the strongest Launches, Guides created tasks that forced students to engage cognitively. Engagement came from continuous decision-making — not simply through participation or task completion.

## Evidence
In the Rainbow Drawing challenge, the Guide created constraints that required students to constantly coordinate, adjust, and rethink their strategy, especially after removing verbal communication in the second round. Students continuously negotiated actions:
> "We should make the cloud first."
> "Hold it like that."
> "Don't hold it as hard as you can."

Students observed and adapted based on other teams:
> "Guys, look. Look, look, look."

Students remained cognitively engaged because success depended on constant decision-making, not repetitive execution.

Source artifact: [Rainbow Drawing Challenge (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1_M6psUaQCIvxErm5qIpS76ZtUHi0r2gw/view?usp=sharing)

In the House of Cards challenge, the task required students to continuously make strategic decisions as their designs succeeded or failed. Rather than repeatedly performing the same action, students constantly evaluated, adjusted, and negotiated their approach. Students proposed strategies:
> "Listen, we have to make two so we can do more stable in the middle."
> "Let's just not do the second base. Let's just keep on doing the normal base."

Students adapted based on observation:
> "They were like bending the cards... flat it's easier to fall over."

Students monitored and revised together:
> "It's okay. It's okay."
> "We got this. We got this."

The engagement came from making ongoing design decisions, not simply stacking cards.

Source artifact: [House of Cards Challenge (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1dY1kBuN2iQZF6CN6e-tYMlpJS21YtuDz/view?usp=sharing)

## Anti-Pattern
In the Self-Love Launch, students stayed occupied throughout the Launch by completing individual writing tasks, but once instructions were given, the activity required relatively little strategic thinking or continuous decision-making. The Guide primarily directed each step:
> "Take a mirror... say one kind thing to yourself."
> "You are going to make the longest self-love link chain."
> "Write what you love about yourself."

During the work period, most interactions were procedural reminders:
> "Think about those choices that you make."
> "You have about two minutes."
> "40 seconds."

Students largely worked independently with minimal evidence of questioning, debating, revising, or making strategic choices. Although students were consistently occupied, the activity emphasized task completion more than continuous cognitive engagement, making it an example of students being busy rather than mentally engaged.

Source artifact: [Self-Love Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1H5B3E3KGFIj2hYnLeZs6orUlXDyo16zw/view?usp=sharing)

## Reusable Coaching Move
Before running a Launch, ask:
> "After students know the instructions, what decisions will they still have to make?"

If the answer is minimal, redesign the task.
