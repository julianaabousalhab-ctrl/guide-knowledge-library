# Give Every Student an Interdependent Role

## Pattern

Design the challenge so that every student has a defined contribution and the group cannot complete the task successfully without the roles working together.

An interdependent role is more than giving each student something to do. Each role should hold information, access, authority, or an action that another role needs. The roles create a reason for students to communicate, listen, coordinate, and respond to one another.

Strong roles answer three questions:

- **What is this student responsible for?**
- **What can this student do or know that the others cannot?**
- **How does the team depend on that contribution?**

Roles may rotate between rounds, but every student should have an active responsibility during each round. Students who are waiting, watching, or listening should also receive an observable job that contributes to the next decision.

## Evidence

*1- One partner holds the information while the other controls the materials*

In a puzzle challenge, the Guide assigns two complementary roles. One student can see the reference image and give directions, while the other student can handle the puzzle pieces.

> “One person on in the partnership is going to be the teller and telling them what to do.”

> “And the other person in the partnership is going to be able to touch the puzzle pieces.”

> “Only the teller can come and look at the cover of the board.”

> “Only the people who are the putters, the people who are putting the puzzle together will be able to touch the puzzle pieces.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because neither partner can complete the puzzle alone. The teller has access to the visual information but cannot manipulate the pieces, while the putter can manipulate the pieces but depends on the teller’s communication.

*2- The walker depends on the guide’s communication*

In a maze challenge, students first collaborate to build the course and then take complementary roles during the navigation phase.

> “One of you will be the walker, and the other one will be the guide.”

> “The walker is going to be blindfolded, walking through the maze.”

> “The guides only have to have really good communication to help the walker get through the maze safely without touching the [lava].”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the walker controls the movement but cannot see the route, while the guide can see the route but cannot complete it directly. Success requires the guide’s precise communication and the walker’s listening and execution.

## Anti-Pattern

*Giving students individual turns while everyone else only waits or stays quiet*

In a presentation sequence, one student is called to the front while the rest of the class is directed to be quiet.

> “[Student], come on out to the front, please.”

> “[Student], you’re up.”

> “Hey class, be quiet.”

> “Let’s afford [the student] the same opportunity that we gave everyone else, please.”

The presenter has an active role, but the transcript does not assign the remaining students an observable contribution such as identifying evidence, recording feedback, comparing ideas, or preparing a question. Taking turns can ensure that everyone eventually participates, but it does not create interdependence during each student’s turn. Much of the group’s time can therefore become passive waiting.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Need–Role–Dependency** planning sequence:

1. **Identify what the task requires.**
   - Information, construction, movement, observation, timing, feedback, or quality checking.
2. **Divide those needs into distinct roles.**
   - Describer, builder, navigator, mover, evidence collector, checker, or recorder.
3. **Give each role exclusive responsibility.**
   - “Only the describer sees the model.”
   - “Only the builder touches the materials.”
4. **State the dependency.**
   - “The builder cannot succeed without clear directions, and the describer cannot complete the structure without the builder.”
5. **Rotate roles.**
   - Switch after a short round so every student practices more than one contribution.
6. **Debrief the interaction.**
   - “What did your partner need from your role?”
   - “How did your role affect the team’s result?”

A useful role test is:

> “If this student stopped participating, would the team have to change what it was doing?”

When the answer is no, the role may be decorative rather than interdependent. Add a real responsibility, information source, decision, or action that the group needs.

For sequential activities such as presentations, assign active audience roles:

- one student identifies the clearest evidence;
- one records a question;
- one listens for the target strategy;
- one provides specific feedback; and
- one summarizes the idea before the next turn.

The purpose is not to make every student perform the same action simultaneously. The purpose is to ensure that every student’s contribution matters to the shared task.
