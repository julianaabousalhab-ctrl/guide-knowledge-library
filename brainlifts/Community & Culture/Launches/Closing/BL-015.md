# Signal that the Launch is complete before transitioning to the next block

## Pattern
Across the strongest Launches, Guides created a clean boundary between the learning experience and the next block of the day. After the final takeaway, they signaled that the Launch was complete, named the next activity, gave one concrete transition direction, and moved students immediately.

This brainlift begins after the lesson takeaway has been crystallized. It is not about adding one more teaching point; it is about closing the container and giving students a clear next step.

## Evidence
In the Balance Board / Grit Launch, the Guide clearly marked the end of the Launch before moving students into 2-hour learning.
> "Those are the things with hard work, consistency, practice that you can get truly awesome at. Sound good?"

> "Okay, guys. We're going to head into… transition into 2-hour learning time."

Students receive a clear signal that the Launch learning experience is over and the next part of the day is beginning.

The strength of this example is not just the final message about grit. The strength is that the Guide marks the end of the Launch with “Sound good?” and then clearly names the next block: “transition into 2-hour learning time.”

Source artifact: [Balance Board / Grit Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1ngVybQbHRgBnusbB1k2U6jhT8mjqjDaZ/view?usp=sharing)

In the Growth Mindset Chime / Music Instrument Launch, the Guide clearly closes the Launch, names the transition, and uses a small closing ritual before sending students onward.
> "We're going to get ready for our core skills."

> "Awesome job with that growth mindset. Don't forget to bring it with you when you start your core skills."

> Guide: "All right, let's bring it in…"

> Guide: "Go take your growth mindset with you."

The Guide does not drift into the next activity. They explicitly say the Launch is moving into core skills, celebrate the completed work, use a brief "bring it in" moment, and then release students. That creates a clean closing boundary.

This Launch may be an anti-pattern for extended Guide talk during practice, but its closing is still strong because the Guide creates a clear boundary before sending students to Core Skills.

## Anti-Pattern
In the Feedback / Kind and Helpful Launch, the Guide transitions into Core Skills goals and computer time, but the close is blended with operational reminders rather than clearly signaling, "the Launch learning experience is now complete."
> Guide: "Guys, I have some feedback for y'all. In core skills, in time back, y'all have been killing it."

> Guide: "Today, I want y'all to remember all of the goals that we have, not just for today, but for Friday as well."

> Guide: "All right, let's keep crushing this time, guys. Let's go ahead and go to our computers and do some time."

The problem is not that the Guide gave operational reminders. The problem is that the operational reminders replaced a clean Launch close. Students hear Core Skills goals, feedback, and computer directions before receiving a clear signal that the Launch itself is complete.

## Reusable Coaching Move
Close → Carry-forward Cue → Transition.

After the final takeaway, end every Launch with three moves:

Close: clearly signal that the Launch is complete.
> “That is our Launch for today.”

> “That is the big idea we are taking with us.”

Carry-forward cue: connect the lesson to the next part of the day in one sentence.
>“Bring that growth mindset into Core Skills.”

>“Use that same teamwork when you move into your next block.”

Transition: give one concrete direction and move students immediately.
> “Open your laptop and go to Core Skills.”

> “Stand up, push in your chair, and head to 2-hour learning.”

Do not reopen discussion, ask a new reflection question, or add a new teaching point during the transition.
A carry-forward cue is allowed; new teaching is not.
