# Preserve Continuity by Naming When the Next Attempt Will Happen

## Pattern

When students cannot complete, improve, or retry the challenge during the current Launch, explicitly name when the next attempt will happen and what learning students should carry into it.

A strong continuity close answers:

- **When will we return to this?**
- **What will we attempt again?**
- **What will we preserve or change based on today’s evidence?**
- **How should students prepare for the next attempt?**

Naming “tomorrow,” “during Q Break,” or another specific block turns an unfinished task into a paused learning sequence rather than an abandoned activity. It communicates that the result is not final and that reflection from the current attempt will matter later.

The next attempt should be credible and specific enough that students can anticipate it. “We can try again sometime” does not create the same accountability or continuity as “We will try this again tomorrow during Launch.”

This pattern is especially important when:

- the timer ends before students succeed;
- the challenge is intentionally difficult;
- students identify a strategy but cannot test it immediately;
- only some students receive a turn; or
- the Launch ends after an unsuccessful attempt.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide schedules the retry for the following day and connects it to today’s learning*

After students do not complete the challenge successfully, the Guide makes clear that the current result is not the endpoint:

> “Are we always going to be successful the first time we try something?”

> “Does that mean we give up right away?”

The Guide then names the next attempt:

> “So, we’re going to do this again tomorrow.”

The Guide also explains what students should carry forward:

> “And we’re going to figure out how to use today’s failure as fuel for tomorrow’s success.”

The close confirms the commitment:

> “Okay, we’re doing it again tomorrow, so be ready.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide does more than encourage persistence. The Guide identifies the exact time—tomorrow—and frames the next attempt as an opportunity to use evidence from the current failure.

*2- The Guide names the next available block for continuing the activity*

When the Launch does not have enough time for every desired round, the Guide names when the activity can resume:

> “We have time for one more.”

After the final turn, the Guide states:

> “So, we can continue during Q break.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because students are not left with a vague promise that the activity may continue later. The Guide identifies a specific part of the day—Q Break—where the remaining participation can happen.

## Anti-Pattern

*Ending an unsuccessful attempt without identifying when students will retry*

After a blindfolded student does not make it safely through the maze, the Guide states:

> “She is in the lava and did not make it across safely.”

The Guide then explains the time constraint:

> “Unfortunately, we do not have time to keep trying, but I do have a few questions for you.”

The reflection identifies a useful adjustment:

> “If we were more specific and she said, take two foot steps to the front with your foot a little to the left. Is that more descriptive than go there?”

The Launch ends with cleanup and a transition:

> “Okay, awesome. Let’s quickly clean this up and get started on our two-hour learning.”

The Guide helps students identify what should change, but the transcript does not name when they will test the more specific directions. The learning sequence ends after diagnosis, so students do not know whether the failed attempt will be revisited or whether the proposed strategy will ever be tested.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Pause–Schedule–Carry Forward** close:

1. **Pause:** Name the status of the current attempt honestly.
   - “We did not complete the maze within the time.”
   - “Not everyone received a turn.”
2. **Schedule:** Name the next attempt precisely.
   - “We will try again tomorrow during Launch.”
   - “The remaining pairs will continue during Q Break.”
3. **Carry forward:** State what will be preserved or changed.
   - “Keep the same roles, but replace broad directions with a number of steps and a direction.”
   - “Use the wider base you identified during today’s reflection.”
4. **Confirm readiness:** Ask students to name the plan.
   - “When are we trying again?”
   - “What will we change first?”
5. **Follow through:** Reopen the next attempt by referencing the prior evidence.
   - “Yesterday, we learned that ‘go there’ was too broad. What will we say instead?”

A concise closing script is:

> “We are pausing here. We will try again during ___. When we return, we will keep ___ and change ___.”

Examples:

> “We are pausing the tower challenge. Tomorrow during Launch, we will rebuild using the base strategies we compared today.”

> “Two pairs still need a turn. They will continue during Q Break, and the observers will keep the same feedback job.”

> “The maze attempt ended before the walker crossed. We will retry Friday morning using directions that include distance and direction.”

Avoid closing with only:

- “We’ll do this again.”
- “Maybe we can try later.”
- “We ran out of time.”
- “We’ll talk about it another day.”
- “Next time, do better.”

These statements acknowledge that the work is unfinished but do not create continuity. Name the next opportunity and the learning students will carry into it.
