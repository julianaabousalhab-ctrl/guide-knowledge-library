# Bridge the Lesson to the Next Context With a Concrete Cue

## Pattern

Close the Launch by naming the next relevant context, the moment that should trigger the lesson, and the action students should take when that moment occurs.

A concrete bridge answers:

- **Where will students use the lesson next?**
- **What will happen that signals it is time to use the skill?**
- **What should students do or say at that moment?**

For example:

> “When you begin TimeBack Learning and reach the first problem that feels difficult, tell yourself, ‘I can get better with practice,’ and try one new strategy.”

The cue makes the transfer usable. “Bring your Growth Mindset with you” points students in the right direction, but “when you get stuck” tells them when to activate it. “Use clear communication in workshop” is stronger when paired with “when your teammate cannot act on your direction, add a distance, direction, or sequence.”

This is a closing move rather than a redesign of the next task. The Guide is creating a clear mental bridge so students can recognize the upcoming moment and retrieve the strategy they just practiced.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide connects a Growth Mindset phrase to getting stuck during Core Skills*

During the Launch, the Guide establishes the trigger for using the strategy:

> “For you when you get stuck with the chime along, if it gets a little tricky, think of something, a phrase you like to tell yourself when you’re stuck.”

A student identifies a phrase:

> “You got this.”

Near the close, the Guide broadens the context and asks when the phrase will be needed:

> “Whether it’s during the workshop, check chart, academics, what would you like to hear?”

> “Any specific time of your day?”

The Guide then bridges directly into the next context:

> “We’re going to get ready for our core skills.”

> “Awesome job with that growth mindset. Don’t forget to bring it with you when you start your core skills.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because students leave with a context—Core Skills—and a concrete cue—when the work becomes tricky or they get stuck. The action is to use the Growth Mindset phrase practiced during the Launch.

*2- The start of TimeBack Learning becomes the cue to use the independence practiced in the Launch*

At the beginning of the Launch, the Guide assigns students an authentic independence task:

> “I want you to set up your space for the day all by yourself.”

> “I want you to get set up for time back on the yellow chair.”

> “I want you to grab all your stuff that you think you need for time back on those tables.”

At the close, the Guide names the transition cue:

> “Guess what time it is now?”

> “It’s time for time back, and we’re already ready to go.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the beginning of TimeBack Learning is an immediate and recognizable cue. Students can see the consequence of the independence strategy they practiced: they prepared the necessary materials without waiting for the next Guide direction and can now begin the learning block.

## Anti-Pattern

*Naming the general lesson before the next task without specifying when or how to use it*

After a maze challenge, the Guide summarizes the importance of communication:

> “Teamwork and listening and communication are so, so important because without it, you don’t know what your teammate is asking you to do.”

> “But if you can be specific, if you can be direct, if you can be clear, your teammate will know, okay, this is exactly what needs to be done.”

The Guide then transitions:

> “Okay, awesome. Let’s quickly clean this up and get started on our two-hour learning.”

The summary identifies a useful strategy, but the closing does not name a cue students should watch for during the next learning block. Students are not told what situation in TimeBack Learning should trigger clear communication or what specific communication action they should take. The lesson is relevant, but the retrieval path remains broad.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Context–Cue–Action** closing:

1. **Context:** Name the next setting.
   - “You are moving into TimeBack Learning.”
   - “Your workshop team meets this afternoon.”
2. **Cue:** Name the moment students should notice.
   - “When you reach a problem that feels difficult…”
   - “When your teammate looks confused or cannot follow the direction…”
3. **Action:** State the strategy students should use.
   - “…pause, use your Growth Mindset phrase, and try a different approach.”
   - “…replace ‘go there’ with a direction that includes distance and direction.”
4. **Confirm retrieval:** Ask one student to complete the bridge.
   - “When will you use the strategy?”
   - “What will you do when that happens?”
5. **Transition with the cue still active.**
   - “TimeBack Learning starts now. Watch for your first stuck moment.”

A concise closing sentence is:

> “In ___, when you notice ___, do ___.”

Examples:

> “In Core Skills, when you notice yourself saying ‘I can’t,’ add ‘yet’ and choose one next attempt.”

> “In workshop, when your partner cannot act on your direction, make the direction more specific.”

> “At the start of TimeBack Learning, independently check that you have the materials you need before opening your first app.”

Avoid closing only with:

- “Use this today.”
- “Take this with you.”
- “Remember teamwork.”
- “Bring your Growth Mindset.”
- “Be clear in your communication.”

These reminders can reinforce the theme, but they need a recognizable cue and an observable action to help students retrieve the lesson in the next context.
