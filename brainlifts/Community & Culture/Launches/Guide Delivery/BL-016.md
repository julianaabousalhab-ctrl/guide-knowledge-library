# Make the LifeSkill necessary by designing asymmetric roles

## Pattern
Across the strongest Limitless Launches, Guides used role design to make the LifeSkill unavoidable. Students were not simply told to communicate, listen, lead, or collaborate. Instead, the Guide created roles with different information, permissions, or responsibilities, so students had to practice the LifeSkill in order to succeed.

This brainlift is not about assigning jobs for classroom management. It is about designing a role gap that makes the target LifeSkill necessary.

## Evidence
In the Giving & Receiving Feedback puzzle Launch, the Guide created a clear information-and-action split between partners. One student could see the puzzle image and give directions; the other student could touch the pieces but could not use the picture directly.

The Guide established the roles:
> "One person... is going to be the teller and telling them what to do. And the other person... is going to be able to touch the puzzle pieces."

Then the Guide tightened the constraint:
> "Only the teller can come and look at the cover of the board... only the people who are the putters, the people who are putting the puzzle together, will be able to touch the puzzle pieces."

Because the teller had the visual information and the putter had the ability to act, feedback and clear communication became necessary. Students later identified the concrete communication move that helped:
> "with the... naming state and like telling to put it next to Kentucky or Iowa."

The strength of this Launch is that the Guide did not simply say, "Use feedback." The Guide designed roles where students could not complete the puzzle unless feedback was specific enough to guide action.

Source artifact: [Giving & Receiving Feedback Launch transcript.](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1bYgdVfvPDFNqcgHOmednJ_D043TiarnQ/view?usp=sharing)

In the Blindfold Maze / Lava Maze Launch, the Guide again created asymmetric roles. One student had to move through the maze without sight, while the other student had to guide them verbally.

The Guide explained the role split:
> "One of you will be the walker, and the other one will be the guide."

Then the Guide added the constraint that made communication necessary:
> "The walker is going to be blindfolded, walking through the maze. So the guides only have to have really good communication to help the walker get through the maze safely without touching the lava."

When the task broke down, the reflection showed why the role design mattered. The Guide anchored the failure in a specific communication moment:
> "There was a point where Olivia said, go there. And Emma said, well, she doesn't know what there is."

The Guide then contrasted vague guidance with specific guidance:
> "If we were more specific and she said, take two foot steps to the front with your foot a little to the left. Is that more descriptive than go there?"

The role design made communication visible. The walker could not succeed by guessing, and the guide could not succeed with vague language.

## Anti-Pattern
In the Thanksgiving Entrepreneurship Launch, the Guide created a role-play scenario in which the student acted as an entrepreneur and the Guide acted as the person with the problem. However, the role design did not create enough independent responsibility for the student because the Guide kept supplying criteria, reminders, and prompts throughout the work.

Before independent work, the Guide repeatedly reminded the student of the customer and constraints:
> "They really like music and animals."

> "Make sure they're really really going to love whatever you designed for them."

During the work period, the Guide continued adding prompts:
> "Do you have a theme for your product?"

> "Could you sell this in a store?"

> "Did you write how much it's going to cost or its name?"

During the presentation, the Guide continued supplying the customer checklist:
> "The dad wanted to know, is it for every one of them?"

> "How did you know my family was going to like it?"

> "How's it helpful?"

The issue is not that the Launch used a role-play. The issue is that the role-play did not withhold enough responsibility from the Guide. The student was designing, but the Guide kept carrying the customer criteria and evaluation prompts, so the role design did not make independent problem-solving as necessary as it could have.

## Reusable Coaching Move
Create a role gap.

Before the Launch, ask:
> "What can one student know, see, say, or do that another student cannot?"

Then design the activity so students need the target LifeSkill to bridge that gap.

Strong role gaps include:
- one student sees, another student builds
- one student speaks, another student moves
- one student has the clue, another student has the materials
- one student is blindfolded, another student guides
- one student leads, others execute
- one student judges with criteria, another performs

Avoid assigning roles that are only labels. "Leader," "helper," or "speaker" only matters if that role has a real constraint, responsibility, or information advantage.

If students can succeed without using the LifeSkill, the role design is too weak.
