# Escalate the Challenge in Short, Visible Rounds

## Pattern
Structure the Launch as a sequence of short rounds that students can clearly distinguish. Begin with a manageable version that lets students understand the task, then add one visible source of difficulty at a time, such as speed, precision, an additional constraint, competition, or student-selected risk.

Each round should have:

- a clear beginning and ending;
- one identifiable change from the previous round;
- a brief opportunity to reset or strategize; and
- a challenge level students can explain.

Visible escalation sustains energy because students repeatedly encounter a new target without having to learn an entirely new activity. It also allows the Guide to adjust difficulty based on what students demonstrate in the earlier round.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide previews a three-round progression*

In a movement challenge, the Guide makes the progression explicit before students begin. The first round is easy, the second increases the difficulty, and the third gives teams responsibility for setting a performance bid.

> “Every team in round one is going to have an easy challenge.”

> “In round two, that challenge will increase. It can become a little bit more difficult.”

> “And then in round three, here’s where it gets tricky. Your team will get to bid against the other team.”

Later, the Guide marks the transition from practice to a timed level:

> “That round we had a little bit of learning. This round will be a little bit more difficult. Now you must complete this path in seven seconds.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because each round has a visible purpose and a distinct increase in demand: learn the route, complete it under a time constraint, and then make a team-selected bid.

*2- Practice comes before speed*

In a team ball-passing challenge, the Guide protects the first round as practice and clearly previews the next level.

> “We’re going to do this first round as a practice to make sure we know what we’re doing.”

> “The second round gets tricky.”

> “This is just practice. You don’t have to go too fast.”

> “Remember, it’s more important to get it right than to go fast. We’ll do speed in the second round.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because students first learn the movement and language routine without speed pressure. The Guide then adds speed as the next visible challenge rather than placing every demand in the opening attempt.

## Anti-Pattern

*Running one long attempt without intermediate levels or checkpoints*

In a puzzle challenge, students work for one extended period and reach the end of the available time without finishing.

> “We’re going to have to try this again tomorrow because we did 15 minutes and we did not finish.”

> “Please put it back in the box.”

The activity gives students a long attempt, but the transcript does not show short rounds, intermediate targets, or visible level changes during those 15 minutes. As a result, students reach the time limit before receiving a structured opportunity to reset around a smaller target or experience progress through completed stages.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Learn–Level Up–Choose** progression:

1. **Learn:** Run a brief practice round with the fewest necessary constraints.
   - “Round 1: Learn the pattern. Accuracy matters more than speed.”
2. **Level up:** Add one source of difficulty.
   - “Round 2: Same pattern, now complete it in 20 seconds.”
3. **Choose:** Let students select or negotiate the final challenge.
   - “Round 3: Choose your time goal or add one constraint.”
4. **Reset between rounds:** Give students 20–30 seconds to identify what they will keep or change.
5. **Name the level:** Announce exactly what changed before restarting.

A useful planning test is:

> “Can students explain in one sentence how this round is harder than the last one?”

Keep rounds short enough that the Launch contains multiple complete cycles. Avoid placing speed, silence, perfect accuracy, complex roles, and competition into the first attempt at the same time. Add difficulty only after students understand the underlying task.
