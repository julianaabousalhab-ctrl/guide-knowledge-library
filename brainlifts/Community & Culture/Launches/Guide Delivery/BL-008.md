# Name the Exact Behavior or Strategy Students Should Repeat

## Pattern

When recognizing successful work, name the observable behavior or strategy that produced progress. Make the feedback precise enough that the student—and other students listening—could intentionally repeat it.

Effective recognition answers:

- **What did the student do?**
- **How did the approach help?**
- **What result did it produce or support?**

This is more useful than broad praise because it turns a successful moment into reusable information. The Guide does not only communicate approval; the Guide identifies the action worth carrying into the next task or attempt.

The behavior should be described without attaching a fixed label to the student. Name “you grouped one type of material before moving to the next,” rather than “you are naturally organized.”

## Evidence

*1- The Guide identifies the organization strategy behind efficient cleanup*

During a classroom-cleanup challenge, the Guide narrates how students are organizing their work rather than only saying that they are working hard.

> “Cleaning up one thing at a time but getting all of them.”

> “She’s getting all the pencils and now she’s working on glue sticks.”

> “He goes right to the next area.”

The Guide also notices an adaptive change:

> “I like that you changed your strategy so it didn’t hurt your knee.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the feedback names repeatable actions: complete one category, move to the next area, and adjust the method when the original approach is not working safely.

*2- The Guide connects restarting to a stronger foundation*

During a pencil-tower challenge, the Guide recognizes the specific improvement made after a restart.

> “I love how you started over and you have a really strong foundation now.”

> “Good job adjusting.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide identifies what made the second approach stronger: restarting and improving the foundation. The student receives information about which part of the adjustment is worth repeating.

## Anti-Pattern

*Offering broad praise without identifying what should be repeated*

At the end of a puzzle attempt, the Guide praises effort generally:

> “You guys did such a good job and I love how hard you worked.”

The statement communicates warmth and encouragement, but it does not identify what students did well. Students are left without a clear behavior or strategy to carry into the next attempt. Because the team did not finish, specificity would be especially useful: the Guide could name the communication move, resource use, perspective shift, or collaboration behavior that helped the team make progress.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Notice–Name–Connect** feedback structure:

1. **Notice:** Observe an action rather than relying on a general impression.
   - “You grouped every pencil before moving to the glue sticks.”
2. **Name:** Identify the behavior or strategy precisely.
   - “That is a category-by-category cleanup strategy.”
3. **Connect:** Explain the effect.
   - “It helped you avoid missing materials and made the cleanup faster.”
4. **Invite repetition:** Tell students when to use it again.
   - “Use that same strategy at the next station.”

A practical feedback sentence is:

> “I noticed you ___; that helped ___; repeat that when ___.”

Examples:

> “I noticed you widened the base before building higher; that made the structure more stable.”

> “I noticed you gave one direction at a time; that helped your partner act without guessing.”

> “I noticed you checked the clue before changing your answer; that kept your revision evidence-based.”

Avoid stopping at phrases such as “good job,” “awesome,” “you’re crushing it,” or “I love it.” These can add warmth, but follow them with the exact action that earned the recognition.
