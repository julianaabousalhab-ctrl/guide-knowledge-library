# Coach With Questions Before Offering Solutions

## Pattern

When a student becomes uncertain or encounters a problem, ask a question that surfaces the student’s thinking before providing a hint, direction, or solution.

The first question should return responsibility to the student. It might ask what the student needs, what they notice, what options they see, or what they could test. The Guide then listens and uses the response to decide whether the student needs more wait time, a narrower question, a small hint, or direct instruction.

The goal is not to withhold help indefinitely. The goal is to avoid solving the problem before the student has had a meaningful opportunity to reason. Effective questioning preserves student ownership while allowing the Guide to add support when the student cannot move forward independently.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide asks students to identify what they need*

During an independent setup challenge, the Guide does not provide students with a complete materials list. Instead, the Guide repeatedly asks them to determine what each work period requires.

> “And you can choose which one you want to set up first. What do you think you need?”

> “What do you need to grab for check chart? What do you think you need?”

> “What do you need for writing?”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide’s questions prompt students to retrieve the routine and make decisions before receiving an answer. The questions keep the cognitive work with the students while still directing their attention to the relevant task.

*2- The Guide asks students to select and test an adjustment*

After an unsuccessful balance attempt, the Guide asks students to reason about how changing their position could improve stability. When students remain unsure, the Guide invites them to test an option rather than immediately declaring the correct placement.

> “Do you need to take a step back or a step in do you think to do a better balance?”

> “I don’t know.”

> “Should we just try?”

> “Yeah.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide begins with a question, accepts uncertainty, and turns the uncertainty into an experiment. Students remain involved in selecting and testing the solution.

## Anti-Pattern

*Asking a question and then supplying the solution before the student reasons*

During a pencil-tower challenge, the Guide begins with an open question but quickly shifts into suggesting where a piece should be placed.

> “It’s okay, let’s fix it. How can we fix it?”

> “Hmm what about Should we put it up here?”

> “Okay, that works.”

The opening question could have elicited the student’s reasoning, but the transcript does not show the student proposing or evaluating an option before the Guide supplies one. The Guide’s solution may help the tower immediately, but it reduces the student’s opportunity to diagnose the problem and generate the next move.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use an **Ask–Wait–Probe–Support** sequence:

1. **Ask:** Start with one question that returns the problem to the student.
   - “What do you notice?”
   - “What do you think you need?”
   - “What could you try?”
2. **Wait:** Allow several seconds for observation and thinking. Do not fill the silence immediately.
3. **Probe:** If the answer is vague, narrow the question without giving away the solution.
   - “Which part is making it unstable?”
   - “What are your two options?”
   - “What happened when you tried that?”
4. **Test:** Let the student choose and try a next move.
   - “Test your idea and watch what changes.”
5. **Support:** Offer the smallest useful hint only when the student remains stuck.
   - “Look at the base.”
   - “Compare these two positions.”
6. **Teach directly when needed:** If the student lacks prerequisite knowledge, explain it clearly rather than continuing an unproductive guessing sequence.

Before offering a solution, use this check:

> “Have I heard the student’s current idea, evidence, or uncertainty?”

Avoid asking a question when the answer has already been decided and the student is only expected to agree. A genuine coaching question leaves room for the student to think, choose, and test.
