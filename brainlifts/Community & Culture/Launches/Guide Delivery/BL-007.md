# Model the Minimum, Then Release Students to Practice

## Pattern

Demonstrate only the unfamiliar process, rule, or quality students need in order to begin. Keep the model brief, make the thinking visible, and then release students to practice while the Guide observes and coaches.

The purpose of modeling is not to complete the task for students or explain every possible success criterion in advance. A useful model gives students enough information to start productively while leaving the meaningful decisions, mistakes, and adjustments to them.

The minimum model usually includes:

- one representative example;
- the key thinking or decision behind the example;
- the constraint students are most likely to misunderstand; and
- a clear signal to begin practice.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide works through one example, then releases students to apply the process*

In a process-of-elimination Launch, the Guide first explains the strategy using a familiar multiple-choice example:

> “I don’t know the right answer, but I know it can’t be A and it can’t be B and it also can’t be D. So, what leaves the right answer?”

> “C. And that is process of elimination.”

The Guide then works through one riddle with students, eliminating the incorrect choices before naming the remaining answer:

> “So after we have eliminated both moon, star, and cloud, what does that leave us with?”

> “The sun.”

After that example, the Guide releases students to use the same process on a worksheet:

> “Your job when we go through the riddles is to first X out the wrong answer with the red colored pencil.”

> “Then lastly, you will check off the right answer with the green colored pencil.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide models one complete reasoning cycle, clarifies the procedure, and then moves students into practice rather than continuing to demonstrate multiple riddles for them.

*2- The Guide demonstrates one small section, then gives students practice time*

During a chime activity, the Guide previews the available cues and plays only the first row:

> “Let me play through it one time.”

> “Do you see how it says four and then five? So, you can either do the numbers or the colors.”

> “So, that’s the first row.”

The Guide then releases students promptly:

> “Go ahead and practice for three minutes.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the model is limited to the information needed to begin: how to read the cues and what one row sounds like. Students retain responsibility for practicing the rest of the sequence.

## Anti-Pattern

*Front-loading multiple definitions and success criteria before students practice*

Before a pen-commercial task, the Guide verbally defines persuasion and audience engagement:

> “Persuasion is the ability to get somebody to believe something that you believe or to buy something that you’re selling.”

> “Audience engagement means you’re not boring your audience. You’re keeping them engaged.”

The Guide then introduces several additional performance features before students begin:

> “I want to encourage you guys to think about using a creative voice. Um gestures might need to have a slogan.”

The information is relevant to the task, but several concepts and criteria are introduced verbally before students have tried the commercial. This increases the amount students must hold in mind at once and delays the point at which the Guide can observe what support they actually need. A shorter model of one opening line or one persuasive move would allow practice to begin sooner and make later coaching more responsive.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Show One–Name One–Release** sequence:

1. **Show one:** Demonstrate one representative part of the task.
   - One riddle, one row, one opening line, or one turn.
2. **Name one:** State the most important thinking move or constraint.
   - “I removed the answers that contradicted the clue.”
   - “I followed the numbers from left to right.”
3. **Check readiness:** Ask one question that confirms students know how to start.
   - “What will you do first?”
4. **Release:** Begin student practice before adding secondary criteria.
   - “Start. I’ll coach as I observe.”
5. **Add support responsively:** Introduce the next criterion only when the practice reveals a need.
   - “Now that your message is clear, let’s work on audience engagement.”

Before extending a demonstration, ask:

> “Do students need more information to begin, or do I need to see them try?”

Stop modeling once students can safely and meaningfully start. Preserve the parts of the task that require judgment, experimentation, or problem-solving for the students.
