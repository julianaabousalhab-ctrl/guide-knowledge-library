# Anchor reflection in specific moments — not abstract LifeSkills

## Pattern
Across the strongest Launches, Guides anchored reflection in specific moments from the activity, helping students analyze concrete experiences instead of discussing abstract LifeSkills.

## Evidence
In the Rainbow Drawing Challenge, rather than asking students whether communication is important, the Guide directed them back to a specific event that had just occurred during the activity. The Guide asked:
> "What made the second round harder?"

Students immediately referred to the experience they had just lived:
> "We couldn't like talk, so we couldn't share like how we think we could make it better."

Another student offered a contrasting observation from the same round:
> "This group struggled immensely when they were able to speak... But when they couldn't speak, they seemed to have a better team dynamic."

Instead of discussing communication in the abstract, the Guide grounded reflection in the silent second round, asking students to analyze what actually happened.

Source artifact: [Rainbow Drawing Challenge (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1_M6psUaQCIvxErm5qIpS76ZtUHi0r2gw/view?usp=sharing)

In the Blindfold Maze / Lava Maze Launch, the Guide did not simply ask whether communication matters. The Guide replayed a concrete moment from the activity when one student gave an unclear direction.

The Guide first asked:
> "What made it hard to give direction? My guys, what was hard about giving direction?"

A student explained the problem with imprecise communication:
> "People didn't know how much... like, how big?"

The Guide then anchored the reflection in a specific moment from the maze:
> "There was a point where Olivia said, 'go there.' And Emma said, 'well, she doesn't know what there is.' And you were like, where's there?"

The Guide then contrasted that vague direction with a concrete alternative:
> "If we were more specific and she said, 'take two foot steps to the front with your foot a little to the left,' is that more descriptive than go there?"

This is a strong example because the reflection stayed attached to an exact moment students had just experienced. The Guide used that moment to help students analyze why specificity mattered.

Source artifact: [Blindfold Maze / Lava Maze Launch — meeting transcript (Read.ai)](https://app.read.ai/analytics/meetings/01KC753AHJQ9MWNT380ZSG8QDT/transcript)

## Anti-Pattern
In the Impossible Jumps challenge, the Guide shifted quickly to discussing the life skill without first unpacking specific moments from the activity. Following the jumping activity, the Guide immediately moved to the abstract lesson:
> "Every single human being jumped further."

followed by:
> "What does that tell us?"

The discussion quickly became about the concept of growth and improvement rather than examining how students changed their approach between jumps.

A stronger reflection would have asked:
> “What changed between your first and second jump?”

> “What did you do differently?”

> “What did you notice in your body or strategy?”

Source artifact: [Impossible Jumps Challenge (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1Uf1AtN9jcPJLO2nrh6X5H0iuCXoHlXRx/view?usp=sharing)

## Reusable Coaching Move
1. **Replay a specific event**
   - "Let's go back to when..."
   - "Think about the moment when..."
   - "Remember when..."
2. **Analyze that event**
   - "What happened?"
   - "Why did that happen?"
   - "What changed?"
   - "What did you notice?"
3. **Only then extract the principle**
   - "What does that tell us?"
   - "What can we learn from that?"
  
Do not ask the abstract LifeSkill question until students have replayed at least one specific moment from the activity.

This brainlift is about the object of reflection: a specific moment.
