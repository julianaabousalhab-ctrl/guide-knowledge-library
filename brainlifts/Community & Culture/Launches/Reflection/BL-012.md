# Use students' observations to allow a valuable reflection

## Pattern
Across the strongest Launches, Guides built reflection from students' own observations, using their comments to shape the discussion rather than relying on preplanned talking points.

## Evidence
In the House of Cards Launch, the Guide allowed a student's observation about complaining to become the central reflection instead of returning to a prepared lesson.

Student:
> "When the tower fell, we all started complaining... but then finally we got back up."

Rather than moving on, the Guide built directly from that comment:
> "That brings up a really good point. Do you guys hear what Samantha said? She said the team started complaining... It's okay to acknowledge the fact that something kind of sucks... but getting back focused and back on task — that's the most important lesson of what today was."

The direction of the reflection emerged from Samantha's observation, not from a predetermined talking point.

Source artifact: [House of Cards Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1dY1kBuN2iQZF6CN6e-tYMlpJS21YtuDz/view?usp=sharing)

In the Listening is Worth a Thousand Mistakes Launch, a student's comment about everyone making mistakes became the foundation for the remainder of the discussion.

Student:
> "We all make mistakes."

Instead of introducing a new reflection topic, the Guide expanded the student's idea:
> "So to Mr. Keaton's point, we all make mistakes. If you're a guide, or a dean of parents, or a campus coordinator, and you've ever made a mistake in your job, please raise your hand."

The Guide then continued building on that same observation before asking:
> "Can we take this into our day?"

The reflection was driven by the student's insight, rather than by a scripted explanation.

## Anti-Pattern
In the Self-Love Launch, the Guide relied primarily on predetermined prompts instead of allowing students' observations to shape the reflection. Throughout the reflection, the Guide followed a planned sequence of questions that stayed focused on the intended message regardless of students' responses.

After asking:
> "Which one of those was more difficult?"

and hearing students' answers, the Guide quickly transitioned back to the planned lesson:
> "Today you are actually going to focus on Valentine's Day being about love... sharing love with others but also sharing love with yourself."

Later, the Guide continued with prepared prompts:
> "How can this self-love Valentine help you throughout your Alpha day?"

Rather than extending students' observations into new lines of discussion, the Guide consistently redirected the conversation toward the preplanned self-love activity, making the reflection more Guide-directed than student-generated.

## Reusable Coaching Move
Instead of returning to your planned talking points, take a student's observation and make it the next discussion prompt.

Three-step formula:

1. **Name the student's idea**
   - "I want to go back to what Samantha just said..."
   - "Did everyone hear what Keaton noticed?"
   - "That's interesting. Let's stay with that."
2. **Elevate it to the group**
   - "Does anyone else agree?"
   - "Who saw something similar?"
   - "What do the rest of you think?"
3. **Extract the principle together**
   - "Why do you think that happened?"
   - "What does that tell us about teamwork?"
   - "What can we learn from that?"

The Guide doesn't introduce a new topic — the students' observation becomes the topic.
