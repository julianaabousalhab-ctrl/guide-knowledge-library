# Debrief From Evidence, to Cause, to Next Step

## Pattern

Sequence the reflection so students first identify what happened, then explain what caused the result, and finally decide what they will keep or change in the next relevant attempt.

A strong debrief follows three connected moves:

1. **Evidence — What happened?**
   - Students point to a specific moment, action, obstacle, result, or difference they observed.
2. **Cause — Why did it happen?**
   - Students identify the behavior, strategy, decision, communication move, or condition that contributed to the result.
3. **Next step — What will you do with that learning?**
   - Students name what they will repeat, change, or test and when they will use it.

The Guide should resist beginning with an abstract question such as “What did we learn?” Students often answer with a theme word—teamwork, grit, listening—without examining the experience that gives the word meaning.

The debrief does not need to be long. It needs to preserve the reasoning chain:

> “This happened. It happened because of this. Therefore, next time I will do this.”

## Evidence

*1- Students identify the difficulty, explain how they worked through it, and connect it to an independent action*

After students independently prepare their learning spaces, the Guide begins with the experience:

> “What was tricky about getting ready today and how did you figure that out?”

A student identifies the specific difficulty:

> “Getting everything ready is tricky to remember everything.”

The Guide then probes the strategy behind the result:

> “Did you just take time to think of each station?”

With another student, the Guide asks:

> “And how did you work through it?”

> “How did you figure it out to remember everything?”

The student identifies the source used to make the decision:

> “Figure out what I got yesterday.”

The Guide makes the causal connection explicit:

> “So you looked at the past and went, oh, This is what I needed yesterday, so this is what I need today.”

The reflection then moves toward a repeatable independent action:

> “Why is it important to take care of your stuff, [student]?”

> “When I drop all of my books, I just clean it up by myself.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the debrief begins with an experienced obstacle, identifies the strategy students used to overcome it, and ends with an action that can be repeated when the same responsibility appears again.

*2- The Guide uses the first attempt and visible tower results to identify strategies and prepare for the next challenge*

After a pencil-tower challenge, the Guide directs attention to a specific event:

> “I noticed that you wound up having to start over.”

> “So what did you learn from the first time you did it?”

The Guide then asks for the strategy behind the adjustment:

> “Was there a specific strategy that you changed?”

The Guide summarizes one causal strategy:

> “So [student] was using his affirmations to get him through.”

The Guide also asks students to compare visible evidence in the completed towers:

> “See how your pencils are much closer together?”

> “And [another student’s] is more spaced out.”

> “So based on looking at these, which one do you all feel like is a better strategy?”

The Guide connects the structure to its result:

> “I would probably agree that the tighter one, because that one was able to go the highest, right?”

The debrief closes with a next-step commitment for a future difficult moment:

> “For everyone to share one thing they’re gonna say to themselves when something is hard today.”

> “When things get harder, I try harder.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because students reflect from concrete evidence—restarting and differences between the towers—to strategies that affected persistence and stability, then choose language to use during the next hard moment.

## Anti-Pattern

*Moving from a theme label directly to a general reminder*

After a Simon Says activity, the Guide asks:

> “What was the purpose of this?”

A student responds:

> “Listen.”

The reflection later returns to another broad prompt:

> “What did we learn about this activity?”

A student names the Launch phrase:

> “Listening is worth a thousand mistakes.”

The Guide then asks:

> “So, how can we take this into our day?”

A student again responds:

> “Listen.”

The debrief reaches the correct theme and begins to connect it to the day, but it does not first examine a specific moment from the activity, what caused a student to miss or follow a direction, or which listening strategy changed the result. Without that causal middle step, “listen” remains a broad instruction rather than learning students can deliberately reproduce.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use an **Evidence–Cause–Next Step** sequence:

1. **Evidence:** Anchor students in a specific moment.
   - “What happened?”
   - “Where did the task become difficult?”
   - “What did you see, hear, or notice?”
   - “What changed between the first and second attempt?”
2. **Cause:** Ask students to explain the result.
   - “What did you or your team do that contributed to that?”
   - “Why did that strategy help?”
   - “What caused the problem?”
   - “Which condition made the task easier or harder?”
3. **Next step:** Convert the explanation into a decision.
   - “What will you repeat?”
   - “What will you change?”
   - “When will you use that strategy again?”
   - “What will we look for in the next attempt?”

A concise debrief script is:

> “What happened? What caused that? Based on that evidence, what will you do next?”

When students answer with a theme word, return them to the experience:

> Student: “Teamwork.”

> Guide: “What did someone actually do that made the teamwork effective?”

Then press for the causal connection:

> “How did that action change the result?”

Finally, require a decision:

> “What will your team repeat in the next round?”

Avoid accepting:

- “We learned grit.”
- “Communication.”
- “Listen.”
- “Try harder.”
- “Work together.”

These may be accurate themes, but they are not yet a complete reflection. Ask for the moment that demonstrates the theme, why it affected the outcome, and the next behavior students will use.
