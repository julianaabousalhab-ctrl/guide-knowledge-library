# Guides deepen reflection by probing beyond students' first answers — rather than accepting surface-level responses

## Pattern
Across the strongest Limitless Launches, Guides rarely accepted students' first answers. Instead, they consistently followed up with probing questions that required students to explain, elaborate, or clarify their thinking.

## Evidence
In the Listening is Worth a Thousand Mistakes Launch, the Guide refused to leave the response at "listening." Instead, they unpacked and clarified the underlying thinking, pushing students toward a more precise understanding. A student initially gave a surface-level response:
> Student: "Listening carefully."

Rather than accepting it, the Guide deepened the idea:
> Guide: "Following directions. I love that. That is not just listening. It is actively implementing the feedback, right? Which, believe it or not, is a life skill — giving and receiving feedback."

Source artifact: [Listening is Worth a Thousand Mistakes Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/14eTjCnch_UNh5LFKc0Ql5NYmSJHf8h6M/view?usp=drive_link)

In the Entrepreneur Launch, rather than accepting the student's first explanation, the Guide asked successive questions requiring the student to justify their reasoning and explain the evidence behind their design decisions. The student initially explained that the product would make people happy. Instead of accepting the answer, the Guide repeatedly probed for evidence:
> Guide: "How did you know my family was going to like it? Because you never even met them."

After the student answered, the Guide continued:
> Guide: "I know some of it's fun, but an entrepreneur, they also think about how is it helpful and not just fun because a lot of things are fun. So, how's it helpful?"

Source artifact: [Entrepreneur Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1PPoU-z9KNanyh-N9d-h9z6AWdhtBbnmZ/view?usp=drive_link)

## Anti-Pattern
In the Tackle It Tuesday Launch, the Guide accepted students' initial answers with little or no follow-up to explore their reasoning. During the reflection, the Guide primarily asked students to identify what they cleaned independently, but generally accepted their first responses without probing the thinking behind them.

Guide:
> "What did you fix or clean without anybody asking you?"

Student:
> "The puzzles."

Guide:
> "I love that you went right to the puzzles this time."

Similarly:
> "Pencils."

Guide:
> "And why did you choose pencils?"

Student:
> "Because I threw a lot of pencils out."

The Guide simply acknowledged the response and moved on.

Source artifact: [Tackle It Tuesday Launch — meeting transcript (Read.ai)](https://app.read.ai/analytics/meetings/01K3KDR66E2K4QBAGJMGY3MCPD?utm_source=Share_CopyLink)

## Reusable Coaching Move
Instead of accepting the student's first answer (or immediately praising it), the Guide asks one follow-up question that requires the student to make their thinking visible.

Formula: Student gives an answer → Guide probes → Student elaborates

The probe should ask the student to:
- **Explain** — "Can you tell me more?"
- **Justify** — "Why did you choose that?"
- **Clarify** — "What do you mean by that?"
- **Provide evidence** — "What happened that made you think that?"
- **Compare** — "Why did that work better than your first idea?"
