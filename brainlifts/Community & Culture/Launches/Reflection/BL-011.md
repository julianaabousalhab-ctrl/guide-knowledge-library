# Guides deepen reflection by probing beyond students' first answers — rather than accepting surface-level responses

## Pattern
Across the strongest Limitless Launches, Guides rarely accepted students' first answers. Instead, they consistently followed up with probing questions that required students to explain, elaborate, or clarify their thinking. 
A true probe makes the student talk more, not the Guide. The purpose of the probe is not to get a more polished answer; it is to make the student's thinking visible.

## Evidence
In the Feedback / Kind and Helpful Launch, the Guide did not accept students' first rating of the feedback example. When students classified the feedback as not kind, the Guide pushed them to explain the reason behind their judgment rather than simply confirming the correct category.

The Guide asked:
> "If I said, nice try, maybe take smaller steps next time, is that kind? Is that kind? You say it's not kind? What do you guys think? Is it kind? Is it helpful? ... Liam, why did you pick? Why is this feedback not kind?"

When the student could not fully explain, the Guide continued probing for the specific reasoning:
> "What part of the feedback felt not nice?"

Then the Guide narrowed the probe even further:
> "Which part felt not nice? Was it the nice try? That felt not nice, to say nice try?"

This is a strong example because the Guide used follow-up questions to uncover the student's reasoning instead of accepting the rating at face value or immediately giving the answer.
Source artifact: [Feedback / Kind and Helpful Launch](https://app.read.ai/analytics/meetings/01K8R8D4PR1YGT9X4VXNDF074Q?utm_source=Share_CopyLink)

In the Entrepreneur Launch, rather than accepting the student's first explanation, the Guide asked successive questions requiring the student to justify their reasoning and explain the evidence behind their design decisions. The student initially explained that the product would make people happy. Instead of accepting the answer, the Guide repeatedly probed for evidence:
> Guide: "How did you know my family was going to like it? Because you never even met them."

After the student answered, the Guide continued probing beyond "fun" by asking whether the product solved the problem:
> Guide: "I know some of it's fun, but an entrepreneur, they also think about how is it helpful and not just fun because a lot of things are fun. So, how's it helpful?"

This reflection became stronger because the Guide required the student to explain the reasoning behind the design instead of accepting the finished product or the first answer.

## Anti-Pattern
In the Tackle It Tuesday Launch, the Guide accepted students' initial answers with little or no follow-up to explore their reasoning. During the reflection, the Guide primarily asked students to identify what they cleaned independently, but generally accepted their first responses without probing the thinking behind them. The Guide asks one “why” question, but does not continue until the student explains the reasoning behind the choice.
 
Guide:
> "What did you fix or clean without anybody asking you?"

Student:
> "The puzzles."

Guide:
> "I love that you went right to the puzzles this time."

Similarly:
> "Pencils."

Guide:
> "And why did you choose pencils?"

Student:
> "Because I threw a lot of pencils out."

The Guide simply acknowledged the response, but did not continue probing until the student explained the reasoning behind the choice, the strategy used, or how the student decided what mattered most.

The missed opportunity was not that the Guide failed to ask any questions. The missed opportunity was that the Guide treated brief answers as sufficient evidence and moved on before students had to make their thinking visible.

## Reusable Coaching Move
Ask one probe before praising or explaining.

Formula:
Student answer → Guide probe → Student elaboration → Guide names or connects the principle.

The Guide should not explain the student’s thinking until the student has first tried to explain it.

This brainlift is about the Guide’s questioning move during reflection.
