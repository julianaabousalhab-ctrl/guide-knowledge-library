# Compare Approaches and Extract a Reusable Strategy

## Pattern

Place two or more student approaches side by side, ask students to compare the evidence from each one, and then name a strategy that can be reused in a future attempt or different task.

The purpose is not simply to identify a winner. Comparison should help students understand:

- what each approach did differently;
- what result each approach produced;
- why one approach worked better for a particular goal or condition;
- what trade-offs each approach created; and
- what principle students can carry forward.

A reusable strategy is broader than the materials in the Launch. “Put these pencils closer together” solves one pencil-tower problem. “Adjust the spacing based on whether the goal is height or stability” is a principle students can test in other building challenges.

The Guide should preserve evidence from the task—structures, times, attempts, student observations, or visible results—so students can compare actual approaches rather than relying on memory or preference alone.

## Evidence

*1- Students compare two tower structures and connect each design to its result*

After the pencil-tower challenge, the Guide places two structures in direct comparison:

> “See how your pencils are much closer together?”

> “And [another student’s] is more spaced out.”

The Guide asks students to evaluate the approaches:

> “So based on looking at these, which one do you all feel like is a better strategy?”

The discussion connects design features to outcomes:

> “He has a bigger space, so you feel like it looks sturdier?”

> “I would probably agree that the tighter one, because that one was able to go the highest, right?”

> “But I also like how [the other student’s] is super, super sturdy.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide does not examine one tower in isolation. Students compare tight and wide spacing against two different outcomes—height and stability. The comparison creates a reusable design principle: choose and adjust the base structure according to the result the team is trying to produce.

*2- Students study another team’s method and extract construction and collaboration strategies*

During a house-of-cards debrief, the Guide explicitly asks students to examine a different team’s approach:

> “I saw some groups looking at other groups which was definitely part of the strategy.”

> “What did you see that they were doing over here?”

A student identifies the structural difference and explains its effect:

> “They were like bending the cards like so that it wouldn’t be like just flat cuz like flat it’s easier to fall over.”

The Guide extracts a broader strategy from the comparison:

> “So you use your contacts, you use your clues around you in order to do a better job with your team.”

> “And I saw you communicate that with your team.”

Later, a student compares separate individual trials with a more coordinated approach:

> “We all got cards and then we all tried to like make something to see if it would work. But maybe all together, maybe it would be a little—”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because students compare flat and bent-card structures, as well as parallel individual attempts and coordinated teamwork. The debrief extracts two strategies that can transfer: study useful approaches already visible in the environment, and communicate the promising strategy so the whole team can use it.

## Anti-Pattern

*Ending with the result without comparing how students approached the challenge*

During a multi-round speed-ladder challenge, students produce several visible data points, including times, mistake totals, team bids, and different attempts. The final round concludes with the result:

> “In 10 seconds and three mistakes.”

> “Congratulations, Mr. [student]. Congratulations everyone let’s go inside. High fives fellas.”

The Guide then postpones the discussion:

> “We’ll look through what we got over there and we’ll talk about it later.”

The activity generates rich comparison evidence, but the transcript ends before students examine which pacing, stopping, movement, or bidding approaches produced different results. Celebration is appropriate, but without comparing approaches, students leave with a score rather than a strategy they can deliberately repeat.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Compare–Explain–Extract–Test** sequence:

1. **Compare:** Select two approaches with a meaningful difference.
   - “This team bent the cards; this team kept them flat.”
   - “This base is narrow; this base is wide.”
2. **Name the evidence:** Ask students to describe the results without judging yet.
   - “Which structure reached higher?”
   - “Which one stayed standing longer?”
   - “What happened to the time and mistake count?”
3. **Explain:** Connect the differing action to the differing result.
   - “Why might bending the cards have increased stability?”
   - “How did working separately affect the team?”
4. **Extract:** State a principle that applies beyond the original materials.
   - “Change the structure when the current design cannot support the goal.”
   - “Observe effective approaches, then communicate them to the team.”
5. **Test:** Ask when and how students will use the strategy again.
   - “Where else could this principle help?”
   - “What will you test in the next round?”

A concise Guide script is:

> “Approach A did ___. Approach B did ___. What result did each produce? Why? What strategy should we carry forward?”

Useful comparison dimensions include:

- speed versus accuracy;
- height versus stability;
- individual work versus coordinated work;
- repeating the same method versus changing a variable;
- broad directions versus precise directions; and
- immediate action versus pausing to plan.

Avoid asking only:

- “Who won?”
- “Which one do you like?”
- “Did everyone have fun?”
- “What was your score?”

Preference and outcome can begin the discussion, but they do not complete it. Require students to explain the difference between approaches and turn that difference into a principle they can use again.
