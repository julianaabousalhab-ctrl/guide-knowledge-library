# Give the Challenge a Story, Role, Problem, or Audience

## Pattern

Frame the activity as a meaningful situation in which students have a role, a problem to solve, or an audience that needs the result. The frame should give students a reason to act beyond following the Guide’s directions or completing the materials.

A strong frame answers at least two of these questions:

- **Who are the students in this challenge?**
- **What has happened or what problem must be solved?**
- **Who needs the students’ work?**
- **What meaningful outcome are they trying to produce?**

The story or audience does not need to be elaborate. It should be brief, understandable, and directly connected to the skill students will practice. The frame creates energy because students enter a situation; the task is the action they take inside it.

## Evidence

*1- Students become a stranded crew with a survival problem*

The Guide introduces a team-planning challenge by placing students in a fictional emergency. Students are not merely told to select a leader and make a plan; they take on the role of a crew that must organize to survive.

> “Today I have some excellent news and an unfortunate situation you find yourselves in.”

> “You and your crew’s boat has crashed ashore and you’ve been split up into two groups.”

> “You quickly realize this is not an ordinary island.”

> “It is filled with dinosaurs.”

> “In order to survive, you must elect a leader. You must come up with a plan.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the story supplies a role, stakes, and a problem. Electing a leader and planning become necessary actions within the scenario rather than isolated instructions.

*2- A recurring character creates a problem and a real audience*

The Guide frames a classroom-cleanup task around a character who does not yet know how to act independently. Students are asked to demonstrate the skill for that character by creating a recording.

> “Disastro, oh no, Disastro, he visited again.”

> “He doesn’t know how to clean up after himself. He’s not independent. He just makes messes.”

> “Should we record you guys cleaning it up so I can show him, hey, Disastro, this is how you clean up your messes.”

> “All right, you ready to show how independent you are?”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the task includes both a problem and an audience. Students are restoring the room, but they are also producing a demonstration intended to teach someone else what independence looks like.

## Anti-Pattern

*Beginning with an extended abstract explanation before students have a situation to enter*

In an integrity Launch, the Guide explains the concept through general statements about future behavior and personal standards, then asks students whether they agree.

> “Integrity is holding that high bar for yourself and no one else.”

> “The rest of your life, you’re not going to have guides. You’re not going to have parents. You’re not gonna have even peers a lot of the time for you guys to hold yourself to a higher standard, to a higher bar.”

> “Would you guys agree with that?”

The explanation may communicate the importance of integrity, but students are initially positioned as listeners rather than actors. There is no immediate role, concrete problem, or audience requiring them to use the skill. This can reduce energy because students must absorb the concept before they have an experience that makes it necessary.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Role–Problem–Purpose** opening:

1. **Role:** Tell students who they are in the activity.
   - “Today, you are a design team.”
2. **Problem:** State what has happened or what needs to be solved.
   - “A younger studio cannot follow the current directions.”
3. **Purpose or audience:** Name who needs the outcome and why.
   - “You will create a clearer version they can use independently.”
4. **Challenge:** Give the task and constraints.
   - “You have eight minutes and may use only these materials.”
5. **Launch:** Start before overexplaining the lesson.
   - Let the experience create the need for the skill, then name the learning during reflection.

A concise framing script is:

> “You are ___. Your challenge is ___. This matters because ___ needs your solution.”

Keep the frame connected to the real learning goal. Avoid decorative stories that add excitement but do not change why students must collaborate, communicate, persist, or think. The role or audience should make the target skill necessary.
