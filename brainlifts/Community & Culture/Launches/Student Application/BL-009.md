# Student application is demonstrated through transfer — not repetition

## Pattern
Across the strongest Limitless Launches, Guides designed activities that required students to adapt their thinking as conditions changed. Whether confronted with failure, new constraints, or new information, students demonstrated learning by revising their strategies rather than repeating the same behavior. Transfer requires a changed condition. If the conditions stay the same, students may simply be repeating a procedure.

## Evidence
In the Rainbow Drawing challenge, the Guide deliberately increased the cognitive demand by introducing a new constraint: students could no longer talk. Rather than continuing with the same approach, students immediately adjusted how they collaborated. After Round 1, the Guide changed the rules:
> "The second round... you cannot talk during the second round."

Students didn't simply persist — they changed how they communicated once the environment changed.

Guide:
> "Why do you think an activity like this would be helpful? Like what kind of life skills can we take away from this?"

Student:
> "Teamwork."

The stronger evidence of transfer is not that students named “teamwork.” The stronger evidence is that students had to adapt their collaboration when the Guide removed verbal communication. The changed condition forced students to apply communication and teamwork differently, rather than simply repeating the same behavior from Round 1.

Source artifact: [Rainbow Drawing Challenge (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1_M6psUaQCIvxErm5qIpS76ZtUHi0r2gw/view?usp=sharing)

In the Perseverance, Perspective, Teamwork Launch, students did not stick to their initial ideas. Instead, they continuously incorporated new evidence into their thinking. Examples:
> "Wait a minute..."

> "There's nothing in there!"

After discovering that one clue didn't work, they immediately searched elsewhere rather than continuing the same approach. Later:
> "Guys, is anyone paying attention to the numbers?"

Students shifted from randomly searching to recognizing that the numbers themselves might be meaningful, changing the direction of their investigation.

## Anti-Pattern
In the Self-Love Launch, the issue was not that students failed to participate. The issue was that the task never introduced a new condition that required students to revise or transfer their thinking. The Guide simply moves students through the task:
> "You are going to make the longest self-love link chain."

> "Write what you love about yourself."

Later:
> "Your ticket into core skills is to make your own self-love Valentine."

During work time, coaching remains procedural:
> "Think about those choices..."

> "You have about two minutes."

Students continue with essentially the same task until completion.

Source artifact: [Self-Love Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1H5B3E3KGFIj2hYnLeZs6orUlXDyo16zw/view?usp=sharing)

## Reusable Coaching Move
After the first attempt, change one meaningful condition and ask students to apply the same principle again.

Examples:
- remove talking
- change teams
- add new information
- limit materials
- shorten the time
- switch roles
- require a second attempt with a different strategy.

Then ask:
> “What changed in your approach, and why?”

This brainlift is about in-task transfer, not end-of-launch life application.
