# Guides deepen application by removing one support students would normally expect

## Pattern
Across the strongest Limitless Launches, Guides intentionally removed or withheld one support students might normally expect — such as examples, hints, reference materials, detailed directions, or solutions — so students had to apply what they already knew.

## Evidence
In the Escape Room Launch, the Guide deliberately withheld the solution to the mystery. Rather than explaining the clues or directing students where to search, the Guide simply established the challenge and let students investigate.
> Guide: "You are two rival detective agencies who have been brought in to see who can solve the mystery first."

When students asked:
> "Underground?"

The Guide only clarified the boundary:
> "All the clues are in this room."

Students were left to interpret the clues themselves, leading to moments such as:
> "Guys, is anyone paying attention to the numbers?"

> "It does something to do with Alpha!"

The Guide intentionally withheld the solution so students had to construct it through discovery.

Source artifact: [Escape Room Launch (Google Photos)](https://photos.app.goo.gl/YRazQena9JkpxQam8)

In Tackle It Tuesday (Independence), the Guide removed a support students had relied on previously and required them to solve the task independently. Rather than providing the classroom reference picture used in an earlier Launch, the Guide announced:
> "You don't have a picture like you had last time."

> "And you don't have any stations."

Students were forced to remember where materials belonged and make independent decisions about organizing the room without additional guidance.

## Anti-Pattern
In the Thanksgiving Launch, the guide continually supplied new hints instead of withholding information.Rather than allowing the student to make design decisions independently, the guide kept introducing additional criteria:
> "They really like music and animals."

Later adding another consideration:
> "Make sure they're really going to love whatever you design."

The guide continued enriching the problem instead of letting the student infer what would make a good product.
Source artifact: [Thanksgiving Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1PPoU-z9KNanyh-N9d-h9z6AWdhtBbnmZ/view?usp=drive_link)

## Reusable Coaching Move
Before introducing a new challenge, ask yourself:
> "What support can I remove without making success impossible?"

Remove one scaffold at a time — examples, reference materials, hints, or detailed directions — so students must apply what they already know rather than wait for the Guide to provide the answer.

Do not remove clarity, safety, or the purpose of the task. Remove support that would give away the thinking.

This brainlift focuses specifically on transfer created by removing scaffolds.
