# Ask for a Specific Personal Context and Action

## Pattern

Ask each student to identify a real situation from their own life in which the Launch skill will matter and name the action they will take in that situation.

A strong application prompt moves beyond whether students *could* use the skill. It asks them to decide:

- **Where or when will this happen?**
- **What challenge, cue, or person will be involved?**
- **What exactly will you do or say?**

The response should be specific enough that the action could later be observed or revisited. “I will use independence at home” is incomplete. “After I finish drawing at home, I will put the pencils and paper back without being asked” contains both a context and an action.

This pattern creates a personal transfer plan. It is distinct from immediately embedding the skill in the next task: here, students first articulate where and how they will apply it.

## Evidence

*1- Students name what they will do independently at home*

After a classroom-cleanup challenge, the Guide shifts from the shared activity to students’ lives outside the studio:

> “All right, what’s something you can fix independently at home? And I want new answers.”

> “Think about all the things you did this morning that are independent. How can you do that at home?”

A student gives a specific context and action:

> “When I finish drawing, I clean it up, and I put it where it was before.”

The Guide continues pressing for observable actions:

> “What can you do at home?”

> “When I drop all of my toys, I need to clean up all of my toys.”

The Guide then increases the application demand:

> “What can you do at home that might not be your mess?”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide names a personal setting—home—and asks students to generate actions they can perform there. Students respond with concrete behaviors tied to drawing materials, toys, beds, and helping family members.

*2- Students connect a personal success to a specific moment during the mile*

The Guide asks students to create an encouragement sign that they will use during a real challenge later that day:

> “During your mile today, you are going to make a sign for yourself that we can hold up and encourage you.”

> “On your sign today, you are going to write your remember when.”

> “I want you to remember when you did something that made you feel great or accomplished or proud.”

When a student chooses a soccer success, the Guide helps connect it to an action during the mile:

> “So when you’re running, can you think about similar things or is it different?”

> “So what could you think about that can push you when you’re running that would be similar to when you won best soccer goal?”

The student identifies a concrete cue:

> “When I look at the finish line.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the application is personal and situated. The student identifies a past accomplishment and decides what to think about when a specific cue—the finish line—appears during the mile.

## Anti-Pattern

*Asking whether students can use the skill, then supplying the action for them*

After a puzzle reflection, the Guide asks a broad transfer question that students can answer with a yes or no:

> “Is that something we can do today during our workshops?”

> “Yes.”

The Guide then provides the application rather than requiring students to generate their own:

> “We can give feedback with clarity. We can give specific examples. We can give really specialized words to help our friends as they’re doing better.”

The Guide names a relevant context and useful actions, but the students do not select a personal situation or state what they themselves will do. Agreement with the Guide’s plan is weaker evidence of transfer than a student-generated context-and-action response.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Context–Cue–Action** sequence:

1. **Context:** Ask the student to choose one real setting.
   - “Where will you need this today: TimeBack Learning, workshop, the field, or home?”
2. **Cue:** Identify the moment that should trigger the skill.
   - “What will happen right before you need it?”
   - “How will you know it is time to use the strategy?”
3. **Action:** Require an observable move.
   - “What exactly will you do or say?”
4. **Make it personal:** Ask why that context matters to the student.
   - “Why did you choose that situation?”
5. **Confirm the plan:** Restate it and have the student verify it.
   - “So when ___ happens, you will ___. Is that your plan?”

A useful response frame is:

> “When I am ___ and I notice ___, I will ___.”

Examples:

> “When I begin TimeBack Learning and feel stuck, I will reread the directions before asking for help.”

> “When my partner is presenting in workshop, I will listen for one clear strategy and give specific feedback afterward.”

> “When I finish using art materials at home, I will return them without waiting to be asked.”

Avoid stopping at:

- “Can you use this today?”
- “Where could this help?”
- “Will you remember this?”
- “Can we do this in workshop?”

Follow broad answers with questions that make the context and action concrete:

> “Which workshop?”

> “At what moment?”

> “What will you do first?”

The goal is not merely for students to agree that the skill matters. The goal is for each student to leave with a personally relevant action they can recognize and carry out.
