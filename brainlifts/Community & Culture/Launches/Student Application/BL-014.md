# Embed Immediate Use in the Next Authentic Task

## Pattern

Design the Launch so students use the target skill immediately in the real task that follows. Build the application into the next TimeBack Learning block, workshop, mile, collaboration, transition, or other authentic part of the day.

The Launch should not end with application existing only as a discussion. The Guide should create a direct bridge between the experience and the next context by specifying:

- **the authentic task in which the skill will be used;**
- **the moment or cue that should trigger the skill;**
- **the action students will perform;** and
- **the evidence that will show the skill was used.**

The application can be embedded through a required routine, role, artifact, strategy, or success criterion. Students might prepare their actual TimeBack Learning materials, carry an encouragement sign into the mile, use a feedback checklist during the next presentation, or apply a communication routine with their workshop team.

This pattern is distinct from asking students to describe a personal application. Here, the Guide structures the next task so that students actually perform the skill.

## Evidence

*1- Students practice independence by preparing for the real learning blocks that follow*

The Guide asks students to set up the materials they will actually need during the school day:

> “I want you to set up your space for the day all by yourself.”

> “I want you to get set up for time back on the yellow chair.”

> “I want you to grab all your stuff that you think you need for time back on those tables.”

> “I want you to set [check chart] up all by yourselves at the middle table with the stools.”

> “Remember when we have writing time? Think about what you need.”

The Launch then transitions directly into the prepared task:

> “Guess what time it is now?”

> “It’s time for time back, and we’re already ready to go.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because independence is not left as a theme or hypothetical plan. Students immediately use it to prepare the real materials and spaces needed for TimeBack Learning, check chart, and writing. The result of the Launch becomes functional evidence: when TimeBack Learning begins, the students are already prepared.

*2- Students create an artifact that will be used during the mile*

The Guide connects the Growth Mindset Launch to a real physical challenge later that day:

> “During your mile today, you are going to make a sign for yourself that we can hold up and encourage you.”

> “On your sign today, you are going to write your remember when.”

The Guide explains how the sign will function during the challenge:

> “So when we hold up the sign, you can see what you should remember to feel good or push you over the edge or dig a little deeper for your grit or push your growth mindset.”

Students then begin creating the artifact:

> “I’m going to give you your cardboard.”

> “When you have your remember when in your head, I want you to let a guide know and then we’ll start making our sign.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Launch produces a concrete tool that will be carried into the authentic mile. The target strategy—recalling a past success—is attached to a visible cue that students will encounter while they are tired and need persistence.

## Anti-Pattern

*Discussing how the skill could transfer, then moving into an unrelated task without embedding its use*

Near the end of a perseverance Launch, the Guides ask students how the ideas could apply during the day:

> “How do we carry any of those ideas towards what we’re doing today from the second we finish this launch?”

The discussion produces broad actions:

> “You can use them as inspiration.”

> “Stay focused.”

> “You can lead by example.”

The Guide closes with a general exhortation:

> “Stay focused. Lead by example. Let’s all persevere today.”

The immediate transition is then operational:

> “All right. By 9:05, you need to start language.”

The discussion identifies relevant principles, but the next task does not contain a defined cue, required action, student role, or evidence of perseverance. Students move into language with encouragement to use the skill, but the task itself has not been designed to make that application visible or necessary.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Name–Embed–Cue–Observe** bridge:

1. **Name the next authentic task.**
   - “TimeBack Learning begins immediately after this Launch.”
2. **Embed one required use of the skill.**
   - “Before opening your app, independently prepare your computer, headphones, notebook, and pencil.”
3. **Create a cue.**
   - “When you reach a problem you cannot solve on the first attempt, use the strategy card beside your computer.”
4. **Define evidence.**
   - “I will know you used the strategy when I see the first attempt, the adjustment, and the second attempt.”
5. **Release students directly into the task.**
   - Avoid an unrelated transition between the Launch and the application.
6. **Revisit the evidence briefly.**
   - “Where did you use the Launch strategy during TimeBack Learning?”

A concise transition script is:

> “In the next task, when ___ happens, you will ___. We will know you used the skill when ___.”

Examples:

> “During the next workshop presentation, observers will record one example of specific feedback and share it after the speaker finishes.”

> “During the mile, when you see your sign, recall the success written on it and choose to continue to the next marker.”

> “When TimeBack Learning begins, set up everything you need without waiting for a Guide prompt.”

Avoid ending with only:

- “Use this today.”
- “Remember to persevere.”
- “Be a leader.”
- “Take this into your workshops.”

These statements can reinforce the theme, but they do not ensure application. Place the target behavior inside the next real task so students have an immediate opportunity to perform it.
