# Require Students to Justify Answers With Evidence

## Pattern

After a student gives an answer, judgment, or rating, require the student to identify the evidence that supports it. Ask what the student saw, heard, noticed, or used from the task, and then ask how that evidence connects to the conclusion.

An answer alone shows what a student selected. A justification reveals whether the student understands the underlying concept or is guessing, repeating a peer, or recalling a label without meaning.

The Guide should make evidence part of the expected response rather than an optional follow-up used only when an answer is incorrect. Students should know that a complete response includes:

- the answer or claim;
- a specific observation, clue, action, or result; and
- an explanation of how the evidence supports the answer.

## Evidence

*1- Students must use clues to explain an eliminated answer*

During a process-of-elimination challenge, the Guide asks students to identify an incorrect choice and explain how they know.

> “Using process of elimination, what is our first wrong answer? And how did you know it is wrong?”

A student responds by referring to the wording of the riddle:

> “The cloud would be wrong because you can see that during the day.”

The Guide continues requiring reasons for later choices:

> “Why is moon another wrong answer?”

> “Moon is supposed to come out in the night time.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because students cannot complete the reasoning by naming a choice alone. They must connect each eliminated answer to a clue in the riddle.

*2- Observers identify the behavior behind a performance judgment*

After a student presents a commercial, the Guide asks the audience to identify where they observed the target public-speaking skills.

> “Can anybody identify where they saw tone, confidence, persuasion, audience engagement, gestures, slogan, things of that nature?”

A student justifies the judgment of confidence with an observable behavior:

> “Confidence is like one thing that she looked at us all the time.”

Another student identifies evidence of engagement:

> “Her whole body was engaged.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because students must point to something the presenter actually did. The target concepts—confidence and engagement—are connected to evidence such as sustained eye contact and body use.

## Anti-Pattern

*Accepting a label or choral response without asking how students know*

At the beginning of a Growth Mindset Launch, the Guide prompts students to name the life skill and select the type of mindset.

> “Can everyone say it with us? Grit, awesome.”

> “And what kind of mindset do you need to have good grit? Fixed or growth?”

The Guide then supplies examples of the two mindsets and moves into repeated definitions and affirmations:

> “I can learn from my friends. I will get better with practice. Trying new things helps my brain grow.”

> “Let’s say the whole thing together.”

Choral recall can build shared language, but it does not show whether individual students can distinguish a fixed mindset from a Growth Mindset using evidence. The exchange would provide stronger evidence of understanding if students had to classify a statement or behavior and explain what made it fit the category.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use an **Answer–Evidence–Link** routine:

1. **Answer:** Ask for the student’s claim, choice, or rating.
   - “Which option does not fit?”
   - “Was that feedback helpful?”
2. **Evidence:** Require a specific observation.
   - “What clue makes you think that?”
   - “What did the speaker do that showed confidence?”
3. **Link:** Ask the student to explain the connection.
   - “How does that clue rule out the answer?”
   - “Why does that behavior demonstrate confidence?”
4. **Test the reasoning:** Present a contrast or possible exception.
   - “Would the same answer still work if this clue changed?”
5. **Invite another student to evaluate the evidence.**
   - “Do you agree with the evidence? Add or challenge one part.”

A complete response frame is:

> “I think ___ because I noticed ___. That evidence shows ___.”

When a student gives only a label, respond with:

> “That is your answer. Now show us the evidence.”

Avoid confirming an answer immediately and moving on. Listen for whether the student’s evidence is relevant and whether the explanation actually supports the conclusion. Correct answers with weak reasoning still need coaching.
