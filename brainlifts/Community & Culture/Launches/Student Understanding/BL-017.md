# Have students evaluate quality before asking them to produce it

## Pattern
Across the strongest Limitless Launches, Guides strengthened student understanding by turning students into evaluators before asking them to perform the skill themselves. Students first judged examples, compared quality, or applied criteria to someone else's attempt. Then they used that same mental model in their own work.

This brainlift is not about lecturing students on criteria. It is about making students use the criteria before they perform.

## Evidence
In the Feedback / Kind and Helpful Launch, the Guide did not begin by asking students to give feedback to each other. First, the Guide gave students a simple rating system and asked them to judge examples of feedback.

The Guide established the criteria:
> "I need you guys to rate whether that feedback is kind and helpful, just kind but not helpful, or not kind and not helpful."

Then the Guide gave sample feedback and asked students to evaluate it:
> "What if I said, nice try, but maybe take smaller steps next time?"

When students disagreed or were unsure, the Guide pushed them to justify the evaluation:
> "Why is this feedback not kind?"

> "What part of the feedback felt not nice?"

Later, after students had judged several examples, the Guide asked them to produce their own kind and helpful feedback:
> "Can someone give me an example from my books walking? What would be an example of kind and helpful?"

Students had a clearer target because they had already practiced evaluating what counted as kind, helpful, both, or neither.

Source artifact: Feedback / Kind and Helpful Launch transcript.

In the Tone of Voice Launch, the Guide first had students evaluate how different tones changed the meaning of the same phrase before asking them to practice using tone themselves.

The Guide modeled one version and asked the student to judge its impact:
> "Oh, excuse me, Griffin. I didn't mean to bump into you... How did that make you feel?"

Then the Guide modeled a harsher version:
> "Excuse me, Griffin."

The student evaluated the effect:
> "Probably make me feel angry."

The Guide then asked students to identify what changed:
> "What was different in the way that I said it?"

Student:
> "You were like yelling it out loud."

Only after students evaluated the examples did the Guide move them into the practice task:
> "So, we're going to practice that today."

Students were not just told, "Use a kind tone." They first experienced and evaluated how tone changed the message.

Source artifact: Tone of Voice Launch transcript.

## Anti-Pattern
In the Public Speaking / Pen Commercial Launch, the Guide gave students several criteria before asking them to create commercials, but students were not asked to evaluate a sample commercial before performing.

The Guide explained the categories:
> "Tone."

> "Confidence."

> "Persuasion."

> "Audience engagement."

The Guide then explained what the categories meant:
> "Tone, it's the inflection and how we use our voice..."

> "Confidence. When we talk, we speak. We are looking... I'm making eye contact..."

> "Persuasion is the ability to get somebody to believe something that you believe or to buy something that you're selling."

Then students were sent directly into production:
> "I will give you two minutes... to come up with a 60 second commercial selling us your pen."

The missed opportunity was not that the Guide lacked criteria. The missed opportunity was that students did not first use those criteria to judge an example. A stronger version would have shown two quick sample commercials and asked students which one had stronger tone, confidence, persuasion, or audience engagement before students created their own.

Source artifact: Public Speaking / Pen Commercial Launch transcript.

## Reusable Coaching Move
Judge one example before students perform.

Before students attempt the skill, give them one quick example and ask them to evaluate it using the criteria.

Formula:
1. Name 2-3 criteria.
2. Show or model one example.
3. Ask students to judge it.
4. Ask students to explain their judgment.
5. Then send students to perform.

Useful prompts:
> "Which example is stronger, and why?"

> "What made that feedback helpful?"

> "Which part showed confidence?"

> "What should this person change before trying again?"

> "What criterion did they meet? What criterion is missing?"

Do not only tell students what good looks like. Make them recognize it before they try to produce it.
