# Use Contrasting Examples and Have Students Infer the Distinction

## Pattern

Present two or more examples that differ on the feature students need to understand, then ask students to notice, compare, classify, and explain the distinction before the Guide states the rule.

Contrasting examples help students build a concept from observable differences. Rather than memorizing a definition, students identify what changes across examples and connect that change to a different effect, category, or outcome.

Effective contrasts:

- keep most features the same while changing the critical feature;
- include both examples and non-examples;
- ask students what they noticed before naming the distinction;
- require students to classify a new example using the distinction; and
- end by naming the rule in language students can reuse.

The Guide may clarify the concept after students reason. The key is that students first have a genuine opportunity to infer what separates the examples.

## Evidence

*1- The same phrase is delivered with two different tones*

The Guide says a similar phrase twice, first in a neutral manner and then in an angry manner. After the second version, the Guide asks students to identify what changed.

> “Say I’m just walking. I’m like, ‘Oh, excuse me, [student]. I didn’t mean to bump into you.’”

The student reports that the first delivery did not make them angry. The Guide then repeats the situation with a different delivery:

> “What if I was walking? And I was like, ‘Excuse me, [student].’”

> “Probably make me feel angry.”

The Guide asks the student to infer the distinction:

> “What was different in the way that I said it?”

> “You were like yelling it out loud.”

> “What else was different?”

> “You were so angry.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the words and situation remain similar while the Guide changes the tone. Students identify volume and emotion as the distinguishing features before the Guide consolidates the learning as “tone of voice.”

*2- Students classify feedback that is unkind, kind-only, or kind and helpful*

The Guide presents a sequence of feedback statements and asks students to rate each one using three categories.

> “I need you guys to rate whether that feedback is kind and helpful, just kind but not helpful, or not kind and not helpful.”

The examples create increasingly precise contrasts:

> “That was terrible.”

> “You’re bad at this.”

> “Good job.”

> “Nice try.”

> “Nice try, but maybe take smaller steps next time.”

The Guide repeatedly asks students to classify and explain:

> “How do we rate that feedback?”

> “Why is that not good feedback?”

> “Why is it not helpful?”

> “Can someone tell [the student], why did you pick that one?”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because students encounter examples that vary in kindness and usefulness. The contrast helps them distinguish praise that is merely positive from feedback that also provides an actionable next step.

## Anti-Pattern

*Naming the categories and supplying the distinction before students examine examples*

At the beginning of a Growth Mindset Launch, the Guide asks students to choose between fixed and Growth Mindset, then immediately supplies the defining statements.

> “What kind of mindset do you need to have good grit? Fixed or growth?”

> “Not fixed. Is when you say things like, I’m afraid to try this. I should give up.”

> “Growth, good job. Let’s see what a growth mindset says. I can learn from my friends. I will get better with practice. Trying new things helps my brain grow.”

The section then moves into shared repetition:

> “Let’s say the whole thing together, ready?”

The statements provide correct language, but students are not first asked to compare unlabeled examples and infer what separates a fixed response from a Growth Mindset response. Students can repeat the category names without demonstrating that they can independently recognize the distinction in a new situation.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use a **Show–Notice–Sort–Name–Test** sequence:

1. **Show:** Present two matched examples that differ mainly on the target feature.
   - Use the same sentence with a calm tone and an angry tone.
   - Use “Good job” and “You kept the base wide, which made it stable.”
2. **Notice:** Ask students to describe differences without supplying the label.
   - “What changed?”
   - “What did you hear or see in the second example?”
3. **Sort:** Ask students to group or classify additional examples.
   - “Which example belongs with the first one? Why?”
4. **Name:** After students identify the pattern, provide the precise term.
   - “The difference you noticed is tone of voice.”
5. **Test:** Give a new example and require students to apply the distinction.
   - “Is this kind only, or kind and helpful? What evidence proves it?”
6. **Generate:** Ask students to create one example and one non-example.
   - “Show us the same message in two different tones.”

A concise Guide script is:

> “Study these two examples. What stayed the same? What changed? What effect did that change have? What rule can we make?”

Avoid changing several features at once. When the examples differ in topic, wording, difficulty, speaker, and outcome, students may not know which difference matters. Keep the contrast focused enough that the target distinction becomes visible.
