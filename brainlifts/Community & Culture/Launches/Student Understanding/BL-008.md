# Student understanding is measured through reasoning

## Pattern
Across the strongest Limitless Launches, Guides did not infer understanding from finished work or correct outcomes. They deliberately looked for evidence of students' thinking by asking them to explain, justify, predict, compare, or defend their ideas before deciding whether learning had occurred. 

## Evidence
In the House of Cards Launch, instead of simply celebrating the tallest tower, the Guide repeatedly asked students to explain why their strategies worked and what they learned from other groups. 
After the challenge, the guide asked:
> "You guys who looked over in other groups and saw a good strategy. Asher, what did you see that they were doing over here?"
Student:
> "They were like bending the cards... flat it's easier to fall over. If you bend it... it's harder to bend over."
Instead of judging the final tower, the Guide sought evidence of students' strategy selection, and observation.
Source artifact: [House of Cards Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1dY1kBuN2iQZF6CN6e-tYMlpJS21YtuDz/view?usp=sharing)

In the Jurassic Park Survival Launch, rather than evaluating students based on the survival plan they produced, the Guide asked them to defend the reasoning behind their choices throughout the discussion.
Students debated survival options during the activity:
> "What if our shelter was high?"
> "Does everybody know how to climb a tree?"
Afterward, the Guide asked students to justify their thinking rather than simply identifying the correct answer.
Students had to explain how they arrived at decisions during the simulation instead of simply presenting their finished plan.
Source artifact: [Jurassic Park Survival Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1JCdqoRkaiRmEAf7yvPVWMVGeypt_vTjY/view?usp=sharing)

## Anti-Pattern
In the Self-love Launch, the Guide accepted finished work with little probing of students' thinking. Most questions focused on what students wrote rather than why they chose those ideas or how they arrived at them.
After students completed their link chains, the guide asked:
> "What are some things that you said you love about yourself?"
Students listed answers:
> "My eyes, my style, my hair, personality, hobbies..."
The guide acknowledged the responses:
> "Ideas. I love that one."
But there was little follow-up asking students to justify, defend, compare, or explain why those qualities mattered or how they chose them.
Source artifact: [Self-Love Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1H5B3E3KGFIj2hYnLeZs6orUlXDyo16zw/view?usp=sharing)

## Reusable Coaching Move
When checking understanding, avoid asking straightforward questions. Instead, ask questions that require students reveal their thinking, such as:
> "Why did you choose that?"
> "How do you know?"
> "What evidence supports your idea?"
> "What would happen if ... ?"
> "Convince me your strategy is better."
If students can explain their reasoning, the Guide has evidence of understanding. If they cannot, the Guide has identified an opportunity for further coaching.
