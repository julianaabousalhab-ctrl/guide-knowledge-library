# Publish Student-Facing Pass Criteria on Day One — Ambiguity Reads as a Motivation Problem

## Pattern

When students fail a Test2Pass, the underlying cause is often not skill or grit but that success was never made concrete from the learner's perspective. The strong practice, converged on independently by guides at multiple campuses:

1. **Translate the rubric into 3–5 student-facing behaviors** ("Pass Looks Like: explain your reasoning before choosing, invite a teammate into the plan, revise after feedback").
2. **Publish it on the first day of the workshop** — visibly posted, not revealed on T2P day.
3. **Use the same language at launch, midpoint, and final reflection**, so students are never guessing what the Life Skill looks like in action.

The diagnostic insight is the sharpest part: unclear success criteria *masquerade* as motivation problems. Students who don't know what success looks like disengage, and guides then coach grit — treating a design failure as a student failure.

## Evidence

*1- The misdiagnosis named, and the fix stated*

> "I noticed several students failing the first attempt at different Test-to-Passes early in the year. Rather than treating each miss as a motivation or grit issue, I looked for the common denominator. The pattern wasn't the students—it was that while we had designed engaging experiences, I hadn't yet coached the guides to make success concrete enough from the learner's perspective." → "Design from the learner's perspective. Make success visible, break it into clear milestones, and ensure every learner knows exactly what success looks like and the next step to get there."

> "When workshop expectations were not broken down early enough (My fault!) into concrete success behaviors, critical thinking and teamwork were the LifeSkill areas most likely to break down..." → "Before every high-complexity workshop, launch a simple 'Pass Looks Like' checklist with 3 student-facing behaviors tied to the rubric. ... Guides should use the SAME language during launch, midpoint reset, and final reflection so students are not guessing what the LifeSkill actually looks like in action."

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is running an engaging workshop whose pass bar stays in the guide's head — revealed to students only on assessment day.

*Criteria withheld until T2P day, and the documented cost*

> "In code crew, students were vaguely instructed that they were working on teamwork but were not given exact/clear metrics on what a 'pass' was for T2P until T2P day, and the pacing of instruction was too slow so many students didn't have adequate practice or knowledge on coding independently."

Result recorded in the same feather: "Two of three students in my guide group did not pass their T2P for Code Crew and Splash Squad because passing metrics and instructional methodologies were not clearly communicated or implemented."

Source artifact:[Feathers and Fix](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1sFC8H7xKUoAvJvpnfQKF3H56KEa1ECeD/view?usp=drive_link)

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Translate → Post → Echo → Check process at every workshop launch.

### 1. Translate the rubric into learner behaviors

Convert each rubric criterion into an observable student behavior a learner could self-check mid-task. If a criterion can't be translated, it isn't assessable yet.

### 2. Post it on day one

Present the pass criteria, one finished example, and the common mistakes at the first session — publicly and permanently visible, so absences and late joiners can still see the target.

### 3. Echo the same language throughout

Use identical criterion language at launch, midpoint reset, and final reflection. New phrasing mid-workshop reintroduces the fog.

### 4. Check clarity before diagnosing motivation

When a student disengages or fails, first verify they can state what passing looks like and their next step. Only coach grit after clarity is confirmed.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The workshop was engaging and the rubric existed in the guide plan."

The standard is:

> "From day one, any student could state what passing looks like, in behavior terms, and what their next step is — and no failure gets read as a motivation problem until clarity has been ruled out."

## Confidence: High — 4 instances converging independently across at least three campuses, + documented cost of withheld criteria.
