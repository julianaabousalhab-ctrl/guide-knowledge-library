# Treat Mass Failure as a Design Signal — Trigger a System Review, Not Individual Remediation

## Pattern

When one student fails a AlphaTest, coach the student. When several students fail the same AlphaTest on the same skill in the same session, the evidence points at the workshop — its instruction, expectations, practice volume, assessment design, or conditions — and the response should be a structured review of the shared barrier, not N parallel individual remediations. The converged practice:

1. **A numeric trigger** that converts failures into a system review automatically (e.g., three students or ≥30% failing the same AlphaTest), removing the judgment call.
2. **A barrier taxonomy** for the review: instruction, unclear expectations, insufficient practice, assessment design, or assessment conditions.
3. **Escalation beyond the guide's control surface** when the barrier is structural: workshop redesign, age-band reassignment, or product-team feedback — because some causes cannot be fixed by any amount of coaching.

The same logic applies longitudinally to one student: repeated failures across workshops are a pattern to diagnose once, not a series of isolated results.

## Evidence

*1- The inference stated plainly*

> "When students hit the Print & Perfect workshop in Session 2, receiving-feedback didn't fully land. Two of three kids failed the same workshop on the same skill in the same session, which points at the workshop, not the kids."

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is absorbing a shared failure as a stack of individual failures — recording each one, remediating each student, and never asking what they had in common.

*Shared failures recorded individually; the pattern never triggered a review*

> "When multiple students failed the same T2P, the results kept being recorded as individual failures instead of automatically triggering a review of the shared failure pattern, targeted practice, and a scheduled retest."

The same failure mode appears longitudinally with one student: "When Ansel failed T2Ps across multiple sessions and skill areas, each failure kept being handled as a separate workshop result rather than being used to identify and address his recurring barrier" — eight attempts before a pattern review was designed.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Trigger → Classify → Test → Route process.

### 1. Set the trigger in advance

Adopt a numeric threshold (three students or 30% of participants failing the same T2P; or one student failing across 2+ workshops) that automatically opens a review — no discretion required.

### 2. Classify the shared barrier

Review the failed rubric criteria against the taxonomy: instruction, unclear expectations (check BL-088 first), insufficient practice or transfer (check BL-089), assessment design, or assessment conditions.

### 3. Test competing fixes small

Where the barrier is ambiguous, run 2–3 recovery variants within the guide group (reteach with examples, guided practice with feedback, revised conditions) and retest — let results pick the fix.

### 4. Route structural causes upward

If the cause sits in workshop design, age-band fit, or product mechanics, escalate with the evidence to whoever owns that layer — and say so in the record, rather than coaching students around a broken system.

The coaching standard is not:

> "Each student who failed received support and a retake."

The standard is:

> "A shared failure automatically produced a review of what the failures had in common — and the fix landed on the actual cause, even when the cause wasn't the students."
