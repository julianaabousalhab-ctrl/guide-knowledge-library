# After the Workshop Ends, Install the Life Skill Into the Daily Routine

## Pattern

Students demonstrate a life skill inside the workshop that taught it, then stop using it when the workshop ends — unless the Guide deliberately installs the skill into the ordinary school day. The converged practice:

1. **Pick one target life skill per workshop** and name where it lives in the daily schedule after the workshop closes — a concrete mapping, not a hope ("Entrepreneurship → ownership of Core Skills; Learn to Learn → note-taking during apps").
2. **Practice it through daily micro-reps** — small, named opportunities during breaks, launches, closings, and Core Skills where students exercise exactly that skill.
3. **Recognize it in the daily flow** — the skill is coached and celebrated during regular routines for the remainder of the session, so the workshop's ending is the practice's beginning.

The mechanism: workshops create the skill under supported, themed conditions; only repeated, recognized use in unthemed daily life makes it a habit. This differs from bridging a single Launch lesson to the next task (BL-014/BL-017): the unit here is a session-long installation of one skill across every ordinary routine.

## Evidence

*1- The gap named, and the one-skill-per-workshop mapping*

> "Students often demonstrated life skills during workshops before consistently applying those same skills during the regular school day. ... When workshops ended, we did not always create enough intentional opportunities for students to practice those same skills during Core Skills, Townhall, and everyday interactions." → "Identify one target life skill after each workshop and intentionally coach and recognize it during daily routines for the remainder of the session. for example: Entrepreneurship → ownership of Core Skills, Friendship Intelligence → conflict coaching, Critical Thinking → coaching conversations, Learn to Learn → note-taking during apps"

*2- Weekly skill teaching converted into daily practice missions*

> "Launch a weekly Connection Moments System: use one 15-minute Morning Launch or Closing each week to teach a concrete friendship skill, then give kids daily connection missions during break to practice invite, include, help, repair, notice, and celebrate. Track group 'Connection Moments' and close the week with a reflection on who each student connected with, helped, or included."

Source artifact:[Feathers and Fix](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1EeM_u3Bhb1wZZcDkEPWys37lRLQTAo3o/view?usp=drive_link)

*3- One consistent skill framework across the whole day, not per-workshop variants*

> "When students encountered difficult tasks outside of intentional coaching moments, they relied on their own instincts instead of a shared growth mindset framework..." → "Create one classroom-wide Growth Mindset Protocol that is used consistently across academics and workshops. Every challenge follows the same sequence: name the challenge, identify the strategy being practiced, celebrate the process, and reflect on what to try next."

A structural variant embeds the skill in existing activities: "restructure something students are already doing so that individual success is only possible when others succeed too. When students need each other to accomplish something, community identity forms naturally through shared stakes — not as a lesson about community, but as a lived exper[ience]."

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is starting the daily installation and letting it lapse — or never building it at all — so the skill remains workshop-bound and the culture gains from early practice evaporate.

*A daily ritual worked, wasn't sustained, and the skill's AlphaTest failed*

> "Make CoreSkills XP tracker a consistent pillar of the culture. Students celebrate every time a student announces a closed ring and updates the tracker. We started this and it gave the culture a burst of widespread support and energy but we didn't consistently enforce it by the end of the year."

The same campus's record shows the cost of the lapse: "All students failed their workshop on uplifting others and also feel as though their classmates do not uplift them as expressed by Claudia in her session 5 survey and Grayson when saying, 'When I got my trophy why did no one else cheer for me?' multiple times."

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Pick → Map → Rep → Recognize process at every workshop close.

### 1. Pick one skill

At the end of each workshop, choose the single life skill it developed that most needs to survive into daily life. One skill, deliberately — not the whole rubric.

### 2. Map it to a daily location

Name exactly where in the ordinary day the skill will now be practiced (Core Skills ownership, note-taking during apps, conflict moments, break-time missions). If you can't name the daily location, the skill has no home and will fade.

### 3. Rep it small and often

Build micro-repetitions into existing routines — daily missions, a consistent protocol language, or a restructured activity where the skill is required, not optional.

### 4. Recognize it until it's habitual — and don't let the ritual lapse

Coach and celebrate the skill in the daily flow for the remainder of the session. Treat the recognition routine itself as a commitment: an installed ritual that quietly stops is how the anti-pattern begins.

The coaching standard is not:

> "Students demonstrated the life skill and passed the workshop."

The standard is:

> "A month after the workshop ended, the skill has a named home in the daily schedule, students are still practicing it there, and the Guide is still recognizing it."

## Confidence: High — 3–4 instances across campuses + a documented lapse where the skill's AlphaTest then failed.
