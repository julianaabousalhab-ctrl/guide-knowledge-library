# Anchor Reflection in a Specific Event

## Pattern
Guides strengthen reflection when they begin with a concrete event the student can mentally revisit rather than asking only for a broad judgment about the week, subject, or experience.

A useful reflection anchor may be:
  - a particular performance
  - one difficult question
  - a conversation with a peer
  - a recent coaching call
  - an observed learning strategy
  - a missed commitment
  - a specific moment when the student adapted successfully

The recurring sequence is:

Identify the event → ask the student to replay what happened → examine the student’s response → identify what can be learned or repeated

Anchoring reflection in an event gives the student something observable to analyze. It reduces vague answers such as “good,” “bad,” or “I don’t know” because the student is not being asked to summarize an entire week from memory.

This pattern concerns where the reflection begins. Follow-up questions may deepen the student’s reasoning, but the first coaching move is to ground the discussion in a real moment.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide anchors reflection in one specific part of a recent performance*

The Guide begins by naming the recent event.

> Guide: “But speaking of which, we had our test to pass yesterday. How'd you feel now that it's over?”

The student identifies one specific portion that felt unsuccessful.

> Student: “I felt good except for the Korean War Memorial.”

The Guide narrows the reflection to that part of the performance.

> Guide: “What was up with the Korean War Memorial?”

> Student: “I messed it up.”

The Guide asks the student to explain the judgment using details from the event.

> Guide: “You messed it up? Why do you feel like you messed it up?”

> Student: “I said a lot of filler words and also I said the wrong thing and I didn't do it good.”

The reflection is anchored in:
  - a particular AlphaTest
  - one named section of the performance
  - observable details the student remembers from that section

The Guide is therefore able to respond to the student’s actual experience rather than discussing public speaking in general.

*2- The Guide anchors reflection in an observed strategy from the previous day*

The Guide refers to something directly observed the day before.

> Guide: “And what about I noticed yesterday that you had a sheet of paper beside you with a lot of different like things that you wrote. Tell me about that.”

The student explains where the strategy was used.

> Student: “Yeah, I used it on the tower of power.”

The Guide checks whether the strategy applied only to that task.

> Guide: “Oh, just the tower of power?”

> Student: “And the other lessons to get past the tower.”

The Guide asks for the result of the specific action.

> Guide: “And then what happened?”

> Student: “I got two tower power.”

The Guide then connects the event to future use.

> Guide: “Oh my gosh. Fantastic. So, do you plan on doing that again?”

> Student: “Yeah.”

The reflection begins with a concrete artifact—the student’s sheet of written notes—and traces what happened after the student used it.

Because the Guide anchors the conversation in an actual strategy and result, the student can identify a behavior worth repeating.

Source artifact: [Limitless Meeting](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1OW_Hh3dQVC0txBDVDDLlw3IxWpH_o9Tatq8H1dYBukk/edit?usp=sharing)

*3- The Guide anchors leadership reflection in the student’s interaction with new students*

The Guide asks the student to identify the best part of the week.

> Guide: “What was the best thing about this week?”

The student identifies an experience involving new students.

> Student: “The important with the new students.”

The Guide then anchors the leadership reflection in that occasion.

> Guide: “How did you think that you were being a leader last week, or on that day? How did you show them.”

The student identifies what they did.

> Student: “I was trying to show them, like, all the things, and I wanted to be nice to them.”

The Guide’s question moves the conversation away from a broad identity claim such as “I am a leader.” The student is asked to use one interaction as evidence of how leadership was demonstrated.

That concrete event makes the reflection more credible and observable.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is asking for a broad judgment, accepting a vague answer, and moving into praise or advice without helping the student examine a specific moment.

A Guide may mention a recent activity, but the reflection remains shallow when the student is not asked to replay what happened.

*A recent workout is mentioned, but the student’s broad answer is not explored*

The Guide refers to the student’s recent preparation and workout.

> Guide: “How are you feeling with preparation for Spartan? How are you feeling after yesterday's workout?”

The student gives a broad response.

> Student: “I'm doing good.”

The Guide responds with recognition.

> Guide: “I know you are. Dude, I'm really impressed. You have thrown yourself entirely into everything, not just academics, but also workshops.”

The praise may be deserved, but the conversation does not return to the workout itself.

The student is not asked:
  - what happened during the workout
  - which moment was difficult
  - what the student did when the work became hard
  - what the experience revealed about their preparation

The activity is referenced, but it is not used as a reflection anchor. The result is recognition without a detailed examination of the experience.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Locate → Replay → Interpret → Transfer process.

### 1. Locate one event

Choose a specific moment that is recent and relevant to the coaching goal.

Use evidence from:
  - an observed task
  - a student work sample
  - a recorded result
  - a conversation
  - an event the student identifies

Avoid trying to reflect on the entire week at once.

### 2. Replay what happened

Ask the student to describe the sequence of the event.

Clarify:
  - what happened first
  - what the student noticed
  - what the student did
  - what happened next

Keep the conversation grounded in what the student remembers or what the evidence shows.

### 3. Interpret the event

Help the student examine:
  - why they responded that way
  - what helped
  - what made the situation harder
  - what the outcome suggests

Do not supply the lesson before the student has examined the event.

### 4. Transfer the learning

Identify what should be:
  - repeated
  - adjusted
  - avoided
  - tested in the next similar situation

The next step should arise from the event rather than from generic advice.

The coaching standard is not:

> “The student gave an opinion about how the week went.”

The standard is:

> “The student examined a real moment closely enough to learn from it.”
