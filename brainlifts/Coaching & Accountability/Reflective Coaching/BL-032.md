# Observe the Student Performing the Task Before Diagnosing the Problem

## Pattern
Guides strengthen reflective coaching when they watch the student perform the actual task, inspect the exact work, or test the reported difficulty before deciding what is causing the problem.

Performance data and student descriptions are useful, but they may not reveal whether the barrier is:
  - content knowledge
  - strategy
  - task directions
  - technology
  - instructional fit
  - physical setup
  - the difference between completing an application and understanding the underlying concept

The recurring sequence is:

Identify the concern → observe a representative task → test what the student knows and does → diagnose the barrier → match the support to the evidence

The Guide does not need to observe every problem for an extended period. A short, purposeful sample may be enough when it directly tests competing explanations.

This pattern differs from diagnosing whether the barrier is strategy, instructional fit, or system failure. That pattern concerns the conclusion the Guide reaches. This pattern concerns the evidence-gathering method used before reaching that conclusion.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide watches the student complete a division lesson before distinguishing application performance from conceptual understanding*

The student says that math feels difficult. Rather than diagnosing the issue from the student’s metrics alone, the Guide asks to observe the work.

> Guide: “You wanna do some math together right now?”

The student opens the division lesson and works through the application while the Guide watches.

During the lesson, the student asks:

> Student: “What does divisible mean?”

The Guide continues examining the student’s understanding through the exact task.

> Guide: “Is 15? Yeah, divisible by five? In other words, can you divide 15 by five?”

The student responds:

> Student: “I still don't know division by like”

After watching the student manipulate the dots and answer within the application, the Guide identifies the difference between procedural completion and conceptual understanding.

> Guide: “Because it sounds like you're not having any trouble with clicking the right button on the app or doing what the app is saying, but at the end they you still don't understand what division is. Would you agree?”

The Guide then matches the next support to the observed barrier.

> Guide: “In the coaching call, I actually want you to tell the coach, hey, can we do some division, not on synthesis, and you explain to me how division works and what it is.”

The diagnosis comes from direct observation: the student can follow the application’s directions, but the application has not produced an understanding of division.

*2- The Guide watches the student read before deciding whether the barrier is word recognition or microphone pickup*

The Guide states the intention to observe a representative sample of the reading task.

> Guide: “Meaning I want to watch you do a little bit of your clear fluency just for just for a few minutes.”

The student then reads the passage aloud while the Guide listens and watches how the application responds.

After the application does not correctly register a word, the Guide tests the student’s knowledge directly.

> Guide: “So say that right there.”

> Guide: “What does that word say?”

> Student: “Belongs.”

Only after hearing the student identify the word does the Guide diagnose the likely barrier.

> Guide: “Yes. So the thing is, this happens to a lot of students. You know the words. It might be a speaking, like, microphone issue.”

The Guide then proposes a strategy matched to that observed problem.

> Guide: “One of the strategies I would incorporate is taking extra care to bring the microphone in front of your mouth and make sure you're speaking directly into it with sharp syllables to it.”

The Guide does not assume that a rejected word means the student cannot read it. Observation shows that the student recognizes the word, shifting the diagnosis toward microphone pickup and enunciation.

Source artifact: [Limitless Meeting](https://docs.google.com/document/d/12hSBbxfCTO3iMX-x60tYjLlBiP8G9niZ70lssxOBDOQ/edit?usp=sharing)

*3- The Guide asks the student to read words marked incorrect before concluding that word knowledge is not the barrier*

The Guide refers to repeated observation across reading lessons.

> Guide: “You know all the words. Like, that is not your problem. I've watched you do several of these lessons.”

The Guide then tests the application’s rejected words directly.

> Guide: “And when we go over each one, like, read the ones it says you didn't get right.”

The student reads them.

> Student: “Away, close, feet.”

The Guide confirms the result.

> Guide: “So you know all of those.”

The Guide then identifies how the microphone may be affecting the task.

> Guide: “Because it is a microphone and it does not, it can't hear you as clearly as I can hear you right now. Over enunciate, meaning that you take your time with each word.”

The Guide uses the student’s live performance to rule out one possible diagnosis: lack of word knowledge. The resulting coaching focuses on how the student speaks into the application rather than reteaching words the student already knows.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is diagnosing the cause from dashboard data, assumptions, or general impressions without first examining what the student actually did.

The Guide may identify a plausible explanation, but a plausible explanation is not the same as observed evidence.

*The Guide assumes missing quizzes caused low XP before confirming what the application required*

The Guide reviews the student’s dashboard and offers an immediate explanation for the low XP.

> Guide: “You didn't master all the units you needed to, you got one out of two. And you only got 60% of XP, it's probably because you didn't take the quizzes and tests.”

The student questions the diagnosis.

> Student: “What quizzes and tests?”

The Guide acknowledges not having observed the task.

> Guide: “I'm sure they have different, I wasn't privy to what exactly you were doing, but there must have been some sort of mastery section that you had to prove, like there had to be something that you had to get through.”

The student then explains that the application contained lessons rather than the quizzes and tests the Guide assumed.

> Student: “No? Usually we just have like a bunch of lessons.”

The weakness is not reviewing dashboard data. The weakness is converting incomplete dashboard information into a diagnosis before inspecting the student’s task or asking the student to demonstrate what occurred.

The student has to correct an explanation that was presented without direct evidence.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the See → Test → Separate → Support process.

### 1. See the actual task

Ask the student to open:
  - the exact lesson
  - the rejected question
  - the work sample
  - the application screen
  - a representative version of the task

Observe long enough to understand what the student is being asked to do.

### 2. Test competing explanations

Use a short check to distinguish possible causes.

For example:
  - ask the student to read a word the microphone rejected
  - ask the student to explain the concept without the application
  - ask the student to repeat the process while narrating their thinking
  - inspect whether the required step was visible in the directions

The test should help rule explanations in or out.

### 3. Separate performance layers

Distinguish between:
  - knowing the answer and getting the application to register it
  - following directions and understanding the concept
  - working slowly and being off task
  - making an error and misunderstanding the entire skill
  - experiencing a technology problem and lacking effort

Do not combine different barriers into one diagnosis.

### 4. Match the support to the evidence

After observing, select support that addresses the identified barrier.

Possible responses include:
  - reteaching the concept
  - changing the strategy
  - adjusting the microphone or workspace
  - clarifying directions
  - arranging a targeted coaching call
  - escalating a system problem

The standard is not:

> “The Guide had a reasonable theory.”

The standard is:

> “The Guide gathered enough direct evidence to know which problem they were coaching.”
