# Co-Create and Confirm the Accountability Agreement

## Pattern

Guides strengthen accountability when the student helps shape the commitment and then explicitly confirms what they are agreeing to do.

A strong accountability agreement clarifies:
  - what the student will do
  - when or how often they will do it
  - what support the Guide will provide
  - whether the student genuinely accepts the plan

The recurring sequence is:

Identify the expectation → invite the student’s input → adjust the plan together → restate the agreement → confirm commitment

Co-creation does not mean the student controls every expectation. Some standards may be fixed. The Guide still preserves ownership by allowing the student to influence how the expectation will be met.

Confirmation matters because silence, passive agreement, or a quick “okay” does not always mean the student understands or accepts the commitment.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide adjusts the practice expectation with the student and confirms the final frequency*

The student explains that typing is contributing to ongoing stress.

> Student: “I’m mostly always feeling stressed out.”

The Guide first considers one possible adjustment.

> Guide: “Would you like to take a break from typing and not do it for a couple weeks?”

The student does not want to stop completely because they are concerned that they will not improve.

The Guide then proposes a middle ground that preserves the goal while reducing the pressure.

> Guide: “So what if we just made typing be once a week for 25 minutes, and so you continue the progress, but you’re not getting stressed out by it? Would that help?”

> Student: “Yeah, okay.”

Later, the Guide restates the final agreement.

> Guide: “So we’ll do, we’ll do typing only for 25 minutes once a week, deal?”

> Student: “Yeah.”

The agreement is co-created because the final plan responds to both positions:
  - the student does not want to abandon typing
  - the Guide does not want the current frequency to continue causing excessive stress

The Guide also confirms the specific commitment: 25 minutes, once each week.

*2- The student agrees to a daily teamwork action and then negotiates a more ambitious version*

The Guide and student discuss peer feedback related to teamwork.

Before proposing an action, the Guide asks permission.

> Guide: “Can I give you an idea?”

> Student: “Yeah.”

The Guide proposes one observable behavior.

> Guide: “Try to have one positive interaction with someone who isn’t really that close with you in class every day.”

The Guide explains that the interaction could be small, such as inviting someone to play or recognizing something they did well.

The Guide then turns the plan into a trackable agreement.

> Guide: “Remember we said one positive connection with someone you’re not close with a day, we’ll make it into a sticker chart.”

The student does not merely accept the original amount. The student asks to increase the commitment.

> Student: “What about if I do two people?”

The Guide accepts the student’s revision.

> Guide: “Okay, I’ll allow two people a day, so I’ll take you half a month.”

> Guide: “Sound good?”

> Student: “Yeah.”

This is strong co-creation because the student changes the terms of the agreement rather than simply complying with the Guide’s original proposal.

The final agreement is explicit:
  - complete up to two positive interactions each day
  - track them on the chart
  - work toward the agreed completion point

*3- The Guide asks the student to generate the transition plan and then restates it as the shared expectation*

The Guide identifies that setup time is reducing the student’s productive learning time.

Instead of assigning a routine immediately, the Guide asks the student to develop the solution.

> Guide: “So what can you do to make sure your transition time for setting up in between apps takes less time?”

The student proposes arriving and preparing earlier.

> Student: “Get here early.”

The student elaborates:

> Student: “And then when I like, get to school, if I get here before time, I can just start getting ready.”

The Guide turns the student’s idea into a concrete shared routine.

> Guide: “Okay, so before you play ping pong, maybe you set up your space and then you go play ping pong in the morning, or, like, do whatever else. Okay, that’s great.”

The Guide then continues asking the student to identify how the agreement will work during transitions.

> Guide: “What will help you transition between your apps quicker?”

> Student: “I have to pause my time.”

The accountability expectation is not imposed as a generic rule. It is built from the student’s own proposed behaviors:
  - set up the workspace before playing
  - manage the timer during app transitions

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is announcing a rule, reward, or consequence and treating the student’s compliance as proof of ownership.

A student may say “okay” because the Guide has authority. That does not mean the student helped shape the agreement, believes it will work, or understands how they will follow through.

*The Guide imposes both the behavior and the consequence without involving the student in the terms*

The student explains that they used a phone shortly before bed.

The Guide responds with a fixed rule.

> Guide: “You’re not allowed to look at a screen for 30 minutes before bed.”

The Guide then assigns a reward and penalty.

> Guide: “And if you complete that, I’ll give you one alpha. If you don’t, I’m gonna take away one alpha.”

The Guide later repeats the terms.

> Guide: “If you follow it, I’ll give you one alpha. If you don’t follow it, I’m gonna steal an alpha.”

The student is not asked:
  - what bedtime routine would be realistic
  - whether 30 minutes is workable
  - what might interfere with the plan
  - how the student will remember
  - what support would help

Although the student does not openly refuse, the agreement is primarily Guide-created.

The student’s response reflects acceptance of the rule, but not clear ownership of how it will be carried out.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Standard → Student Input → Shared Terms → Confirmation process.

### 1. State the standard

Clarify what expectation must be met and why it matters.

Separate fixed expectations from negotiable details.

### 2. Ask the student how they can meet it

Invite the student to propose:
  - the routine
  - the timing
  - the frequency
  - the reminder
  - the type of support they need

The student should contribute something meaningful to the plan.

### 3. Negotiate the terms

Test whether the proposed agreement is:
  - specific
  - realistic
  - measurable
  - appropriately challenging

The Guide may revise the student’s idea, but should explain why and preserve as much student ownership as possible.

### 4. Restate the agreement

Before ending the conversation, summarize:
  - the student’s responsibility
  - the Guide’s responsibility
  - the timing
  - how follow-through will be checked

### 5. Confirm commitment

Ask the student to explicitly accept or restate the agreement.

A strong confirmation shows that the student understands:
  - what they are doing
  - when they are doing it
  - what support is available

The standard is not:

> “The Guide told the student what should happen.”

The standard is:

> “The student helped shape the commitment and clearly confirmed ownership of it.”
