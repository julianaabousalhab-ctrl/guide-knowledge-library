# Teach a Repeatable Help-Seeking Routine

## Pattern

Guides strengthen student ownership when they teach students a repeatable process for recognizing when help is needed, identifying the exact barrier, requesting appropriate support, and using that support effectively.

The routine should help the student answer:
  - When should I keep trying independently?
  - What evidence tells me I need help?
  - What exactly am I stuck on?
  - Who is the right person to ask?
  - What should I say?
  - What do I do if the first support does not help?

The recurring sequence is:

Try independently → notice a clear help signal → identify the exact problem → ask the right person in specific language → apply the support → escalate or adjust if the problem remains

The purpose is not to make students dependent on coaching calls or Guides. The purpose is to make help-seeking a skill the student can use without waiting for an adult to notice the problem.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide teaches the student how to describe the exact learning need during a coaching call*

The Guide identifies that the student can complete the application steps but does not understand the underlying concept.

> Guide: “Because it sounds like you're not having any trouble with clicking the right button on the app or doing what the app is saying, but at the end they you still don't understand what division is. Would you agree?”

The Guide explains that the coaching call should focus on the actual barrier rather than repeating the application lesson.

> Guide: “But in the coaching call, I don't want you to just go through the lesson with the academic coach.”

The Guide gives the student specific language to use.

> Guide: “In the coaching call, I actually want you to tell the coach, hey, can we do some division, not on synthesis, and you explain to me how division works and what it is.”

Later, the Guide restates the message the student needs to communicate.

> Guide: “Making sure that we tell them, Hey, I know how to do this lesson well enough. But even though I know how to answer the questions in the lesson, I don't get division.”

The Guide explains why specificity improves the support.

> Guide: “That helps the coaching instructor help you.”

The Guide then generalizes the routine.

> Guide: “Sometimes with the coaching instructors, if you feel like it's not helpful for what you needed, tell them exactly what you need.”

This is strong evidence because the Guide teaches more than “ask for help.” The student learns to distinguish the visible task from the actual learning barrier and communicate the difference clearly.

Source artifact: [Limitless Meeting](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1tP-6x1hr5172TIEdsOsHvi1ybgmnZeu7hc0D-SZvSrE/edit?usp=sharing)

*2- The Guide and student define an observable trigger for seeking help and rehearse the full process*

The Guide asks what should happen if the student’s current strategy does not work.

> Guide: “So if that doesn't work, What's the next step? Like, if the checks are not working for us, what should we do?”

The Guide proposes a coaching call, but the student and Guide then calibrate when that step should happen.

> Guide: “I think we should book a coaching call. Like, if we get down to, like, 80% or lower on Amplified, I think it's time for a coaching call.”

The student contributes to the threshold discussion.

> Student: “So maybe about 70 maybe.”

The Guide and student settle on the trigger.

> Guide: “Maybe even 80. I think 80% it gets to 80% we're gonna book a coaching call?”

Later, the Guide asks the student to restate the plan.

> Guide: “Yeah, you tell me the plan. What is the plan?”

The student explains the routine.

> Student: “So, um, checks on Amplify. Cool. And then write down the words.”

> Student: “And then, um, if you see on and amplify that I am only getting 80% or less. Book a coaching call that day.”

The Guide clarifies that the first booking will be supported rather than completed entirely for the student.

> Guide: “We are going to book a coaching call, right? Not me, but us together.”

The student continues the sequence.

> Student: “And then we get approval.”

The Guide adds another preparation step.

> Guide: “And then we're going to know the coach's name beforehand.”

The final routine includes:
  - complete the Amplify checks
  - write down difficult words
  - use 80% or lower as a signal that support is needed
  - book the coaching call together
  - obtain approval
  - identify the coach before the call

This is repeatable because the student can use the same decision process during future lessons.

*3- The Guide teaches the student to track which coaches are effective and use that information when requesting support*

The Guide explains that one option when a lesson does not make sense is to book a coaching call.

> Guide: “So you get two options there. One, you book that coaching call, right?”

The student signals that the effectiveness of the support depends on the coach.

> Student: “It depends on the person.”

The Guide responds by teaching the student to request a coach who communicates effectively.

> Guide: “You can request. That's the thing. And also, you tell me, who are your favorite coaches?”

The student says:

> Student: “I don't remember their names.”

The Guide then gives the student a repeatable tracking routine.

> Guide: “From now on, this is what your job is.”

> Guide: “I want you to pay attention to who you like, coaching-wise.”

The Guide makes the documentation step explicit.

> Guide: “So figure out who you like, make a note, actually make a note, not a mental note.”

The Guide gives an example of what the note could contain.

> Guide: “Write down, like, Bruna is awesome. I love doing math with Bruna. Write that down during your lesson so you can tell me.”

This evidence strengthens the pattern because the student is taught what to do when support quality varies:
  - notice which coaches communicate effectively
  - document their names
  - use that information when requesting future help

The routine helps the student become more intentional about choosing support rather than passively accepting any coaching experience.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is telling the student to ask for help without teaching when, how, from whom, or what information to communicate.

“Ask for help” may sound supportive, but it is not yet a usable routine. The student may still wait too long, ask too vaguely, or depend on the Guide to notice the problem.

*The Guide names available support but does not define a repeatable process*

The Guide encourages the student to use academic support.

> Guide: “Reading the explanations and if you need a coaching call you should talk to me or [another Guide] and get that done.”

The Guide later reinforces the general importance of using available resources.

> Guide: “Take advantage of the resources. You have so many people that care about your success.”

The message is encouraging, but the student is not taught:
  - how to know when a coaching call is needed
  - how long to try independently
  - what information to bring
  - what to say to the Guide
  - how to identify the exact barrier
  - what to do if the coaching call is not helpful

The student is told that help exists, but not how to use it independently and consistently.

A stronger approach would turn “talk to me or a Guide” into a specific routine the student can repeat without waiting for adult direction.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Try → Notice → Name → Ask → Apply → Escalate process.

### 1. Try independently

Clarify what the student should attempt before requesting help.

This may include:
  - reading the directions
  - reviewing an example
  - trying one strategy
  - checking notes
  - completing a short representative portion of the task

The goal is productive independence, not prolonged struggle.

### 2. Notice the help signal

Teach the student what evidence means it is time to seek support.

Possible signals include:
  - accuracy falls below an agreed threshold
  - the same error repeats
  - the student cannot explain the concept
  - the student has tried the agreed strategies without progress
  - the application and the student’s understanding do not match

The signal should be observable rather than “when it feels hard.”

### 3. Name the exact barrier

Before contacting someone, the student should identify what they need.

The student should distinguish between statements such as:

> “I need help with math,”

> “I can follow the application steps, but I do not understand what division means.”

Specific requests produce more useful support.

### 4. Ask the right person

Teach the student who can solve the problem.

Depending on the barrier, that may be:
  - a Guide
  - an academic coach
  - a peer
  - a system-support contact
  - another adult with the required authority.

The student should know when they can ask directly and when the Guide must support the escalation.

### 5. Apply the support

After receiving help, the student should use the strategy or explanation on the next task.

Help-seeking is incomplete if the student receives an answer but does not apply or retain it.

### 6. Escalate or adjust if needed

When the first support does not work, teach the student to:
  - explain what remains unclear
  - request a different explanation
  - ask for a different coach
  - document the problem
  - involve the Guide

The standard is not:

> “The student knows that help is available.”

The standard is:

> “The student knows exactly when and how to seek useful help, and can repeat the process independently.”
