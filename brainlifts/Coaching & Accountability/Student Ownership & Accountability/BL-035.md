# Turn Leadership Potential Into Authentic Responsibility

## Pattern

Guides develop student leadership by moving beyond praise or leadership labels and giving the student a real responsibility that affects other people.

Authentic responsibility includes several of the following:
  - meaningful decisions
  - people or resources to coordinate
  - a visible outcome or deliverable
  - responsibility for communication
  - clear standards for success
  - later follow-up on execution

The recurring sequence is:

Recognize leadership potential → identify a real need → give the student meaningful ownership → clarify responsibilities and boundaries → support without taking over → follow up on execution

The student should not merely be called a leader or asked to “set a good example.” The student should have something consequential to lead.

## Evidence

*1- The student owns the design and operation of a 3D-printing club*

The Guide establishes that the student is not simply participating in the club.

> Guide: “So it’s a 3D printing club. You’re the president.”

The Guide asks the student to define what the club will actually do.

> Guide: “What does it look like, Do you teach kids about 3D printing? Do you guys print things out together?”

The student begins planning the first session.

> Student: “The first class, I’m gonna like make something.”

> Student: “I’ll 3D print my the thing I want and then I’ll explain to them how it works.”

The student also establishes the schedule.

> Guide: “How many days a week?”

> Student: “Two, Thursday and Tuesday.”

The responsibility extends beyond having an idea. The student must coordinate use of a shared resource with other groups.

> Guide: “You personally as the president, need to talk to each one of their guides.”

> Guide: “You need to find out what their schedule is so that you’re not conflicting with their 3d printing schedule because that’s... your responsibility.”

The Guide rehearses the conversation but leaves the student responsible for making the request.

> Student: “I’ll be doing a 3D printing club every Tuesday and Thursday and the second half of lunch.”

> Student: “And I want to ask if it’s fine with your schedule for 3D printing.”

When a possible conflict is introduced, the student generates a solution.

> Student: “Tuesdays, like, we won’t 3D print anything. We’ll just, like, sculpt or just talk about what we want to do. Thursdays are the days that we’re going to 3D print something.”

Later, the student communicates the plan directly to another Guide.

> Student: “I made a 3D printing club. And on the second half of lunch, we will be doing it for the two students. Is it fine? Because I see you guys do use a 3D printer a lot.”

The student’s responsibility includes:
  - designing the club format
  - teaching other students
  - deciding when planning and printing occur
  - coordinating equipment access
  - communicating with Guides
  - determining how students enter the club

The Guide provides structure and rehearsal without becoming the club’s operator.

*2- The student owns a school-wide tournament with real logistical decisions*

The Guide makes clear that the tournament belongs to the student.

> Guide: “Right now this is entirely yours like entirely your imagination which is a good thing so you can make it however you would want it to be because you’re in control.”

The Guide asks the student to identify the next steps rather than taking control of planning.

> Guide: “So having said that, what’s your next steps?”

The student proposes a process for collecting participants.

> Student: “So I would get every, like, so I would go with a jar...”

The Guide asks the student to think through additional operational decisions.

> Guide: “You got to figure out what the date is.”

> Guide: “The idea is you have the entire vision planned out, so you can start executing it.”

In the later meeting, the Guide follows up on whether the student acted on the responsibility.

> Guide: “Ping-pong tournament, where we at? Last week we talked.”

The student reports progress.

> Student: “So I have a lot of people, like a lot of names.”

The student explains the sign-up process.

> Student: “Last Friday I got like these pieces of paper and then I told them to like... put their name in the jar. That means they’re submitted in the tournament.”

The student identifies the next deliverable.

> Student: “And then I’m going to have to put them on a bracket at home today.”

The Guide helps the student identify additional leadership responsibilities without taking ownership away.

> Guide: “You need to have a date so that everyone knows where to be and when to be there. You have to have a date, a time.”

The Guide reinforces that the student should make the decision.

> Guide: “It’s your project.”

> Guide: “Pick the time, pick the date.”

The student also receives responsibility for delegating work.

> Guide: “Recruit people to your team so you don’t have to do everything.”

> Guide: “Write down what you have to do, and if you’re going to do it, or if someone else should do it, and then ask them to do it.”

This is authentic responsibility because the student must produce a visible school-wide event involving:
  - participant recruitment
  - sign-ups
  - brackets
  - dates and times
  - communication materials
  - delegation
  - execution

The later meeting also demonstrates follow-up rather than allowing the leadership assignment to disappear after the initial conversation.

*3- The student receives responsibility for running Town Hall rather than merely assisting*

The Guide defines the student’s role.

> Guide: “Let’s talk through town hall, since you are our town hall specialist.”

The student is responsible for ensuring that proposals are submitted.

> Guide: “The goal is, by the end of the day, anyone who has a town hall thing, it’s been submitted.”

The student also helps determine which topics meet the purpose of Town Hall.

> Guide: “The goal is to choose topics that we feel like make our class better.”

The Guide assigns responsibility for preparing and opening the meeting.

> Guide: “You’re going to use the checklist the agenda so you... write down a little agenda of topics.”

> Guide: “Be ready to give a quick recap of last week’s votes.”

The student proposes creating a presentation.

> Student: “On Thursday night or on Friday at some time, I can make like a presentation on Canva with like all the different topics.”

The Guide accepts the student’s contribution.

> Guide: “Yeah, that works great.”

The Guide then clarifies the student’s meeting-management responsibilities.

> Guide: “During Town Hall, you start with announcing the agenda item... and the speaker.”

> Guide: “You’re passing around the talking sticks.”

> Guide: “You’re gonna repeat what the votes are so everyone understands.”

> Guide: “You write notes on what’s happening, and then you move to next agenda item.”

The student confirms the size of the responsibility.

> Student: “I have to literally run it.”

The Guide confirms:

> Guide: “Basically correct.”

The Guide establishes support without removing ownership.

> Guide: “I know it’s your first time, so if you are, like, confused, or you need help, just tell me, and I’ll help you. But if you’re feeling good, I’m just gonna let you go and do your thing.”

The student is responsible for a visible community process involving:
  - collecting submissions
  - selecting eligible topics
  - preparing the agenda
  - opening the meeting
  - managing speakers and discussion
  - explaining voting options
  - recording outcomes
  - preparing the next recap

The role contains real decisions, public accountability, and a clear deliverable.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is calling a student a leader or encouraging them to influence others without giving them a defined responsibility, decision, deliverable, or follow-up point.

Leadership praise may be accurate and encouraging, but praise alone does not develop the student’s ability to lead.

*The student is repeatedly described as a leader, but the leadership expectation remains broad*

The Guide tells the student:

> Guide: “You continue to emerge as a leader in the classroom.”

The Guide explains that other students observe the student’s behavior.

> Guide: “All the other students look to you for like social cues.”

The Guide later encourages the student to support new students.

> Guide: “You do a good job taking them under your wing. So like introducing, making them feel comfortable. Do that with other students too.”

The recognition is positive, and welcoming new students is valuable. However, within this leadership discussion, the student is not given:
  - a defined group of students to support
  - a specific onboarding responsibility
  - a decision to make
  - a visible deliverable
  - a time when the responsibility should occur
  - a follow-up point

“Do that with other students too” communicates a leadership expectation, but not an authentic leadership assignment.

A stronger move would convert the observed potential into a clear responsibility, such as owning a defined part of the shadow-student welcome process and later reflecting on the outcome.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Need → Ownership → Boundaries → Deliverable → Follow-Up process.

### 1. Identify a real need

Choose a responsibility that matters to the community.

The work should solve an actual problem, create a useful experience, or improve a real process.

### 2. Give meaningful ownership

Clarify what the student controls.

Ownership may include:
  - designing the approach
  - choosing among options
  - coordinating people
  - managing resources
  - communicating decisions

The student should do more than carry out a fully scripted adult plan.

### 3. Define boundaries and support

Make clear:
  - what the student may decide
  - what requires adult approval
  - what standards must be met
  - when the Guide will provide help

Support should make success possible without transferring ownership back to the Guide.

### 4. Establish a visible deliverable

Define what the student is responsible for producing or leading.

Examples include:
  - an event
  - a club session
  - an agenda
  - a presentation
  - a roster
  - a design
  - an improved community process

### 5. Follow up on execution

Return to the responsibility in a later meeting.

Ask what the student completed, what happened, what was difficult, and what should change next.

The standard is not:

> “The student was told they are a leader.”

The standard is:

> “The student was trusted with something real, made meaningful decisions, and remained accountable for the outcome.”
