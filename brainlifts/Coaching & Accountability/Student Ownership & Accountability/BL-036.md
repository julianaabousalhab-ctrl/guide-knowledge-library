# Pair Accountability With a Clear Repair Pathway

## Pattern

Guides strengthen accountability when they respond to a missed commitment, broken standard, or harmful choice by making both the problem and the path forward clear.

A strong repair pathway helps the student understand:
  - what specific behavior occurred
  - who or what was affected
  - why the standard matters
  - what the student must do to repair the immediate situation
  - what behavior will prevent the problem from happening again

The recurring sequence is:

Name the behavior → clarify its impact → distinguish a support need from a choice → define the repair → establish how trust or standing can be rebuilt

Accountability is weakened when the conversation ends with a warning, penalty, lost reward, or general instruction to “do better.” A consequence may still be appropriate, but the student must also understand how to return to successful participation.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide explains the integrity violation, why the standard matters, and exactly how the student can redo the work*

The Guide identifies both the incorrect assignment response and the use of ChatGPT.

> Guide: “So you're supposed to pick a perspective. You didn't do that.”

> Guide: “Also, not only did you not do that, you went on chat GPT, and you typed in, write a book report.”

The Guide makes clear that the consequence is not permanent and explains how the student can rebuild their standing.

> Guide: “Now, the good thing about this is, it's not the end of the world. You can get those taken off, By not doing it again and exhibiting good behavior. By basically following the rules.”

The immediate repair is also explicit.

> Guide: “I'm going to make you do it again. I need you to show your work.”

The Guide later explains why the academic-integrity standard matters.

> Guide: “I don't want what chat GPT thinks about a book. I want what [Student] thinks about the book.”

> Guide: “I would rather have less words and entirely your own creativity, your own perspective, because that's who I'm interested in. I'm interested in you.”

The Guide then gives the student a concrete restart point.

> Guide: “Here's your next steps. You are going to open up that email during check chart time for the mic drop. You're going to Read every line.”

The Guide also helps the student identify a valid perspective and the evidence required for the rewritten assignment.

> Guide: “I want you, [Student], to write down that, who am I, and then write what frog feels during that scene, use text evidence, quotes from the book.”

This is a strong repair pathway because the student is told:
  - what standard was broken
  - why original thinking matters
  - that the mistake can be repaired
  - how to redo the assignment
  - what future behavior will rebuild standing

*2- The Guide distinguishes lack of focus from refusal, explains the impact, and creates a way to avoid escalation*

The student explains that neither the main room nor the alternate room has consistently supported productive work.

The Guide asks the student to consider the dilemma.

> Guide: “How can I help you, if, when I put you in another room, you're distracting others, but then, if you're in our room, you're not focused. What are you expecting me to do as a guy in that situation?”

The student asks for a separate workspace.

> Student: “Have my own room.”

The Guide considers possible alternatives.

> Guide: “I can try to find a space for you, full moon is perfect, or like that, yoga room.”

> Guide: “Okay, I can look into that. We can do something like that.”

The Guide then distinguishes inconsistent attention from a more serious choice not to participate.

> Guide: “There's a difference between I'm not focused and I'm sitting here, I'm saying I'm not going to do it, and I'm moving areas, and I'm distracting other people in my class.”

The Guide names the impact of one specific action.

> Guide: “Did we shut down someone else's laptop and cause them to lose their work?”

The student confirms that the action occurred.

> Student: “Yeah.”

The Guide explains that the student is approaching a formal consequence but still has an opportunity to avoid it.

> Guide: “So I'm not giving you yellow card, but I don't want to give you yellow card either, and I'm not giving you one right now, but I want you to know that we're getting close to it, and I would really rather avoid that situation.”

The Guide later defines an immediate path back to productive participation.

> Guide: “Okay, plan for today. Let's try to get 100 XP by lunch, I'm gonna give your own place where you can work. Would that help you?”

The Guide does not treat every difficulty as misconduct. The conversation separates:
  - a need for a better workspace
  - inconsistent focus
  - refusal to work
  - behavior that affected another student

The repair pathway combines a clear warning with environmental support and an immediate opportunity to demonstrate productive behavior.

*3- The Guide names a missed commitment, explains its effect on others, and develops a prevention routine*

The Guide identifies that two scheduled coaching calls were missed.

> Guide: “Why do you think that yesterday we had two coaching calls that we missed One you didn't write down.”

The Guide acknowledges shared responsibility for the second missed call.

> Guide: “The second one, we just, we talked about it, and then we forgot about it.”

The Guide explains why the missed commitment matters beyond the individual student.

> Guide: “There's plenty of kids who need coaching calls. And I don't like us taking up the time the slot for a limited number of coaches.”

The Guide then asks the student to help create the prevention plan.

> Guide: “What are some strategies that you can do and how I can help you, do you think, so that we don't ever miss a coaching call again?”

The Guide later gives a concrete process for repairing the missed reading appointment.

> Guide: “I want you to find when on Friday, if they have any times on Friday. If not, book Monday, write it on the board, and we'll get a reading one.”

This is a repair pathway because the Guide does more than express disappointment.

The conversation includes:
  - the missed commitment
  - the Guide’s share of responsibility
  - the impact on a limited community resource
  - a replacement appointment
  - a visible reminder system intended to prevent recurrence

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is assigning a reward or penalty after identifying a problem without helping the student understand the barrier, repair the situation, or build a prevention routine.

A consequence may produce short-term compliance, but it does not automatically teach the student how to succeed differently next time.

*The Guide imposes a screen rule and an Alpha consequence without developing a repair plan*

The student reports not sleeping and says:

> Student: “Right before bed, I walked my phone.”

The Guide responds with a fixed rule.

> Guide: “You're not allowed to look at a screen for 30 minutes before bed.”

The Guide then attaches a reward and penalty.

> Guide: “And if you if you complete that, I'll give you one alpha. If you don't, I'm gonna take away one alpha.”

The rule may address a contributing behavior, but the conversation does not establish:
  - what the student will do instead of using the phone
  - how the student will remember the boundary
  - what support is needed at home
  - what should happen after a difficult night
  - how the student can repair the effect of fatigue on the next school day

The response defines a consequence but not a full repair pathway.

The student knows what can be gained or lost, but not how to rebuild the routine when the expectation is missed.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Name → Understand → Repair → Prevent → Recheck process.

### 1. Name the behavior precisely

Describe the observable action without labeling the student’s character.

Separate:
  - what the student did
  - what the student intended
  - what the outcome was

### 2. Understand the cause

Determine whether the miss resulted primarily from:
  - confusion
  - lack of support
  - forgetting
  - an environmental barrier
  - poor planning
  - refusal
  - a deliberate violation

Different causes require different responses.

Understanding the cause does not remove accountability. It ensures that the repair addresses the actual problem.

### 3. Define the immediate repair

Clarify what must now be done to address the missed commitment or harm.

The repair may include:

  - redoing work independently
  - rescheduling a missed appointment
  - restoring something affected
  - apologizing directly
  - completing the original commitment
  - demonstrating the required skill correctly

The student should know exactly what completion looks like.

### 4. Build a prevention routine

Identify what will change next time.

The prevention plan may involve:
  - a reminder
  - a calendar entry
  - a workspace change
  - a check-in
  - a help-seeking step
  - a different decision point

The student should contribute to this plan whenever possible.

### 5. Explain how trust or standing is rebuilt

Make clear what evidence will show that the student has repaired the situation.

This may be:
  - completing the redo
  - following the standard consistently
  - meeting the next commitment
  - demonstrating the replacement behavior over time

### 6. Recheck the repair

Return to the issue later.

Confirm:
  - whether the repair was completed
  - whether the prevention routine worked
  - whether additional support is needed

The standard is not:

> “The student received a consequence.”

The standard is:

> “The student understands what happened, knows how to repair it, and has a credible route back to trust and successful participation.”
