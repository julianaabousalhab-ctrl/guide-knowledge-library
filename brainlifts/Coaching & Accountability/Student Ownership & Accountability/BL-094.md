# Replace Rescue With Scheduled, Time-Boxed Support

## Pattern

When guide support arrives on demand — sitting with a struggling student for long stretches, responding to every hand — the student's output can look fine while independence quietly erodes: the adult becomes part of how the student works. Guides who broke this cycle changed the *delivery structure* of support rather than the amount of care:

1. **Scheduled check-ins instead of rescue check-ins** — support arrives at predictable, pre-announced times, not in response to distress.
2. **Time-boxed contact** — brief touchpoints (minutes, not blocks) with a return later, so the student works independently between contacts.
3. **Independence made visible** — the student can show what they completed alone before adult help, so both sides can see the ratio shifting.
4. **A stated fade** — the structure is introduced with the explicit goal of the guide stepping back as the student's independent stretches lengthen.

This is the Guide-side complement to BL-034 (the student's help-seeking routine): BL-034 teaches the student when to ask; this pattern governs how the Guide answers so that asking doesn't become the default work mode. The library's own node notes previously excluded "increase independence after a strategy works" for lack of evidence; this corpus supplies it.

## Evidence

*1- An executed system: structure first, support faded on purpose*

> "When students became frustrated or overwhelmed, they relied on adults to regulate their emotions, causing work avoidance, repeated interruptions, and reduced independence." → "I built a repeatable self-regulation system before expecting independence. Students used timers, planned reset breaks, reflection routines, and meditation strategies while I gradually faded my support over time. Rather than solving problems for them, I coached them to recognize when they needed a reset and return to learning independently."

> "I am spending about half of core skills time helping George out. He is answering questions at a high accuracy, but is earning little XP and works very slowly. The same Guide's design uses presence itself as the scheduled element: "I'll tell him that if he closes certain rings or hits certain XP benchmarks I'll come back and check his work - I'll only do this when he can EASILY complete the work on his own and is just not demonstrating the necessary independence to do it."

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is on-demand rescue as the standing support model — the adult absorbs every hard moment, and the student's results come to depend on adult presence.

*Co-regulation dependence, with the academic cost recorded*

> "When Jax's academic success depended on adult co-regulation instead of a clear independence process, hard math felt out of reach, so his growth mindset, XP production, and math growth stayed weak."

The New York record (Evidence 1) documents the same mechanism at group scale: adult-dependent regulation produced "work avoidance, repeated interruptions, and reduced independence" — and George's record (Evidence 3) shows what it looks like mid-stream: half of Core Skills spent at one student's side, high accuracy, and almost no independent output.

Source artifact:[Feathers and Fix](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1sCk-e8OcVkrhBOgjdgViKMimetv8BTBk/view?usp=drive_link)

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Announce → Box → Evidence → Fade process.

### 1. Announce the schedule

Tell the student when support will come ("I'll check on you after each ring," "check-up in 20 minutes"). Predictability replaces the comfort function of on-demand presence.

### 2. Box the contact

Keep each touchpoint short and purposeful — verify, encourage, point to the next step, leave. If a real skill gap appears, route it to the proper channel (coaching call, targeted instruction) rather than extending the visit.

### 3. Make independent work the evidence

Track what the student completed alone between check-ins (a proof-of-growth tracker, rings closed before help). Review it with the student so independence becomes the visible win.

### 4. Fade on evidence

As independent stretches lengthen, widen the check-in interval — and say so, so the student experiences the fade as promotion, not abandonment.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The Guide is always there the moment the student struggles."

The standard is:

> "The student knows exactly when support is coming, works independently between contacts, and can show what they did alone — and the interval is growing."

## Confidence: High — 3+ instances across three source types (Feathers and Fix, 360 Reviews, Motivational Models), with the dependence cost documented in the anti-pattern case.
