# Revisit the Prior Plan With Evidence and Decide What Changes 

## Pattern
Guides strengthen follow-up coaching when they return to a previously agreed plan, examine evidence of what happened, and deliberately decide whether to keep, revise, replace, or stop the plan.

A strong follow-up answers:
  - What did the student agree to try?
  - Did the student actually try it?
  - What evidence shows what happened?
  - Which part helped?
  - Which part did not help?
  - What should remain the same?
  - What should change next?

The recurring sequence is:

Restate the prior plan → inspect implementation and results → interpret the evidence with the student → decide what to retain or change → establish the revised plan

Follow-up is not merely asking, “How is it going?” It requires connecting the previous commitment to observable evidence and using that evidence to make a decision.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide checks whether the previous subject-order plan helped and changes it when the evidence shows a negative tradeoff*

The Guide explicitly returns to the plan from the previous conversation.

> Guide: “So last time we talked, we decided to do the hardest things first.”

The Guide checks whether the student followed the plan and whether it helped.

> Guide: “Have you been doing that this week? You've been doing Lililo first? Is that helping you?”

The student reports that the plan has not produced the intended result.

> Student: “Not that much.”

The student identifies the negative effect.

> Student: “It's just making fast math go down.”

The Guide does not defend the original plan or tell the student to continue longer without evidence. The Guide invites revision.

> Guide: “Okay, so what can we do to change our plan then if the Lelilo first isn't working?”

The student proposes a new order based on something that worked that day.

> Student: “Maybe I could do Lelilo second because I did that today and it worked out.”

The Guide confirms the revised plan.

> Guide: “And it worked out really well? Okay, so for this next week we want to do Lelilo second.”

This is strong follow-up because the Guide:
  - restates the earlier strategy
  - checks whether it was implemented
  - asks whether it helped
  - identifies an unintended cost
  - changes the plan using the student’s recent experience as evidence

*2- The Guide compares accuracy before and after the support plan, then shifts additional coaching toward the subject that still needs it*

The Guide revisits why a math coaching call had been scheduled.

> Guide: “One of the reasons I had you do a math coaching call was math was something that you were going between like a 70 and 80% for accuracy last week.”

The Guide then compares that baseline with current evidence.

> Guide: “Well, let me tell you, it's it's working because look at that big 100% you had yesterday, yeah, and look at this 89% from today. So it's working.”

The Guide checks the next scheduled coaching call and discovers that the student has another math call but no reading call.

> Guide: “And do you have another coaching call this week?”

> Student: “Tomorrow, same time.”

> Guide: “And what's that one on math? That one's also math?”

The Guide revises the support plan based on the current subject-level evidence.

> Guide: “Are you okay keeping that math one tomorrow and scheduling a reading one for the day after okay?”

The Guide explains the evidence behind the change.

> Guide: “Math and reading last week were the two subjects that you were just struggling a little bit with an accuracy, and they're better this week.”

The Guide then identifies which subject still needs additional support.

> Guide: “They're both better this week, but reading, I would say, is still the one that's between 70 and 80% so I want to help you get that up.”

The Guide does not repeat the same support distribution automatically. The decision changes because:
  - math accuracy improved
  - reading accuracy remained lower
  - the next coaching call could therefore be redirected toward the remaining need

*3- The Guide inspects the result of a previously taught strategy and decides that the student should continue using it*

The Guide returns to previously established CoreSkills plans.

> Guide: “You have been doing fantastic with those CoreSkills. You've been following all those plans from Lilo and FastMath, using those number keys.”

The Guide then references a specific strategy observed the previous day.

> Guide: “And what about I noticed yesterday that you had a sheet of paper beside you with a lot of different like things that you wrote. Tell me about that.”

The student explains where the notes were used.

> Student: “Yeah, I used it on the tower of power.”

The Guide checks whether the strategy was used beyond one task.

> Guide: “Oh, just the tower of power?”

> Student: “And the other lessons to get past the tower.”

The Guide asks for the result.

> Guide: “And then what happened?”

> Student: “I got two tower power.”

The Guide uses the result to decide whether the strategy should be retained.

> Guide: “Oh my gosh. Fantastic. So, do you plan on doing that again?”

> Student: “Yeah.”

This example shows that follow-up can result in keeping a plan, not only changing it.

The evidence indicates that:
  - the student used written notes
  - the strategy supported progress through multiple lessons
  - the student completed two Tower of Power tasks

Because the strategy produced a useful result, the decision is to continue it.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is mentioning the prior plan, discovering that it was not completed, and simply postponing it without examining why it failed or changing the conditions for the next attempt.

A yes-or-no completion check is not enough. When the plan was not implemented, the Guide still needs evidence about the barrier before repeating it.

*The prior plan is deferred to the next week without investigating why the student forgot it*

The Guide returns to a plan discussed the previous day.

> Guide: “Did you try designing the architecture workshop the way we talked about yesterday.”

The student responds:

> Student: “No.”

The Guide checks whether the plan was forgotten.

> Guide: “Do you forget about that?”

> Student: “Yeah.”

The Guide then postpones the same plan.

> Guide: “That's okay. We'll do it next week. I'll remind you next week.”

The response is supportive, but the follow-up does not examine:
  - why the student forgot
  - whether the plan was written down
  - whether the student understood the task
  - when the design work was supposed to happen
  - hat should change so the next attempt is more likely to occur

The only revision is that the Guide will remind the student later.

A stronger follow-up would use the missed implementation as evidence that the original reminder or execution plan was insufficient.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Recall → Verify → Interpret → Decide → Reset process.

### 1. Recall the prior plan

State the previous agreement precisely.

Include:
  - what the student intended to try
  - when it was supposed to happen
  - what result the plan was intended to produce

Do not rely on vague references such as “the thing we talked about.”

### 2. Verify implementation and results

Check two separate questions:
  - Did the student try the plan?
  - What happened when they tried it?

Use available evidence such as:
  - accuracy
  - XP
  - completed work
  - student notes
  - scheduling records
  - direct observation
  - the student’s description of a specific attempt

A plan cannot be evaluated fairly if it was never implemented.

### 3. Interpret the evidence with the student

Determine whether:
  - the strategy helped
  - the plan was only partially followed
  - the plan created an unintended problem
  - the conditions were not right
  - the result is still unclear

Avoid deciding from one number without understanding what produced it.

### 4. Make an explicit decision

Choose one of four responses:
**Keep it:** The plan worked and should continue.
**Refine it:** Part worked, but one condition needs adjustment.
**Replace it:** The approach did not address the real barrier.
**Pause it:** More evidence or a different prerequisite is needed first.

State the decision clearly so the student understands what has changed.

### 5. Reset the revised plan

Define the next trial:
  - what the student will do
  - when they will do it
  - what evidence will be reviewed
  - when the Guide and student will revisit it

The standard is not:

> “The Guide remembered the previous conversation.”

The standard is:

> “The Guide used evidence from the previous plan to make the next plan better.”
