# Track Guide and System Commitments to Closure

## Pattern
Guides strengthen follow-up coaching when they treat actions owned by the Guide or a broader system as trackable commitments rather than informal promises.

These commitments may include:
  - contacting an academic or application team
  - requesting a test, course, or coaching call
  - correcting missing XP
  - investigating an application problem
  - obtaining a decision from another adult
  - providing a resource the student was promised

The recurring sequence is:

Name the commitment → record or complete the Guide-owned action → check the external status → update the student → continue following up until Guide-owned action → check the external status → update the student → continue following up until there is a final outcome or an explicit interim resolution

Closure does not always mean the student receives the outcome they requested. A commitment is closed when:
  - the request is completed
  - the system gives a clear final decision
  - an alternative is implemented
  - the student receives a transparent status, interim protection, and a defined next action while the external issue remains open

The Guide should distinguish between:
  - the Guide’s action: sending the request, documenting evidence, following up, or correcting a temporary record
  - the system’s action: approving, fixing, grading, assigning, or issuing a final decision

## Evidence

*1- The Guide follows up until a final system decision is received and communicates the available alternative*

The student asks about gaining access to a different application.

> Student: “Can I try the Astro Mask thing.”

The Guide explains that the system initially suggested the request might be possible.

> Guide: “They suggested they might be able to get it for you.”

The Guide then reports the result of the follow-up.

> Guide: “And then when I followed up, they said we can't give it.”

The Guide explains the likely system reason.

> Guide: “I think they might be also phasing it out.”

The Guide then communicates the available alternative and the information received from the academic team.

> Guide: “But actually [academic lead], who's our head academic person, said she feels like Math Raiders is the best fast math app. So you're actually in the best one.”

The student’s request does not disappear after the initial conversation.

The Guide:
  - follows up with the responsible system
  - receives a final answer
  - communicates that answer to the student
  - explains the likely reason
  - identifies the current alternative

The requested outcome was denied, but the commitment still reached closure because the student received a clear decision rather than an indefinite “we are looking into it.”

*2- The Guide repeatedly checks an unresolved XP issue and protects the student from the delay*

The student reports that a completed reading test still has not been credited.

The Guide checks the tracking system.

> Guide: “And no, it still has not tracked.”

The Guide then identifies the Guide-owned action.

> Guide: “So last week's reading did not track for you, so I'm going to send out a message.”

The Guide reviews the previous communication and shows that another follow-up has already been sent.

> Guide: “And then I said hi. Has there been any update on this?”

The Guide communicates the current status honestly.

> Guide: “So we don't have any answer for that.”

Rather than allowing the unresolved system delay to continue harming the student’s record, the Guide creates an interim correction.

> Guide: “Okay, we'll do 60.”

The Guide records the reason for that correction.

> Guide: “Added 60 back to tracker because academics has not respond.”

The system issue has not reached final technical resolution, but the Guide does not treat “waiting” as the end of the responsibility.

The Guide:
  - checks whether the credit appeared
  - confirms it is still missing
  - follows up with the academic team
  - communicates that there is no final answer yet
  - documents the status
  - applies an interim adjustment so the student is not penalized while waiting

This is strong commitment tracking because the Guide keeps both the external request and the student-facing impact visible.

*3- The Guide completes the Guide-owned escalation during the meeting instead of leaving it*

The student reports that part of an application is blocked.

> Student: “Connected was the one. Yeah, connected was the one that was blocked.”

The Guide chooses to act immediately.

> Guide: “I'm going to reach out right now so I don't forget.”

The Guide explains the personal tracking strategy used to prevent commitments from being lost.

> Guide: “When you tell me to do something, I do it, but I have I have to do it like, either I have to write it down and do it later, or I have to do it right then and there.”

The Guide confirms:

> Guide: “So, I'm doing it right now.”

The transcript then shows the Guide beginning the message.

> Guide: “Okay, [system contact].”

The external system may still need time to respond, but the Guide-owned portion of the commitment is not left as “I will remember later.”

The Guide closes the immediate action by contacting the responsible person during the meeting.

This evidence demonstrates an important part of the pattern:

When the Guide can complete their part immediately, they do not create an unnecessary open loop.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is allowing an unresolved request to close, expire, or disappear because no one is consistently tracking the next follow-up.

Repeated messages alone do not equal closure. The issue needs:
  - a named owner
  - a visible current status
  - a next follow-up point
  - confirmation that the problem was actually resolved before the request is closed

*A system ticket closes because follow-up stops even though the underlying problem remains*

The student explains that multiple messages were sent.

> Student: “Because I sent them a, like, five emails.”

The student then explains why the request disappeared.

> Student: “Because I forgot to respond to the email, and if you stop responding to the email, it closes?”

The student describes the system assumption.

> Student: “If you don't follow up for a week, then it closes, and they believe that the problem's solved.”

The Guide asks whether unresolved issues remain.

> Guide: “So do you think that there's things that are unresolved that could be fixed, or are we just kind of...”

The student confirms that the issue remains unresolved and that the communication trail has been lost.

> Student: “It can't actually be fixed, you know why? Because I don't know where the email ended.”

> Student: “And if I follow up right now, they probably won't respond.”

The problem is not a lack of initial effort. Several emails were sent.

The failure is that the request was not tracked through its full lifecycle:
  - the system was still working on the issue
  - no one maintained the response cadence
  - the ticket closed automatically
  - the system treated silence as resolution
  - the student no longer knew how to restart the request

A stronger approach would place the open issue in a Guide-owned tracker with a next follow-up date and require confirmation from the student before the ticket is considered resolved.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Own → Record → Follow Up → Update → Close process.

### 1. Own the commitment precisely

State who is responsible for the next action.

Separate:
  - what the student will do
  - what the Guide will do
  - what an external team must do

Avoid vague language such as:

> “We’ll look into it.”

A clear commitment identifies the action and owner.

### 2. Record the commitment

Place the action somewhere it can be revisited.

The record should include:
  - the issue
  - the student impact
  - the responsible person or team
  - the date submitted
  - the next follow-up date

When possible, complete simple Guide-owned actions during the meeting.

### 3. Follow up until the external status changes

Do not assume that sending one message completes the responsibility.

Check whether the request is:
  - received
  - in progress
  - waiting for information
  - resolved
  - denied
  - closed without resolution

If the external team needs more evidence, gather and send it.

### 4. Update the student

Tell the student what has happened, even when there is no final answer yet.

A useful update distinguishes between:
  - what has been completed
  - what is still waiting
  - who currently owns the issue
  - what will happen next

Silence can make students believe the Guide forgot.

### 5. Protect the student while waiting

When appropriate and authorized, create an interim response so the student is not unfairly affected by the delay.

Examples include:
  - documenting completed work
  - correcting a temporary tracker
  - providing an alternate resource
  - creating a temporary learning pathway

The interim response should not falsely imply that the system issue itself is resolved.

### 6. Close deliberately

Close the commitment only when one of the following is true:
  - the requested action was completed
  - the problem was fixed
  - a clear final decision was communicated
  - an agreed alternative was implemented
  - the student confirmed that no further action is needed
    
Record the final outcome so the issue does not reopen through confusion.

The standard is not:

> “The Guide sent a message.”

The standard is:

> “The student knows the final outcome, or knows exactly what remains open, who owns it, and what will happen next.”
