# Break the Goal Into the Next Sequenced Step

## Pattern

Guides strengthen action planning when they help the student identify the immediate step that logically comes next instead of leaving the student with only a distant outcome.

The next step should:
  - move the student closer to the larger goal
  - occur before later stages of the plan
  - be small enough to begin
  - resolve the most immediate dependency

The recurring sequence is:

Name the larger goal → identify what must happen before it → determine what is already complete → select the next unfinished step → begin there

This pattern is about sequence, not merely making the goal smaller. The Guide helps the student avoid working on a later stage before completing the step that makes that stage possible.

A next step may involve:
  - researching a tool
  - drafting one section
  - selecting a resource
  - creating a storyboard
  - identifying an ending
  - contacting one person
  - completing the first prerequisite

## Evidence

*1- The Guide turns a long-term apparel-business vision into one task the student can complete that day*

The Guide asks what the student is working toward with the apparel company.

> Guide: “What are we doing this week with the jerseys and the apparel? What kind of goals?”

The student identifies an immediate need: finding an application that can support the jersey-design process.

> Student: “I want to find an app that creates, that you can choose your own design and you can choose the size and you can basically throwing the jersey into canvas.”

The Guide checks that this is the next step.

> Guide: “So that's your next step?”

The student confirms.

The Guide then converts it into a same-day action.

> Guide: “Why don't you make that one of your daily goals? Find that app today, and that way you can take the next step with your business.”

The larger vision involves designing jerseys for a future event. The Guide does not ask the student to complete the whole business plan at once. The immediate dependency is finding a tool that can create and size the designs.

The sequence becomes:
  - find the design application
  - use it to create the jerseys
  - move the apparel business toward production.

*2- The Guide works backward from publishing a book until the student identifies the next writing step*

The Guide asks the student to identify a starting point for completing the book’s illustrations.

> Guide: “What would be a good starting point for you to figure out how to get the illustrations done?”

The student asks for clarification.

> Student: “Starting point?”

The Guide explains that the goal should be worked backward into prerequisite stages.

> Guide: “What's an easy first step actionable plan that you could have that would get you closer to fully illustrating.”

> Guide: “So if your end goal, if our end goal is a finished book that you can see on bookshelves, how do we walk back each phase of that.”

The student identifies one illustration step.

> Student: “Before I do the illustrations I would need to like sketch it on a piece of paper.”

The Guide then asks whether the written chapters or illustrations should come first.

> Guide: “Do you want to finish all of the written chapters first and then do illustrations or do you want to do it at the same time?”

The student chooses the sequence.

> Student: “I'd rather do all the finished chapters first because I might have a brand new idea.”

The Guide clarifies the immediate priority.

> Guide: “If that's the case, Your main priority, your main objective should be finishing the book.”

The Guide then identifies the next dependency within the writing process.

> Guide: “You have to have an ending in mind. I think you should figure out how you want to end the book. And then work backwards from there.”

The student says the book will end with a cliffhanger, and the Guide proposes the immediate step.

> Guide: “Without telling me what it is yet, maybe you write that first, and then you can work your way back from there.”

The Guide prevents the student from beginning illustrations before the story is stable. The sequence becomes:
  - determine and write the ending
  - complete the written chapters
  - sketch the illustrations
  - create the finished illustrations
  - move toward publishing.

*3- The Guide identifies the storyboard as the next unfinished step in a writing project*

The Guide checks the student’s progress on writing a myth.

> Guide: “Writing your own myth. Any steps on that one yet?”

The student explains that part of the story has already been written.

> Student: “Yeah, I sent you what I already wrote.”

The Guide checks whether the characters have been created.

> Guide: “Have you created the characters?”

The student confirms that they are already included in the writing.

The Guide then checks the next planning component.

> Guide: “Do you have a storyboard?”

The student responds:

> Student: “I'll make one. That's the next step. No problem.”

The Guide confirms that the earlier work is already complete.

> Guide: “Sounds good. I mean, hey, if your step one's already done, you're happy with what you've got.”

The Guide does not restart the project or give the student a list of every remaining task. The conversation identifies what has already been completed and selects the next unfinished prerequisite: creating the storyboard.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is setting a credible long-term goal but ending the conversation before identifying what the student will do first.

A clear outcome and deadline are not yet an action plan. Without an immediate step, the student may understand where they are going but not how to begin.

*The Guide sets an end-of-year Fast Math goal but does not identify the student’s next action*

The student identifies a goal related to multiplication and division.

> Student: “Get to fast math division.”

The Guide asks how long the development might take.

> Guide: “How long do you think it like let's let's put a number of like weeks or months on it.”

The student estimates:

> Student: “Like three months.”

The Guide expands the timeline and creates an end-of-year outcome.

> Guide: “If it takes three, four, five, even six months to get good at it, we could probably push you to finishing it by the end of the year because that's eight months.”

The Guide summarizes the goal.

> Guide: “Get good at the um multiplication, multiple and division um fast math and then finishing fast math.”

The goal is ambitious and time-bound, but the conversation then moves to the student’s academic statistics.

Within the available exchange, the Guide does not identify:
  - which operation the student should work on first
  - what practice should happen next
  - what current lesson or level begins the sequence
  - what the student will do that day or week

The student leaves with a destination—finish Fast Math by the end of the year—but without a first executable step.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Outcome → Dependencies → Completed Work → Next Step process.

### 1. Name the outcome

Clarify what the student ultimately wants to produce or accomplish.

Keep the larger goal visible so the next step has meaning.

### 2. Identify the dependencies

Ask what must be true immediately before the final outcome can occur.

Then continue working backward until the plan reaches something the student can begin now.

For example:
  - before publishing, the book must be finished
  - before illustrations are finalized, the story must be stable
  - before the story is stable, the ending must be decided
    
### 3. Remove steps that are already complete

Check the student’s current progress.

Do not assign the student to redo planning or work that is already finished unless revision is genuinely necessary.

### 4. Select one next unfinished step

The next step should be:
  - necessary
  - logically ordered
  - small enough to begin
  - connected to the larger goal

Avoid giving the student an unranked list of everything remaining.

### 5. Confirm the sequence

Ask the student to explain why this step comes before the later stages.

The student should understand not only what to do next, but why it is next.

### 6. Stop planning when the student can begin

Do not overbuild the plan when the immediate action is already clear.

The standard is not:

> “The student knows the final goal.”

The standard is:

> “The student knows the next unfinished step that unlocks the rest of the plan.”
