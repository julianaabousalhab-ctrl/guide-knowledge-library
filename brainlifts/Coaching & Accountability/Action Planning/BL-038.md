# Define the Execution Conditions and Evidence of Completion

## Pattern

Guides strengthen action planning when they define the conditions under which the student will carry out the plan and the evidence that will show the action is complete.

A clear plan answers the relevant execution questions:
  - What will the student do?
  - When will they do it?
  - Where will it happen?
  - For how long or how often?
  - With whom or with what support?
  - What evidence will prove completion?

The recurring sequence is:

Identify the action → define the execution conditions → clarify what completion looks like → confirm the student can begin

This pattern differs from breaking the goal into the next sequenced step. Sequencing identifies what comes next. Execution conditions define how that next step will actually happen and how everyone will know it was completed.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide defines when the student will begin, what must be completed, and what evidence is required before moving forward*

The Guide first defines when the student will begin the Plan check.

> Guide: “We’re gonna have you start that today. That’s the first thing you can do during check chart time.”

The Guide makes the order explicit.

> Guide: “Before you do typing test, I want you to do that one.”

The Guide then explains that the student must follow the document in sequence.

> Guide: “You have to follow this document, step by step. Exactly what it says.”

The document itself defines the evidence required before the student can continue.

> Guide: “Once finished with the video and guided note sheet, show your document to a guide before moving to creating your budget.”

The Guide points to the visible completion marker.

> Guide: “Do you see where it says guide signature for completion.”

The Guide restates the threshold.

> Guide: “You have to do the document from the top all the way until here, and then you have to show us that spot before you get to the next section.”

The execution conditions are clear:
  - begin that day
  - work during check-chart time
  - complete it before the typing test
  - follow the document step by step
  - finish the video and guided notes
  - obtain a Guide signature before advancing

Completion is not based on the student saying they are done. The completed document and Guide signature provide visible evidence.

*2- The Guide defines the work period and has the student state every remaining completion requirement*

The student identifies the goal for that day.

> Student: “I can use my check chart planner to organize my steps and completely one check. I haven’t done that one yet, but I’m going to finish that.”

The Guide defines when the plan will be executed.

> Guide: “Because we still have check chart time today.”

The Guide then reviews the exact completion requirements.

> Guide: “Okay, what is your check?”

The student reads the requirements.

> Student: “Complete page of quiz for each chapter, then pass the cumulative quiz and reflection using this GPT.”

The Guide checks what remains unfinished.

> Guide: “What else do you need to do today in order to make that check complete?”

> Student: “Finish the GPT and the cumulative test.”

The Guide notices that some chapter quizzes are also incomplete.

> Student: “I think I have to do like two more or three more.”

The Guide asks the student to state the final execution plan.

> Guide: “Okay, so what’s your plan for today?”

> Student: “Finish quizzes and cumulative test and reflection.”

The Guide confirms the work period and tool.

> Guide: “Make sure we’re using that planner.”

> Guide: “And so we just got to execute it during check chart time.”

The plan defines both the conditions and completion evidence:
  - work during that day’s check-chart period
  - use the planner
  - finish all remaining chapter quizzes
  - pass the cumulative test
  - complete the GPT reflection

The check is complete only when each named requirement is finished.

*3- The Guide specifies the time, duration, people, setting, and success target for a performance attempt*

The student says they want to work on a physical check.

> Student: “My mom wants me to practice the workout today.”

The Guide asks when the action will occur.

> Guide: “Do you want to do that today? When should we do that today?”

> Student: “Check chart.”

The Guide confirms the time and clarifies whether the student is practicing or attempting to pass.

> Guide: “So during check chart time today, you want to do the workout? Do you want to just practice or do you want to like really try to pass it?”

> Student: “Really try.”

The conversation then defines the approximate duration.

> Student: “I want to cut a little in workshops so I have enough time.”

> Guide: “Okay, that makes sense. So, like 20 minutes in the workshop to do the workout.”

The Guide asks who should participate.

> Guide: “Which person do you want to do the workout with?”

> Student: “I think [peer] could do it.”

The Guide clarifies how permission and support will work.

> Guide: “First I’m going to ask [adult] if it’s okay and then if she says yes, you are gonna ask [peer].”

The Guide also defines the environment and safety condition.

> Guide: “So you want to do it alone? The workout? You want to do it just me and you and [peer]?”

> Guide: “Because we were not really safe with [peer’s] body last time, I think we probably need an adult there this time.”

The execution conditions include:
  - that day
  - during check-chart time
  - approximately 20 minutes
  - with a named peer
  - with adult approval and supervision
  - in a lower-audience setting
  - as an attempt to pass rather than only practice

The pass attempt provides the completion evidence: the student either meets the workout standard or receives information about what still needs work.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is assigning a broad action without defining when it will happen, what the student must actually do, or what evidence will show that the action occurred.

A direction such as “be a leader,” “help others,” or “work harder” may communicate a positive expectation, but it is not yet executable or verifiable.

*The Guide encourages broader leadership behavior without defining an execution condition or completion evidence*

The Guide recognizes the student as a classroom leader.

> Guide: “You continue to emerge as a leader in the classroom.”

The Guide then encourages the student to extend supportive behavior to others.

> Guide: “You do a good job taking them under your wing. So like introducing, making them feel comfortable. Do that with other students too.”

The expectation is positive, but the plan does not define:
  - which students the student should support
  - when the interaction should occur
  - what specific action the student should take
  - how often it should happen
  - what would count as evidence that the student followed through

“Do that with other students too” describes a general intention, not an executable plan.

A stronger version would define a real occasion, behavior, and observable result—for example, welcoming the next shadow student, showing them two key spaces, introducing them to two classmates, and checking afterward whether they felt included.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the What → Conditions → Proof → Confirm process.

### 1. Define what the student will do

Use an observable action.

Replace vague verbs such as:
  - improve
  - focus
  - help
  - prepare
  - be responsible

with the exact behavior the student will perform.

### 2. Define the execution conditions

Clarify the relevant details:
  - when
  - where
  - for how long
  - how often
  - in what order
  - with whom
  - with what resources or support

Not every plan needs every condition, but enough must be specified for the student to begin without guessing.

### 3. Define the proof of completion

Identify what will show that the action is finished.

Evidence may include:
  - a completed document
  - a Guide signature
  - a submitted form
  - a passed test
  - a scheduled appointment
  - a recorded score
  - a visible deliverable
  - an observed behavior

Avoid relying only on “I did it” when stronger evidence is available.

### 4. Confirm the student understands

Ask the student to restate:
  - what they will do
  - when they will do it
  - what will count as complete

This reveals missing conditions before execution begins.

### 5. Keep the evidence proportional

The proof should be strong enough to verify the action without turning the plan into unnecessary surveillance or paperwork.

The standard is not:

> “The student knows what they should generally work on.”

The standard is:

> “The student knows exactly how the action will happen and what evidence will show that it is complete.”
