# Surface a Personally Meaningful Goal Distinct From Required Performance Targets

## Pattern
Guides strengthen goal discovery when they help the student identify something the student genuinely wants to develop, accomplish, experience, or become beyond required performance targets.

Required targets may include:
  - XP totals
  - check completion
  - application levels
  - grade-level advancement
  - attendance expectations
  - qualification for a school reward

These targets can still matter, but they do not automatically reveal what is personally meaningful to the student.

A personally meaningful goal may involve:
  - independence
  - confidence
  - a relationship
  - a creative or athletic skill
  - a future aspiration
  - a habit the student wants to build
  - an academic interest the student has chosen for themselves

The recurring sequence is:

Ask broadly about what the student wants → continue beyond the first required target → explore why it matters → identify a goal the student recognizes as their own

The Guide does not need to reject performance targets. The Guide makes sure the conversation does not stop there.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide explicitly opens goal discovery to school and non-school aspirations*

The Guide uses an open-ended prompt to help the student imagine the end of the session.

> Guide: “Okay, so let's pretend we one you had a magic wand yeah and you could fast forward to the end of session one what do you hope you accomplished”

The student initially responds generally.

> Student: “All my goals.”

The Guide asks the student to define those goals rather than supplying them.

> Guide: “And what are your goals”

The Guide makes clear that the goals can be developed collaboratively and do not need to be finalized immediately.

> Guide: “We can make them right now we can brainstorm.”

The student identifies a personally chosen creative goal.

> Student: “I want to be able to do high notes.”

The student then identifies a personal-development goal.

> Student: “I also want to try to be more mature.”

The Guide asks what maturity means to the student.

> Guide: “Mature how?”

The student explains:

> Student: “Like I always want her to come with me places okay so I want to like get better at that but it's really hard”

The Guide recognizes that the goal was self-identified rather than assigned.

> Guide: “I think that's a really cool goal because no one asked you to do that right but you noticed about yourself like hey this is something I can get better at and you set it for yourself”

This is strong evidence because the Guide creates room for goals related to music, maturity, and independence rather than restricting the conversation to school metrics.

*2- The Guide continues asking after the student first names an XP target*

The Guide begins with a broad question.

> Guide: “What do you want out of session two?”

The student first identifies a performance target.

> Student: “At least 100 XP every day.”

The Guide acknowledges the answer but does not allow XP to become the entire goal conversation.

> Guide: “What else? What else would you like out of this session.”

The student identifies a personally meaningful habit goal.

> Student: “I want to, like, clean up my stuff more.”

The student explains the current behavior.

> Student: “Because I'm like, I'm always leaving, like, wrappers around right? My cubbies usually messed up.”

The Guide recognizes the goal.

> Guide: “I think that's a fantastic goal.”

The Guide continues looking for goals beyond the first two answers.

> Guide: “Anything else?”

The student then identifies a social-development goal connected to how they interact with other people.

> Student: “I want to work on that too.”

The Guide connects the answer to an earlier conversation.

> Guide: “We talked about just how we're interacting with other people, right? I think that's a really good one.”

The Guide surfaces multiple student-owned goals by continuing beyond the required XP target:
  - maintaining an organized space
  - improving personal habits
  - interacting more effectively with others

*3- The Guide separates academic, growth, and personal goals before asking what the student wants to achieve*

The Guide establishes that goal setting will include more than academics.

> Guide: “I have three different goals that I want you to think about and set.”

> Guide: “The first is an academic goal, the second is a growth goal and the third is a personal goal.”

The Guide gives the student choice over where to begin.

> Guide: “You can start with anyone that you want.”

The Guide then asks about the student’s personal goal.

> Guide: “What's your personal goal?”

The Guide clarifies the type of thinking they are inviting.

> Guide: “What's going to help you? Be the person you want to be.”

> Guide: “What do you want to achieve?”

The student identifies an athletic aspiration.

> Student: “I want to achieve to be the achieve, to be the best tennis player.”

The Guide recognizes the goal and begins exploring how the student might pursue it.

> Guide: “How do you think we can get you to be the best tennis player?”

The student identifies personally meaningful actions and beliefs.

> Student: “By practicing a lot and by trying your hardest”

> Student: “Believing in myself”

The Guide’s structure prevents the student’s goal-setting experience from being limited to an academic target. The student is invited to identify an aspiration that matters outside the required school metrics.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is treating a performance metric, internal currency total, or reward purchase as the full goal without exploring what the student personally wants to develop or become.

Performance targets can support a larger goal, but they should not automatically replace goal discovery.

*Goal setting remains centered on Alpha accumulation and a reward item*

The Guide recommends that the student set a goal.

> Guide: “I would recommend you picking a goal because you You are very goal-oriented.”

The Guide then supplies an example focused on internal currency.

> Guide: “I want to have, I want to get 100 alphas by the end of Thursday.”

The Guide encourages the student to report these targets for accountability.

> Guide: “Set these goals, tell them to me so I can hold you accountable”

Later, the Guide connects the goal to purchasing an item.

> Guide: “Pick something up for Emporium.”

> Guide: “Something big that you can, like, pick something out that you want to get alphas to achieve.”

> Guide: “I want it to be a goal of yours that you see every day.”

The goal is concrete, but the conversation remains centered on:
  - accumulating Alphas
  - reaching a short-term numerical target
  - purchasing a reward

Within this exchange, the Guide does not explore a broader aspiration, habit, skill, relationship, or area of personal growth that the student wants to develop.

The performance target may still be useful, but it has not yet become a personally meaningful growth goal.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Open → Continue → Deepen → Distinguish process.

### 1. Open beyond required metrics

Begin with a question broad enough to include:
  - school
  - life outside school
  - relationships
  - interests
  - habits
  - skills
  - future aspirations
    
Do not begin by limiting the student to XP, checks, or application levels.

#### 2. Continue after the first answer

Students may first name the target they believe adults expect to hear.

When the first answer is a required metric, acknowledge it and continue exploring.

The Guide should not assume the first answer is the student’s most meaningful goal.

### 3. Deepen the student’s answer

Ask the student to explain:
  - why the goal matters
  - what would change if they achieved it
  - how they would feel
  - whether the goal was chosen by them or assigned by someone else

A goal becomes more personally meaningful when the student can explain its significance.

### 4. Distinguish the goal from the measurement

Separate what the student wants from how progress will be measured.

For example:
  - XP may measure consistency, but consistency is the growth goal
  - a reading level may measure progress, but becoming a confident reader may be the meaningful goal
  - a typing score may measure skill, but working independently may be the larger goal
    
### 5. Preserve both types of goals

A student can hold:
  - required performance targets
  - personally meaningful goals

The coaching standard is not to remove required targets.

The standard is:

The student leaves with at least one goal that feels personally chosen and meaningful—not merely assigned, measured, or rewarded.
