# Calibrate the Goal So It Is Ambitious, Specific, and Credible

## Pattern
Guides strengthen goal setting when they help the student turn a broad aspiration into a target that is simultaneously:
  - ambitious: it requires meaningful growth
  - specific: the student can clearly describe what success looks like
  - credible: the target is grounded in the student’s current ability, available time, resources, and willingness to practice

A goal that is ambitious but not credible can feel imaginary. A goal that is credible but not ambitious may require little growth. A goal that is ambitious and credible but not specific cannot be tracked or evaluated.

The recurring sequence is:

Identify the aspiration → establish the current starting point → define a measurable target and deadline → test whether the target requires growth and remains achievable

The Guide should not decide credibility through optimism alone. The calibration should use evidence such as:
  - current performance
  - prior progress
  - demonstrated consistency
  - available practice time
  - the student’s ability to explain how they will pursue the goal

## Evidence

*1- The Guide turns “getting good at basketball” into a measurable end-of-year challenge grounded in the student’s baseline*

The student identifies an interest in playing basketball.

> Student: “Women’s basketball.”

The Guide distinguishes joining a team from developing a meaningful level of skill.

> Guide: “Playing on a team is not hard to do. Getting good can be hard, right?”

The Guide asks how improvement could be measured.

> Guide: “What’s something that we can like measure how good you’ve gotten?”

The student first suggests tracking calories. The Guide explains why that measure does not directly represent basketball skill.

> Guide: “The hard part about that is you can burn a lot of calories without playing any basketball.”

The Guide then proposes a basketball-specific target.

> Guide: “I wonder if we could do a challenge that by the end of the year you can hit 9 out of 10 free throw shots or something like that.”

The student raises the target.

> Student: “And I can do 10 in a row.”

The Guide accepts the student’s more ambitious version.

> Guide: “You can do 10 in a row? Ooh, I like that. 10 in a row sounds great. That’s a good challenge.”

The student identifies the current baseline.

> Student: “I can only do 4 in a row.”

The Guide frames the gap as difficult but developable.

> Guide: “Well, not yet. Yeah. But you could practice and get better.”

The final goal is:
  - specific: make 10 shots in a row
  - time-bound: by the end of the year
  - ambitious: more than double the current streak of four
  - credible: the student already plays regularly and can currently make four consecutively.

The Guide also replaces a weak measurement—calories—with one directly connected to the skill the student wants to improve

*2- The Guide explicitly teaches that a credible goal must be challenging without becoming impossible*

The Guide explains how achievability and challenge must coexist.

> Guide: “We said that our goals should be achievable. They should be realistic, but also challenging, right?”

The Guide clarifies that the goal should require growth without being impossible at the student’s current level.

> Guide: “We want to make goals that are possible for us to achieve, but will also challenge us, not be easy.”

The student supplies an example of an unrealistic starting target.

> Student: “Not like make 100 baskets in a row right when you start. That was not going to be possible.”

The Guide agrees.

> Guide: “Exactly.”

The student then describes progressively harder targets.

> Student: “When you start, I would try to do two in a row, then six in a row.”

The Guide reinforces the calibration process.

> Guide: “And then you can grow it, right.”

Later in the same meeting, the Guide applies this reasoning to a concrete academic goal. The Guide identifies the daily target and grounds it in recent evidence.

> Guide: “My challenge for you and the goal that we are setting is going to be to get two Tower of Powers with no Tower of Power alerts.”

> Guide: “Each day.”

The Guide establishes credibility by referring to recent performance.

> Guide: “And you’ve done it for the past two days, which is awesome.”

The target is specific and repeatable, while the multiweek consistency requirement makes it more demanding than a one-time success.

The Guide does not lower the goal to something effortless or raise it to something unsupported. The target stretches demonstrated ability into sustained performance.

*3- The Guide uses prior academic growth to set a specific stretch goal for the end of the year*

The student describes rapid progress in reading.

> Student: “I used to be in first grade reading but then I passed the test and then I passed the second grade test with a perfect score and then now I’m in third grade reading.”

The Guide asks the student to interpret what the progress suggests about their capability.

> Guide: “What does that teach you about just the that you’re capable of?”

The Guide then asks the student to choose a future level.

> Guide: “If you had to pick, so if you’re at third right now, right? And if you had to pick a goal, what grade do you want to be in by session five for reading?”

The student considers two possible targets.

> Student: “Maybe fifth grade or fourth grade.”

The Guide selects the more ambitious target and makes it precise.

> Guide: “I think fifth grade.”

> Guide: “I want you on by session five to be in fifth grade reading, okay?”

The Guide then tests the route to the goal.

> Guide: “What do you think we can do to get there? What steps can we take? What steps can you take specifically?”

The student identifies strategies.

> Student: “Like, maybe, like, read carefully.”

> Student: “And maybe double check your answers. Make sure you interpret the question correctly.”

The goal requires significant additional progress, but it is connected to evidence that the student has already advanced rapidly from first-grade to third-grade reading.

The target is:
  - specific: reach fifth-grade reading
  - time-bound: by session five
  - ambitious: advance beyond the current third-grade level
  - credible: grounded in recent demonstrated progress and supported by identified reading strategies

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is accepting an inspiring aspiration as a finished goal without defining success, establishing a deadline, or testing whether the target is credible.

A statement such as “be the best” may express genuine ambition, but it does not yet tell the student:
  - what performance would demonstrate success
  - who or what they are comparing themselves with
  - when the goal should be reached
  - whether the target is realistic given the current starting point

*“Be the best tennis player” remains undefined and untested*

The student identifies a personal aspiration.

> Student: “I want to achieve to be the achieve, to be the best tennis player.”

The Guide responds positively.

> Guide: “Do you want to be the best tennis player? I love that. That’s amazing.”

The Guide asks how the student could pursue the aspiration.

> Guide: “How do you think we can get you to be the best tennis player?”

The student answers:

> Student: “By practicing a lot and by trying your hardest.”

The student also adds:

> Student: “Believing in myself.”

The conversation identifies useful attitudes and practice behaviors, but the goal itself remains uncalibrated.

The meeting does not define:
  - what “best” means
  - the student’s current level
  - a measurable performance target
  - a deadline
  - an achievable intermediate milestone

The aspiration is ambitious, but it is not yet specific or credible enough to guide action or evaluate progress.

A calibrated version might preserve the student’s long-term dream while establishing a nearer target based on current performance, such as a measurable skill, competition result, practice frequency, or coach-verified milestone.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Baseline → Stretch → Specify → Reality-Check process.

### 1. Establish the baseline

Identify the student’s current level using real evidence.

The baseline may come from:
  - a recent score
  - a current streak
  - an application level
  - observed performance
  - prior completed work
  - a repeated behavior

Do not calibrate the target without knowing where the student is starting.

### 2. Add meaningful stretch

Ask what target would require the student to improve rather than simply repeat what they can already do.

The stretch should be large enough to create growth but not so extreme that neither the student nor Guide can explain a credible route to it.

### 3. Make success specific

Define:
  - what the student will accomplish
  - how it will be measured
  - by when

Replace words such as:
  - better
  - more
  - best
  - improve
  - work harder

with observable outcomes.

### 4. Reality-check the target

Ask whether the student has:
  - enough time
  - access to the needed resources
  - a realistic practice opportunity
  - evidence that the target is within reach

Credibility should be tested, not assumed.

### 5. Adjust without shrinking the aspiration

When a goal is not yet credible, preserve the long-term dream and create an intermediate milestone.

The Guide should not respond to an unrealistic goal by removing ambition entirely.

The standard is:

The student leaves with a goal that is difficult enough to require growth, clear enough to measure, and grounded enough to believe.
