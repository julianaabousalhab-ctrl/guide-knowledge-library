# Identify the Conditions Affecting Performance

## Pattern

Guides strengthen reflection and metacognition when they help students identify the conditions that make strong performance more or less likely.

Relevant conditions may include:
  - physical readiness, such as sleep, illness, hunger, or fatigue
  - the learning environment, including noise, crowding, and interruptions
  - emotional states, such as frustration, stress, embarrassment, or discouragement
  - motivation and personal interest
  - social dynamics
  - task timing and subject order
  - the pace at which the student is working

The recurring sequence is:

Notice a change in performance → compare the conditions surrounding stronger and weaker performance → identify the conditions that matter → adjust one condition → observe whether performance changes

The Guide avoids turning one result into a judgment about the student’s character or ability. Instead of concluding that the student is lazy, unfocused, or incapable, the Guide helps the student examine what was happening around and within them when performance changed.

This pattern focuses on identifying the conditions influencing performance. It does not replace accountability. Once the conditions are understood, the student and Guide can decide what the student can change, what support is needed, and what expectations remain fixed.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide helps the student identify noise, crowding, cognitive fatigue, and subject order as performance conditions*

The student begins with a broad statement about distraction.

> Student: “Some days I get distracted kind of, especially last week.”

The Guide asks the student to identify what is happening in the environment when focus decreases.

> Guide: “Tell me, can you tell me so I can understand as your guide, what is you find what do you find distracting in the morning?”

The student compares the quieter part of the day with the later environment.

> Student: “But then like later on during the day close to Kubrick after Kubrick, everyone comes outside in the couch and they start talking about everything, exchanging candies and it gets too noisy and crowded, they start playing fun games and they like I can't concentrate and stuff.”

The Guide then explores whether the performance problem is affected only by the environment.

> Student: “Other times I can just like too tired.”

> Guide: “Like, like the eyes get tired or your brain is like, I'm tired.”

> Student: “No, from like working, I kind of get tired.”

The conversation narrows further to the interaction between cognitive fatigue and subject order.

> Guide: “Which subjects do would you say reading takes you the longest?”

> Student: “Yeah.”

> Guide: “So, if if a subject takes us the longest and our brain at the end of the day is tired, what do you think we should do?”

> Student: “If I do reading last, then I won't have enough time.”

The Guide and student identify multiple conditions affecting performance:
  - the space is quieter earlier and more crowded later
  - conversation and games attract the student’s attention
  - mental energy decreases after extended work
  - reading requires more time and effort than other subjects

The issue is therefore not reduced to “the student gets distracted.” The conversation makes visible when distraction occurs and which conditions increase it.

*2- The student and Guide connect concentration problems to noise, task pace, and frustration*

The student explains that nearby conversation disrupts attention.

> Student: “Yeah, I don't really like it when people talk and I'm trying to focus on them. So like when I'm in class at Leawood, everyone was talking and then I have to like, and then I look and then I don't pay attention to my work and I don't know.”

The Guide acknowledges that the environment affects motivation.

> Guide: “You lose the motivation because it's like, there's no one taking this seriously but me.”

The student then identifies another condition: the pace used during reading.

> Student: “And I also read faster than when I, I think that's the other problem with me and like, the reading thing.”

> Student: “I actually need to go slower than what I need to do and then I get frustrated.”

The student identifies a possible adjustment.

> Student: “So maybe I should do it slower.”

The Guide connects the environmental condition to a possible workspace change.

> Guide: “Yeah, I think having the right environment, the right space, like this, this might be a great space for you to work out of Because it's very zen. It's very relaxed, right?”

The reflection identifies two conditions that interact:
  - external noise pulls the student’s attention away
  - rushing increases frustration during reading

The student can now make a more specific adjustment than simply trying to “focus harder”: move to a quieter environment and slow the reading pace.

*3- The Guide connects late-night work and insufficient sleep to memory, mistakes, and next-day readiness*

The student reflects on the effect of staying awake late.

> Student: “I didn't know staying up could like destroy your memory a little bit.”

The Guide asks what the student could change before the next school week.

> Guide: “So what could you do this Sunday so that you avoid that for next week?”

The student identifies that the work should happen earlier.

> Student: “Do it when I wake up instead of like at 3:00 a. m.”

The Guide connects the late work time to performance during school.

> Guide: “Yeah, yeah, you'd be done but you'd probably be super tired during our actual core skills.”

The Guide then asks the student to identify the condition that would improve Monday readiness.

> Guide: “So on Sunday, what will help you get to um be not tired on Monday?”

The student responds:

> Student: “Maybe like don't go too crazy on stuff. Okay. Go to sleep I guess.”

The Guide summarizes the relationship between sleep and performance.

> Guide: “So a reasonable sleep time will help you avoid some of the mistakes from this week.”

The Guide later connects the pattern across multiple days.

> Guide: “There's been multiple days where you've come in and you said, ‘I'm a little tired,’ right?”

This is strong metacognitive coaching because the student is helped to connect:
  - working at approximately
  - reduced sleep
  - tiredness during core skills
  - memory difficulty
  - increased mistakes

The student leaves with a clearer understanding of the condition affecting performance, not only an instruction to improve the result.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is naming several possible conditions but moving to compliance or effort expectations before determining which condition is actually affecting the student’s performance.

The Guide may ask questions, but the reflection remains incomplete when illness, motivation, distraction, environmental disruption, and technology problems are combined without being separated.

*Illness, motivation, distraction, and application performance remain unresolved before the Guide asks the student to “get on board”*

The student first identifies a physical condition.

> Student: “It's just like I was feeling so sick.”

The Guide asks for clarification.

> Guide: “You're feeling sick? What do you mean feeling sick? Was it like tired?”

The student responds:

> Student: “Feeling sick sick I got a headache.”

Later, the student says the problem may involve motivation or distraction.

> Student: “I don't know if it's like I'm not feeling motivated or I'm getting distracted. I don't know.”

The Guide replies:

> Guide: “It could be, it could be both.”

The student then identifies another possible condition.

> Student: “It's either it doesn't work or it does work, but it's super slow.”

The Guide compares the student with peers.

> Guide: “It's for everybody, but other people are still working.”

The Guide later shifts to the expectation.

> Guide: “What I need you to do is I need you to get on board with what everyone else is doing.”

The Guide does ask questions and considers whether a different workspace might help. However, the conversation does not establish which condition is primarily driving the performance problem:
  - the student is still recovering from illness
  - motivation is low
  - attention is scattered
  - the applications are slow
  - the meeting is occurring during a disrupted week away from the normal campus

Because these conditions are not separated, the final coaching move emphasizes participation without giving the student or Guide a clear understanding of what should be changed.

A stronger reflection would compare the student’s performance when healthy, when applications function normally, and when working in different environments before selecting the response.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Compare → Scan → Name → Test → Learn process.

### 1. Compare stronger and weaker performance

Ask the student to identify:
  - when the task went well
  - when it went poorly
  - what was different between those moments

Conditions are easier to recognize through comparison than through a broad question such as, “Why weren’t you focused?”

### 2. Scan the major condition categories

Explore the categories relevant to the situation:
  - physical readiness
  - environment
  - emotions
  - motivation
  - social dynamics
  - task difficulty
  - pace
  - timing
  - subject order

Do not assume the first condition mentioned is the only one.

### 3. Name the condition precisely

Replace broad labels with observable descriptions.

Instead of:

> “I get distracted.”

Clarify:

> “I lose focus when the room becomes crowded after the break and other students begin playing games.”

Instead of:

> “I am bad at reading.”

Clarify:

> “Reading takes longer when I rush, become frustrated, and work on it after my mental energy is already low.”

### 4. Treat the condition as a working explanation

Avoid presenting an untested theory as fact.

Use the identified condition to form a hypothesis:
  - quieter space may improve attention
  - earlier subject placement may reduce cognitive fatigue
  - slower pacing may improve reading accuracy
  - more sleep may improve memory and readiness

### 5. Change one condition and observe the result

When possible, adjust one meaningful variable.

Then compare:
  - focus
  - accuracy
  - work completed
  - stress
  - independence

before and after the change.

Changing too many conditions at once makes it difficult to learn what actually helped.

### 6. Separate conditions from identity

Do not convert a temporary condition into a fixed label.

The student may be:
  - tired, not lazy
  - distracted by a noisy setting, not incapable of focusing
  - frustrated by rushing, not weak at reading
  - unwell, not uncommitted

The standard is not:

> “The Guide found a reason for weak performance.”

The standard is:

> “The student can recognize the conditions that influence their performance and use that awareness to make a better choice next time.”
