# Diagnose Whether the Barrier Is Strategy, Instructional Fit, or System Failure

## Pattern
Guides strengthen reflection and metacognition when they distinguish among different types of barriers before selecting a response.

A student may struggle because of:
**Strategy:** The student understands the material or task but is using an ineffective approach, such as rushing, skipping directions, or failing to review mistakes.
**Instructional fit:** The student can follow the activity but does not understand the underlying concept, or the explanation does not match how the student needs to learn.
**System failure:** The application, internet connection, account configuration, XP tracking, microphone, or another tool is malfunctioning.

These barriers can produce similar visible outcomes:
  - low XP
  - incomplete work
  - repeated errors
  - slow progress
  - frustration
  - disengagement

The recurring sequence is:

Inspect the evidence → identify what the student can already do → test competing explanations → classify the primary barrier → choose support that matches the diagnosis

The Guide avoids assuming that every low result is caused by effort or strategy. The Guide also avoids treating every difficulty as a technical problem.

This pattern concerns the type of barrier identified. “Observe the student performing the task before diagnosing the problem” concerns the evidence-gathering method used before reaching that conclusion.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide distinguishes successful application use from a failure of instructional fit*

The student can follow the application’s instructions but continues to report that division does not make sense.

> Student: “I still don’t get it okay.”

> Student: “I still don’t know division.”

After observing the student use the application, the Guide distinguishes between completing the activity and understanding the concept.

> Guide: “Because it sounds like you’re not having any trouble with clicking the right button on the app or doing what the app is saying, but at the end... you still don’t understand what division is. Would you agree?”

The Guide identifies the problem as a mismatch between the application’s instruction and the student’s conceptual understanding.

> Guide: “So the app didn’t do a good enough job for you right now to explain division, right?”

The Guide then chooses support that matches that diagnosis.

> Guide: “In the coaching call, I actually want you to tell the coach, hey, can we do some division, not on synthesis, and you explain to me how division works and what it is.”

The student does not need more practice clicking through the same lesson. The student needs a different explanation of the underlying concept.

The Guide therefore diagnoses an instructional-fit barrier, not a lack of effort or a system malfunction.

*2- The Guide creates separate responses for a system defect and insufficient instruction*

The student reports that the questions and instructional material are unclear.

> Student: “They’re not very clear.”

> Student: “Right in the video? Made none of sense.”

The Guide does not immediately decide whether the problem is the application or the student’s understanding.

Instead, the Guide defines two possible diagnoses.

> Guide: “One is I can look at it and see if I agree, and if I agree, I’m gonna give it to our app developers as feedback so they can fix that.”

The Guide then defines the instructional-fit possibility.

> Guide: “Number two is, if it is genuinely something you still don’t understand, it’s not providing enough instruction, we can book a coaching call.”

The response depends on what inspection reveals:
  - if the question or explanation is defective, escalate it to the application developers
  - if the material is functioning but not teaching the student effectively, arrange additional instruction

This is strong diagnosis because the Guide does not collapse two different problems into one vague category of “the app is hard.”

*3- The Guide uses accuracy data and observed work habits to identify a strategy barrier*

The Guide reviews why the student did not receive reading XP.

> Guide: “You know why you got none in reading?”

The Guide points to the student’s accuracy.

> Guide: “Number 57... What’s the goal? Number 80, okay, so you didn’t get enough questions right on your reading app.”

The Guide then compares that result with the student’s more effective behavior on another day.

> Guide: “Something you did really well was you were accurate with them, because I saw you taking notes, and that was really good.”

The Guide asks the student what behaviors will improve the result.

> Guide: “What do we need to do to make sure that happens?”

The student identifies several strategies.

> Student: “Focus.”

> Student: “Don’t get distracted.”

> Student: “Go into the app really quickly.”

The Guide prompts the student to retrieve another strategy already taught.

> Guide: “What are some of the things I’ve been tying to class that could help?”

> Student: “Write notes?”

The Guide makes the note-taking strategy more precise.

> Guide: “Something important that it’s teaching you, like if it’s giving you a lesson about something or if you get something wrong, write a note about what you got wrong and why.”

The evidence points toward a strategy barrier:
  - accuracy is below the required threshold
  - the application is producing visible data
  - the student’s accuracy improves when they take notes and process mistakes carefully

The support therefore focuses on the student’s learning process rather than escalating the application as broken.

*4- The Guide investigates whether missing XP is caused by the system rather than student performance*

The student reports an application problem.

> Student: “The math app is, I don’t know, it’s not giving me XP for it.”

The Guide begins by identifying the exact application.

> Guide: “Which math app?”

The student explains that the application was newly assigned.

> Student: “It’s a new one. I just got it today.”

The student then repeats the problem.

> Student: “I started it today and it’s just not giving me any XP.”

The Guide asks for evidence about how much work the application recorded.

> Guide: “How much have you done in there?”

The student replies:

> Student: “I’m not sure because it’s just not loading.”

The Guide then considers a broader technical condition.

> Guide: “I’ve also heard there’s wifi problems today.”

The student provides a comparison that supports a system explanation.

> Student: “Yesterday when I got home, everything loaded.”

The Guide does not immediately tell the student to work harder or use a different learning strategy.

The available evidence points toward a system or connectivity failure:
  - the application is not loading
  - work volume cannot be viewed
  - XP is not appearing
  - other Wi-Fi problems have been reported
  - the student reports that the material loaded outside the school environment.

The Guide therefore investigates the technical pathway rather than treating the missing XP as proof of weak performance.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is selecting a diagnosis from incomplete dashboard information and presenting it as the cause before confirming what the student actually experienced.

A plausible explanation is not yet a diagnosis.

*The Guide assumes the student missed quizzes and tests before confirming whether those tasks existed*

The Guide reviews the student’s performance data and offers an immediate explanation.

> Guide: “You didn’t master all the units you needed to, you got one out of two. And you only got 60% of XP, it’s probably because you didn’t take the quizzes and tests.”

The student questions the explanation.

> Student: “What quizzes and tests?”

The Guide then acknowledges not knowing exactly what occurred inside the application.

> Guide: “I wasn’t privy to what exactly you were doing, but there must have been some sort of mastery section that you had to prove.”

The student clarifies that the application did not work in the way the Guide assumed.

> Student: “No? Usually we just have like a bunch of lessons.”

The Guide moves from dashboard data to a cause without first determining whether the barrier was:
  - the student’s strategy
  - missing instruction
  - an application rule
  - a system-recording problem

The student is required to correct a diagnosis that was presented without sufficient evidence.

A stronger response would inspect the exact lesson history, ask the student to show the workflow, and verify the application’s completion rules before assigning responsibility.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Evidence → Capability → Classification → Match → Verify process.

### 1. Gather evidence

Inspect the exact:
  - lesson
  - work sample
  - accuracy data
  - XP record
  - error message
  - application screen
  - student demonstration

Do not diagnose from the final result alone.

### 2. Identify what the student can already do

Determine whether the student can:
  - explain the concept
  - follow the application steps
  - read the rejected word
  - apply an effective strategy
  - complete the work when the system functions

This helps rule out incorrect explanations.

### 3. Classify the primary barrier

Ask which category best fits the evidence.

**Strategy barrier**

The task and instruction are available, but the student’s current process is reducing performance.

Possible signs include:
  - rushing
  - skipping directions
  - not reviewing errors
  - using an ineffective sequence
  - failing to apply a known strategy.
    
**Instructional-fit barrier**

The student participates but does not understand what is being taught.

Possible signs include:
  - correct clicking without conceptual understanding
  - repeated confusion after the same explanation
  - improvement when the idea is explained in a different format.

**System-failure barrier**

The tool is preventing valid performance from being recorded or completed.

Possible signs include:
  - missing XP despite completed work
  - failure to load
  - incorrect microphone capture
  - unavailable assignments
  - a problem that changes across devices, locations, or internet connections

More than one barrier may be present. Identify the primary barrier first instead of applying every solution at once.

### 4. Match the response to the diagnosis

Use:
  - strategy coaching for a strategy barrier
  - a different explanation, coach, or instructional format for an instructional-fit barrier
  - technical documentation or escalation for a system failure

Do not prescribe increased effort for a broken system.

Do not escalate the application when the evidence shows that the student is rushing.

Do not repeat the same lesson when the student can complete it but still does not understand the concept.

### 5. Verify the diagnosis

After the intervention, check whether performance changes.

For example:
  - Did accuracy improve after the strategy changed?
  - Did the student explain the concept after receiving different instruction?
  - Did the XP appear after the technical issue was resolved?

The standard is not:

> “The Guide found a possible reason.”

The standard is:

> “The Guide identified the barrier supported by the evidence and selected a response that directly addressed it.”
