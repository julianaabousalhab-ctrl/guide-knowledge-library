# Extract a Reusable Learning Strategy From Experience

## Pattern

Guides strengthen metacognition when they help students turn a specific success, difficulty, or mistake into a strategy that can be used again.

The student moves beyond describing what happened and identifies:
  - what they did
  - how that action affected the result
  - when the strategy would be useful again
  - what they should repeat or change in the next similar situation

The recurring sequence is:

Revisit the experience → identify the action or response → connect it to the result → name the strategy → transfer it to a future situation

A learning strategy is reusable when it is specific enough to guide future behavior.

Examples include:
  - reading carefully before answering
  - double-checking the interpretation of a question
  - continuing after one incorrect answer
  - slowing down to improve accuracy
  - comparing an unfamiliar problem with something already known
  - using notes to preserve important information

This pattern differs from recognizing a specific behavior behind progress. Recognition helps the student understand what they did well. Strategy extraction goes one step further by helping the student intentionally reuse that behavior in another attempt or context.

## Evidence

*1- The student converts previous reading progress into strategies for the next level*

The Guide asks the student to reflect on the area of greatest academic growth.

> Guide: “What do you think your biggest success was academically this past session? What do you think you had the most improved?”

The student identifies reading and describes the progression.

> Student: “I feel like reading because I used to be in first grade reading but then I passed the test and then I passed the second grade test with a perfect score and then now I'm in third grade reading.”

After setting a future reading target, the Guide asks the student to identify strategies that can support continued progress.

> Guide: “What do you think we can do to get there? What steps can we take? What steps can you take specifically?”

The student identifies one strategy.

> Student: “Like, maybe, like, read carefully.”

The Guide asks for another.

> Guide: “Read carefully, that's a good one. What else, [Student]?”

The student adds:

> Student: “And maybe double check your answers. Make sure you interpret the question correctly.”

The conversation turns the student’s previous reading success into three reusable strategies:
  - read carefully
  - double-check answers
  - confirm that the question has been interpreted correctly

The student can apply these strategies to future reading passages rather than treating the earlier success as an isolated result.

*2- The student identifies how one incorrect answer changes their behavior and extracts a persistence strategy*

The Guide notices that reading accuracy has been lower and asks for a strategy.

> Guide: “Your reading accuracy is a little lower. What are some strategies that we can use to make sure we're accurate?”

The student identifies a recurring response to mistakes.

> Student: “When you get one wrong, don't, like, be discouraged, because that's what happens to me.”

The student explains the sequence.

> Student: “When I get one wrong, like, if I got the first one right, I'm like, yes. Then if I get the second one wrong, I, like, zone out.”

The Guide reflects the pattern.

> Guide: “I get it, because it gets frustrating, and you kind of, like, just tune out.”

The Guide and student then identify the replacement strategy.

> Guide: “So we can not get distracted, or not distracted, but not upset that we're, like, We got it wrong. We just keep going.”

> Guide: “That's a very good strategy.”

The student’s experience becomes a reusable strategy:

When one answer is wrong, notice the discouragement and continue instead of mentally leaving the task.

This strategy can be applied beyond the original reading lesson whenever one mistake begins affecting the student’s next attempt.

*3- The Guide uses the previous week’s reading difficulty to establish a strategy the student can apply in future timed and untimed work*

The Guide asks the student to reflect on the previous week.

> Guide: “Let's look back on last week. Look at what could be better and try to figure out how we can fix that.”

The Guide identifies reading as one of the areas that prevented the student from reaching the weekly goal.

> Guide: “In reading what was especially difficult for you in reading?”

The conversation establishes that:
  - the stories were getting longer
  - the words were becoming harder
  - missing a quiz meant losing significant time

The Guide then asks the student to generate a strategy.

> Guide: “What is a strategy this week? What is something we could do this week to help us get our reading goals and stay on track every day.”

The student initially says they should focus. The Guide asks for a more usable version.

> Guide: “It sounds good to focus, but how do we actually do that? How do we actually practice focus?”

> Guide: “What's something specific we can do?”

The student responds:

> Student: “Reading carefully.”

The Guide connects the strategy to a previously learned phrase.

> Guide: “Do you remember what [a previous Guide] said last year?”

The student recalls:

> Student: “Slow means smooth, smooth means fast.”

The Guide explains why the strategy works.

> Guide: “If you go slower and you read the questions carefully and all the information, you're going to remember what you read.”

Later, the Guide adapts the strategy according to whether the task is timed.

> Guide: “If we see a timer on Fast Math, we need to try to be fast.”

> Guide: “And if we don't see a timer, what do we need to do?”

The conversation produces a reusable and conditional strategy:
  - slow down enough to understand and remember
  - use careful reading when accuracy matters
  - adjust pace when a visible timer changes the task demand

The strategy is extracted from the previous week’s difficulty and prepared for use in future work.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is discussing a success or challenge without helping the student identify what produced the result or what should be repeated next time.

The Guide may offer praise or reassurance, but the experience remains an isolated event rather than becoming useful learning.

*The student reports doing well after a workout, but the successful behaviors are not examined*

The Guide asks about a recent workout.

> Guide: “How are you feeling with preparation for Spartan? How are you feeling after yesterday's workout?”

The student responds broadly.

> Student: “I'm doing good.”

The Guide replies with praise.

> Guide: “I know you are. Dude, I'm really impressed. You have thrown yourself entirely into everything, not just academics, but also workshops.”

The recognition is positive, but the Guide does not ask:
  - what the student did during the workout
  - what helped the student perform well
  - how the student responded when the workout became difficult
  - which behavior should be repeated during the next training session

The experience is celebrated without producing a reusable strategy.

A stronger metacognitive response would preserve the praise and ask the student to identify the specific preparation, mindset, pacing, or persistence behavior that contributed to the successful workout.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Experience → Action → Effect → Strategy → Transfer process.

### 1. Select a real experience

Begin with a specific:
  - success
  - setback
  - mistake
  - improvement
  - repeated difficulty

Use something recent enough that the student can remember what happened.

### 2. Identify what the student did

Ask the student to describe the action, choice, or thought process used during the experience.

Move beyond labels such as:
  - focused
  - tried hard
  - did better
  - stayed positive

Clarify the observable behavior.

### 3. Connect the action to the effect

Help the student examine what changed because of that action.

The Guide should avoid claiming causation without evidence, but can help the student form a reasonable explanation.

A useful structure is:

> “When you did ___, what happened next?”

### 4. Name the strategy

Turn the experience into a short, memorable strategy the student can recognize and repeat.

The strategy should describe an action, not a personality trait.

For example:
  - “Read the full question, then check what it is actually asking.”
  - “After one wrong answer, reset before starting the next one.”
  - “Slow down until the work becomes smooth and accurate.”
  - “Compare the unfamiliar problem with something you already understand.”
    
### 5. Transfer it

Ask where the strategy could help next.

The student should identify:
  - another lesson
  - another subject
  - another performance
  - another future situation

where the same approach could be tested.

### 6. Revisit the strategy later

At the next relevant opportunity, ask whether the student used it and what happened.

The standard is not:

> “The student talked about an experience.”

The standard is:

> “The student left the experience with a strategy they can deliberately use again.”
