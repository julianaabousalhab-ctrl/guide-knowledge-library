# Move the Motivational Bar in Both Directions

## Pattern

A motivational target inside an ongoing model is not a fixed setting. Guides who sustain motivation over multiple weeks treat the bar as adjustable in both directions, on a weekly cadence, based on the student's observed response:

- **Raise the bar promptly after success.** Once the student clears the current threshold comfortably, holding it static wastes the momentum; the observed Guides increase the target (or extend it to a new domain) in the next cycle.
- **Shrink or reframe the bar when it discourages.** When the student repeatedly lands short and the gap starts producing discouragement rather than effort, the observed Guides scale the target down, re-anchor it to what the student controls, or reframe attention onto what has already been achieved.

The signal for each direction is behavioral: ease and boredom signal "raise"; fading effort, frustration, or avoidance signal "shrink or reframe." A static bar produces decay in either direction — too easy stops stretching the student, too hard stops feeling reachable.

## Evidence

*1- Raising the bar after success, at the student's own request*

> "The sticker chart will need to be adjusted because it is way too easy for her to accomplish all of them in a week. We are going to make the goals a bit more challenging to push her to the next level of success."

The following week: "Maren's Sticker Chart is being redone so it has a more tangible reward and more challenging parts. Maren even asked for the bar to be raised!"

*2- Scaffolded escalation of the same goal*

> "In academics, he stayed committed to our agreed-upon goal of closing two rings a day, earning 280 XP. While hitting 600 XP by the end of the week remains our long-term target, this scaffolded approach is supporting him well right now. His next goal is to consistently close three rings per day when we return from break."

The progression ran one ring per day → two rings → three rings across successive weeks.

*3- Raising after a crushed goal; shrinking when the metric overwhelms*

After the student hit two triple-digit XP days: "Now that he has shown he can get 2 triple digit XP days, his new goal will be 3 days for next week." Two weeks later, the same Guide moves the bar the other way when the metric becomes counterproductive:

> "XP began to overwhelm Smith again this past week. We're going to shift back to motivation being 100% based on things in his control since his XP doesn't necessarily correlate to the work I see him putting in. Smith's goals will be 20 minutes at least in each app including 40 in ClearFluency."

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is holding the bar static and simply "continuing" the model while the student's response decays — either grinding a student against a target that is too rigorous, or letting a pacing structure convert progress into a visible deficit.

*1- A static team target produces fade-out*

> "continue with team goal of 150xp per day -- Aleina started to fade out towards the end. might be a little too rigorous"

The fade was observed, but the target was continued unchanged rather than rescaled for this student.

Source artifact:[360 Student Review](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1AYEhoKI53HMzroVrMucIZPRpXsADKRg4NuZ9ZaldfUk/edit?usp=drive_link)

*2- A fixed pacing calendar turns progress into discouragement*

> "This sticker chart has been a game changer for Reed to visualize his progress. The calendar has been somewhat helpful, but since Reed keeps going slower than the chart, he gets quite discouraged."

The fixed calendar kept displaying the gap between plan and reality; motivation recovered only when the Guide replaced it with an incremental structure and reframed the goal (Evidence 5).

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Read → Direction → Rescale → Re-anchor process, on a weekly cadence.

### 1. Read the response, not just the result

Each week, look past whether the target was hit to how it was hit: with ease and spare energy, or with fading effort, frustration, or avoidance.

### 2. Choose the direction

- Cleared comfortably → raise the threshold, extend the streak, or expand into a new domain (checks, behavior, workshops).
- Repeatedly missed with discouragement → shrink the threshold, shorten the horizon, or shift the metric to something fully in the student's control (minutes of focused work rather than XP).

### 3. Rescale in one visible step

Make the change explicit with the student so the new bar is understood and agreed — ideally letting the student propose the raise themselves.

### 4. Re-anchor in wins

When moving the bar down or reframing, explicitly name what the student has already achieved so the adjustment reads as strategy, not failure.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The model is still running, so we will continue with it."

The standard is:

> "This week's response told us which direction the bar moves next, and we moved it."

## Confidence: High — 5 instances + 2 anti-pattern cases covering both directions of the bar.
