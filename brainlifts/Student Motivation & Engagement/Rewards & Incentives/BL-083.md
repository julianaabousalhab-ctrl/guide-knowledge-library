# Co-Design Incentives Around Meaningful Progress

## Pattern

Effective incentives are built with the student and earned through observable progress toward the skill, habit, or outcome the student is trying to develop.

The Guide does not begin by selecting a generic prize and then persuading the student to want it. The Guide first clarifies the target, asks what would genuinely matter to the student, and then creates an earning rule that reinforces the desired progress.

A strong incentive contains four aligned elements:

1. **A meaningful target**
   - The incentive supports a real learning, performance, behavioral, or developmental goal.

2. **Student-valued reinforcement**
   - The student has meaningful input into the reward, privilege, theme, or experience.

3. **An observable earning rule**
   - Both people can tell exactly when progress has occurred and when the incentive has been earned.

4. **A fit check**
   - The Guide verifies that the system rewards the intended growth rather than attendance, vague compliance, rushing, or unrelated point accumulation.

Student choice alone is not enough. Letting a student choose a prize does not create a strong motivational system when the earning rule is unclear or disconnected from the intended growth.

This BrainLift focuses on **incentive design**. It is distinct from identifying a student’s general motivational driver, creating an accountability agreement, or dividing a goal into action steps.

## Evidence

*1- The student chooses the reinforcement while the Guide connects it to incremental typing progress*

The Guide introduces a progress chart and gives the student genuine choice over the reinforcement:

> “What would you want these to be, stickers, trading cards, something else. What would you like them to be?”

The student chooses:

> “Basketball cards?”

The Guide then connects smaller rewards to intermediate progress and a larger reward to completion:

> “What if we use the ones that we pick now as these, and you get the full pack of the other ones once you get to the end and you complete that check.”

The Guide explains why the chart is divided into smaller milestones:

> “I think right now you're like, I'm at 11, and I have to get to 35 and that feels so unreachable, right?”

The student agrees:

> “Yeah.”

The Guide reframes the distant target as repeated, visible progress:

> “Just feel like, in a few weeks, I just need to get up to XP or not, words per minute, up two words per minute, up two words per minute, and you'll get there eventually.”

This aligns with the pattern because the reward is selected by the student, the milestones are observable, and the system is tied directly to progress toward completing the typing requirement.

*2- The student helps design the tracker, theme, and mastery-based earning criterion*

The Guide first asks which support format would help:

> “Would it help if you had like a tracker or a sticker chart or anything like that or would you like a calendar?”

The student responds:

> “A calendar and then maybe a sticker chart.”

The Guide then personalizes the system through student choice:

> “What would you like the theme to be?”

The student answers:

> “I like squirrels.”

The conversation moves beyond the appearance of the tracker to the earning rule:

> “And then and then how we uh how will we get the sticker?”

The student proposes:

> “If I master one of these.”

The Guide tests whether the criterion should be mastery or merely a change in the outcome measure:

> “Do you want it to be mastery or do you want it to be that your words per minute increases?”

The student maintains the mastery-based criterion and explains its connection to improvement:

> “Because I think I thought mastery.”

> “Because that also makes your Um words per minute go up.”

The student later explains how the plan continues after the first cycle:

> “So if I finish all of this and my words per minute isn't done, I'd restart and keep going until I get it because I will already know a lot of the letters.”

This aligns with the pattern because the student helps determine the support, personalization, and earning criterion. The Guide also tests whether the criterion reinforces mastery rather than simply rewarding participation.

## Anti-Pattern

*Selecting and defending a presumed reward after the student signals that it does not feel motivating*

The Guide proposes a recurring gift card:

> “So if I were giving you Amazon gift card every week, you wouldn't love school.”

The student directly signals a mismatch:

> “It sounds like bribery.”

Instead of first exploring why the reward feels misaligned, the Guide initially defends the assumption:

> “To me. It sounds like motivation for you. I know what motivates you.”

> “I've always known that either the soccer stuff or money are the things that normally drive you.”

The Guide later softens the position:

> “Okay listen, if you don't want it, cool, you don't want it.”

This recovery is important, but the initial response still illustrates the anti-pattern: relying on prior assumptions and arguing for the reward after the student has said it feels wrong.

A reward is not motivational merely because it is valuable or because it motivated the student previously. The student’s current response is evidence that must shape the design.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use **Target → Value → Rule → Fit → Confirm**.

### 1. Target — Define what the incentive is meant to strengthen

Ask:

> “What specific progress are we trying to help you make?”

Name a skill, behavior, milestone, or consistent habit rather than a vague outcome such as “doing better.”

### 2. Value — Let the student identify meaningful reinforcement

Ask:

> “What would make the progress feel exciting or worth celebrating to you?”

The reinforcement may be an object, privilege, experience, recognition, competition, choice, or visible tracker.

Do not assume that money, food, stickers, free time, or a previously successful reward still matters.

### 3. Rule — Define exactly how it is earned

Ask:

> “What would you need to do for one step on the tracker?”

The earning rule should be visible and difficult to dispute:

- master one lesson
- increase by a defined increment
- complete the agreed practice routine
- demonstrate the replacement behavior
- sustain the target across the agreed period

### 4. Fit — Check what the incentive will actually encourage

Ask:

> “Could someone earn this while rushing, avoiding the hardest work, or missing the real goal?”

Revise any rule that rewards activity without meaningful progress.

### 5. Confirm — Have the student explain the complete system

Ask:

> “Tell me what you are working toward, how you earn each step, and what happens when you reach the target.”

Do not launch the incentive until the student can explain it and says the design feels motivating and fair.
