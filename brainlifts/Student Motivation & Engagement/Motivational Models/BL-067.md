# Turn a Distracting Friendship Into a Joint Contingency

## Pattern

When a peer bond is strong enough to distract students from work, Guides get better results by restructuring the bond into a joint contingency — a shared goal both students must hit to unlock a shared reward — than by relying on separation alone.

The structural features that recur across successful cases:

- **Both students must individually meet the bar** (e.g., each earns a 5-day streak); one student cannot carry the other.
- **The reward is the relationship itself** — a shared lunch, outing, or joint privilege — so the social energy that caused the distraction becomes the thing being earned.
- **Peers begin holding each other accountable**, converting the Guide's enforcement burden into mutual encouragement.

Separation (seat changes) may still be a necessary first step to stop active distraction, but in the observed cases it is a holding action, not the motivational solution. The durable gains came when the same friendship was re-attached to the work through a shared contingency.

## Evidence

*1- A friendship that required a seat change becomes the strongest lever*

The pairing begins as a problem:

> "Will is distracted by sitting too close to Hayden. Seat change is necessary."

Weeks later, the same Guide restructures the bond into a joint streak contingency:

> "When Will and Hayden earn a 5-day streak, they unlock a special best-friend lunch. The streak shows teamwork and self-control. Supporting each other helps them both succeed."

The model persists through a missed day and succeeds:

> "They got their special lunch. Now the stakes have been upped. If they can get another streak, they can add an additional privilege onto their reward!" ... "Their special lunch was the best time for each of them! They also unlocked Fun Friday which made it an extra special Friday!"

The Guide's deep-insight documentation makes the design explicit:

> "While their friendship has sometimes been a distraction in the past, we are now leveraging it as a positive model for motivation and accountability."

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is letting peer connection run unstructured alongside the work — either as an unmanaged distraction, or as a reward that itself generates peer disruption during work time — instead of routing it through a contingency.

*A reward creates unstructured peer interaction during Core Skills*

The Guide's card-printing reward worked individually but leaked into peers' work time because no structure governed the social component:

> "when he gets and laminates his poki cards, he gets excited to show his friends, but he ends up distracting them too much during their core skills time. I am planning to tie this academic goal with his behavioral goal, and state that distracting his peers during core skills time would constitute a yellow card."

Proximity without structure produced distraction and forced a seat change before the joint contingency reclaimed the friendship for the work.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Stabilize → Pair → Structure → Escalate process.

### 1. Stabilize first if needed

If active distraction is harming work, separate seats as a temporary measure — but treat this as containment, not the plan.

### 2. Pair around a bond that already exists

Choose a friendship or team with real pull. The model borrows energy that is already there; it does not manufacture it.

### 3. Structure the contingency

Each student must individually meet a clear bar; the unlock is shared and social (joint lunch, outing, shared privilege). State explicitly that supporting each other is part of how the goal gets met.

### 4. Escalate after success

When the pair or group succeeds, raise the stakes or add privileges to the next round rather than retiring the model — the observed pairs re-engaged immediately for the upgraded reward.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The friends were kept apart so they couldn't distract each other."

The standard is:

> "The friendship itself was placed on the other side of the work, so the students pulled each other toward the goal."
