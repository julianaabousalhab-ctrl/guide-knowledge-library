# Match the Reward to the Student's Demonstrated Value System, Not a Generic Prize

## Pattern

When Guides build a motivational model, the reward works when it is selected from the student's own demonstrated value system — a specific passion, collection, artist, or type of experience the student has shown they care about — rather than a generic prize that any student would accept.

The recurring sequence is:

Learn what the student specifically values → choose a reward only that student would want → attach it to a clear performance bar → verify the response

Two properties recur in the successful cases:

- **Specificity:** the reward belongs to the student's identity (a favorite artist, a collection they maintain, a character they love), not to the general category of "treats."
- **Verified fit:** the Guide confirms the reward's pull by watching the student's response, and treats a flat response as information that the reward is wrong — not that the student is unmotivatable.

Generic rewards (points portals, standard trackers, a second round of the same treat) frequently produce weak or fading responses in the same students who later respond strongly once the reward is matched to something personally meaningful.

## Evidence

*1 — A value-matched reward transforms a student's self-belief about epic days*

The Guide reports discovering the lever through a Limitless Meeting:

> "Elaina and I have seemingly hit the lottery in terms of figuring out what will truly get her motivated to get her epic days. Not too long ago, epic days seemed like far fetched goals, but through our limitless meeting I found out that Elaina absolutely loves the singer Forrest Frank, and was willing to work her absolute hardest to get a Forrest Frank vinyl. Not only did Elaina step it up during core skills time at school, but she even took pride in doing a good job from home. Every morning this week she ran up to me with excitement and said, 'I hit my goal last night!'"

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is defaulting to a generic, off-the-shelf incentive — the standard portal, tracker, or treat — and concluding from a flat response that motivation itself has stalled, instead of concluding that the reward was not matched to the student.

*1- Generic levers fail before a matched lever is found*

> "XP Portal as motivation has not worked for Oliver. ... Nothing has been successful this week."

The same student later responded strongly once the Guide found rewards with personal pull (boba and hot chocolate sprints) — but note that doubling the same generic treat also failed: "4 straight was a second hot chocolate which was not enough to motivate."

Source artifact:[360 Student Review](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1fmBg5PucL3XsPQfS0JgCDgh_EyMIFM7kMBPR9OIoWUE/edit?usp=drive_link)

*2- A level-wide tracker underperforms for an individual student*

> "Will likes the star tracker, but it did not motivate him as much as I had hoped."

The tracker was a shared, generic mechanism; Will's strong responses came later from levers matched to what he specifically values (working with his best friend — see BL-067).

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Inventory → Select → Attach → Verify process.

### 1. Inventory the student's value system

From Deep Insights, Limitless Meetings, and lunches, list what this student demonstrably cares about: collections, artists, characters, experiences, foods, status, or time with specific people. Note explicitly what does *not* move them (e.g., material items an adult at home will simply buy).

### 2. Select a reward only this student would choose

Apply the test the evidence supports: if the reward would work equally well for any student in the level, it is a convenience choice, not a matched choice. Prefer rewards that connect to identity and, where observed, rewards that last over rewards consumed in minutes.

### 3. Attach it to a clear bar

Tie the reward to a specific, observable performance threshold the student understands.

### 4. Verify the response and treat flatness as data

Watch the first week. Excitement, unprompted progress reports, and work beyond required hours confirm fit. A flat response means the reward is wrong — return to step 1 rather than raising the prize's size or repeating it.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The student was offered a reward."

The standard is:

> "The reward was chosen from evidence about this specific student, and the student's response confirmed the match."
