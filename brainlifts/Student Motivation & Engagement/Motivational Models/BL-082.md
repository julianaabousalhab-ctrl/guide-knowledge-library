# Let Students Author the Model — Co-Design, Vote, or Self-Build the Incentive System

## Pattern

A motivational model works harder when the students helped write it. The corpus shows three authorship levels, all with the same rationale — a system students help create converts the incentive from something offered *to* them into a commitment they made:

- **Voted into existence**: level models proposed and adopted through Townhall votes, so the rules are the students' own legislation.
- **Co-designed 1:1**: the Guide brings the structure (checkpoints toward a larger unlock) and the student supplies the content — the theme, the rewards, the milestones — in an explicit "we come up with it together" negotiation.
- **Self-built components**: students design their own trackers and themes, choosing what is measured and how it looks.

Authorship also does diagnostic work: what the student proposes reveals what actually motivates them (see BL-066), and a voted model needs far less enforcement because peers adopted the bar publicly.

## Evidence

*1- A campus whose level models are legislated in Townhall, session after session*

The Palm Beach hub records the origin of model after model in the same way: Extra Q-Break — "Voted on in Town Hall (S2)"; EPIC Parties — "Voted on in Town Hall (S1)"; Super EPIC Surprise — "Voted on in Town Hall (S2)"; 2x Trailblazer — "Voted on in Town Hall (S2)"; EPIC Bets — "Voted on in Town Hall (S3)". The hub explicitly contrasts one guide-authored predecessor ("This was implemented by Guides in S1 prior to the students voting for 2x Trailblazer in Town Hall in S2").

*2- The co-design conversation itself, on record*

A transcript captures a Guide building a model with a student rather than for him:

> "What I would like us to do is we're going to think of a way that we can come up with a big project at the end, but we're going to come up with little checkpoints where we can earn small things working up to a big project."

When the student asks what the reward would be: "We get to come up with that together." The Guide then routes the student's own interests (trains, area codes) into the model's content: "I was wondering if maybe we could come up with like a huge map slash model of the main train systems."

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is delivering a fully guide-authored model to a student who never asked for it — and reading their flat response as unmotivatability rather than as missing authorship.

*Guide-built models produce "no interest" until ownership is offered*

After a student showed no interest in any offered model, the Guide's documented plan reverses the authorship:

> "He has had no interest in a motivational model thus far, but allowing him to feel like he is creating the model, the rules associated and anything involved will give him the power to own it. Deep down, it seems he wants that, and once being upfront about his behavior, I believe he will feel empowered to own his growth."

The student's profile explains why authorship was the missing ingredient: "He likes to play by his rules, rules he creates, or rules that appropriately align to his energy, interests, and being."

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Frame → Invite → Ratify → Enforce-by-reference process.

### 1. Frame the structure, not the content

The Guide owns the skeleton: there will be a goal, checkpoints, and an unlock; the bar must be credible (see BL-069). Bring that — and nothing else — to the table.

### 2. Invite the student (or level) to fill it

For individuals: co-design the theme, milestones, and rewards in conversation ("we get to come up with that together"). For levels: put the proposed model — or its reward — to a Townhall vote, with students free to amend.

### 3. Ratify and record authorship

Write the model down with its origin visible ("voted S3," "designed with [student]"). The record of authorship is part of the model's force.

### 4. Enforce by reference to their rules

When the bar is missed, point to the authorship, not to Guide authority: this is the system you built/voted. Enforcement becomes a reminder of their own commitment.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The Guide designed a motivating system and rolled it out to students."

The standard is:

> "The students can say which parts of the model they wrote or voted for — and when the bar bites, it's their own rule doing the biting."
