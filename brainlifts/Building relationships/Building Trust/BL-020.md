# Establish and Honor Recording Boundaries

## Pattern
Guides build trust when they establish the recording boundary before or during a Limitless Meeting and respond immediately when a student wants the recording paused or the conversation moved off record.

The recurring behaviors are:
  - asking for permission before recording
  - offering the possibility of an off-record conversation
  - treating consent as an ongoing choice rather than a one-time formality
  - honoring a request to pause without requiring the student to justify it

The strongest sequence is:

Ask permission → clarify the boundary → remain alert to changes in comfort → pause or stop when requested

Recording boundaries matter because students may become less honest when they are uncertain whether a sensitive disclosure is being preserved. The Guide protects trust by making it clear that the student can speak up when the recording affects what they are willing to share.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide asks for permission before continuing with the recorded meeting*

At the beginning of the meeting, the Guide asks directly:

> Guide: “Is it okay with you if I record our meeting?”

The student gives a clear response:

> Student: “Yes.”

The Guide acknowledges the answer before continuing:

> Guide: “Okay, cool.”

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide does not treat recording as invisible or automatic. The student is informed that recording is occurring and is given an opportunity to respond before the meeting proceeds.

*2- The Guide immediately honors the student’s request to pause*

Near the end of the meeting, the student interrupts the conversation and asks:

> Student: “Oh my god. Wait, hold. Can I pause this?”

The Guide responds:

> Guide: “Yeah. Okay.”

The Guide does not ask the student to explain why the recording should be paused and does not pressure the student to continue on record.

This is strong evidence of honoring a recording boundary because the student’s request produces an immediate change in the recording condition.

*3- The Guide proactively offers off-record support*

At the end of the meeting, the Guide tells the student:

> Guide: “If you ever just need someone to talk to off record, we don’t have to record. I’ll support you.”

The Guide explicitly separates support from recording. The student is told that access to the Guide does not depend on being willing to have the conversation preserved.

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide establishes an ongoing boundary: the student can seek support and request that the conversation remain off record.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is waiting until the student notices the recording before explaining it.

Even when the recording has a legitimate coaching purpose, explaining it only after the student asks can create uncertainty about whether the student had a meaningful opportunity to consent.

*The recording purpose is explained only after the student asks why recording is occurring*

At approximately 2:40, the transcript records the question:

> “Why are you recording?”

The explanation follows:

> “Because we record... sometimes we record videos and stuff just to help us get better.”

The Guide later adds:

> “So it helps me get better.”

The explanation gives the student useful information about the purpose of recording. However, within the available transcript, that information appears only after the student notices the recording and asks about it.

The weakness is the sequence, not the explanation itself. A stronger approach would establish the recording boundary before the substantive conversation begins.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Permission → Purpose → Options → Pause process.

### Permission

Ask before recording and wait for a clear response.

Suggested coaching language:

> “Is it okay with you if I record this Limitless Meeting?”

Do not treat silence, distraction, or uncertainty as clear agreement.

### Purpose

Briefly explain why the meeting is being recorded.

Suggested coaching language:

> “The recording helps us review the meeting and improve our coaching.”

Only state purposes and access rules that are accurate. Do not promise confidentiality beyond the actual recording policy.

### Options

Tell the student what they can do if part of the conversation feels too sensitive to record.

Suggested coaching language:

> “You can ask me to pause at any point.”

> “We can talk about something off record if you need to.”

This makes the boundary usable rather than symbolic.

### Pause

When the student asks to pause or stop, do it immediately.

After pausing, confirm the next step:

> “It is paused. Tell me when you are ready to continue.”

Before restarting:

> “Are you comfortable with me recording again?”

The Guide should never require the student to disclose the sensitive information first and request privacy afterward. The boundary must be available before the disclosure occurs.
