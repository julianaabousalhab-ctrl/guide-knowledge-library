# Adjust the Guide's Approach When the Student Signals a Mismatch

## Pattern
Guides build trust when they treat a student’s feedback as useful information about the fit of the current support.

A mismatch may involve:

  - an instructional explanation that is not clear
  - a coaching format that is not helping
  - an environment that makes concentration difficult
  - a workload or routine that creates excessive stress
  - a strategy that does not address the student’s actual barrier

The Guide does not automatically abandon the goal or lower the standard. Instead, the Guide changes the route to the goal.

The recurring sequence is:

Student signals that the current approach is not working → Guide investigates the mismatch → Guide changes the support → Guide checks whether the revised approach is a better fit

The key behavior is responsiveness. The student’s feedback produces a visible change in what the Guide does next.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide creates two different support paths after the student reports that the instruction is unclear*

The student asks to give feedback about the reading application and reports that the material is unclear.

> Student: “Can I give you feedback on the whole um, reading movie acts?”

> Student: “They’re not very clear.”

The Guide does not simply tell the student to continue using the same explanation. The Guide creates two different responses depending on the source of the mismatch.

> Guide: “Okay, when that happens again, will you tell me, so I can do two things... One is I can look at it and see if I agree, and if I agree, I’m gonna give it to our app developers as feedback so they can fix that.”

The Guide then identifies a second possibility: the application may be functioning, but the student may need a different instructional explanation.

> Guide: “Number two is, if it is genuinely something you still don’t understand, it’s not providing enough instruction, we can book a coaching call, but please show me, because that’s really valuable feedback.”

The Guide adjusts the response according to the student’s actual barrier:

  - system problem → send feedback to the application team
  - instructional mismatch → arrange additional teaching support

This is adaptive coaching because the Guide does not assume that one solution fits both problems.

*2- The Guide reduces the frequency of a stressful task without removing the long-term goal*

The student communicates that the current experience is creating ongoing stress.

> Student: “I’m mostly always feeling stressed out.”

The Guide first considers pausing the task.

> Guide: “Would you like to take a break from typing and not do it for a couple weeks?”

The conversation then produces a revised approach that preserves progress while reducing the pressure.

> Guide: “So what if we just made typing be once a week for 25 minutes, and so you continue the progress, but you’re not getting stressed out by it? Would that help?”

> Student: “Yeah, okay.”

The Guide does not eliminate the typing goal. The Guide adjusts the frequency and duration so the student can continue progressing without the task dominating the student’s experience.

The revised approach responds directly to the student’s signal that the existing routine is not sustainable.

*3- The Guide changes the learning environment after the student explains that noise disrupts focus*

The student explains how nearby conversations interfere with concentration.

> Student: “Yeah, I don’t really like it when people talk and I’m trying to focus on them. So like when I’m in class... everyone was talking and then I have to like, and then I look and then I don’t pay attention to my work and I don’t know.”

The Guide recognizes that the standard environment is not supporting this student’s focus and offers a different workspace.

> Guide: “So what can we help you with to do that? What subjects in particular can we, you can come back here and work on a certain subject. How quiet and nice is this? You like this room? Yeah, we can have you do that.”

The Guide later makes the adjustment explicit.

> Guide: “This whole campus should feel like a productive workspace for you.”

> Guide: “You say, whatever subject it is, I don’t care if it’s reading or math, I don’t care if it’s writing when you get there. Say, I would like to have some space, do you mind if I use the back room? And we’ll say yes, okay?”

The academic expectation remains unchanged. What changes is the environment in which the student is expected to meet it.

This is a strong example of adjusting the support rather than interpreting the student’s difficulty as a lack of effort.

## Anti-Pattern
The anti-pattern is continuing with the original coaching strategy after the student provides credible information that the strategy does not fit the present situation.

The Guide may listen to the student’s words but fail to let those words change the plan.

*The student reports a physical condition affecting speech, but the Guide continues the same reading strategy*

While the Guide is coaching the student to speak more clearly during a reading activity, the student explains that the current performance may be affected by a recent dental procedure.

> Student: “I think the thing is because I can’t feel my lip. Really? I got numbed. I got two teeth ripped out.”

The Guide briefly confirms the physical condition.

> Guide: “Oh gosh, yeah. You’re right, they’re gone.”

The Guide then dismisses the student’s explanation and returns to the original strategy.

> Guide: “It’s not just today, by the way. I don’t buy this... It’s when you sit up, and here’s something that will help you immediately.”

The Guide continues coaching posture, microphone position, volume, and enunciation without adjusting for the student’s temporary physical limitation.

The problem is not that posture or enunciation could never help. The problem is that the student supplied new information that should have changed the immediate plan.

A more responsive approach would have been to separate:

  - the student’s usual reading pattern
  - the temporary effect of the dental procedure
  - and what could be safely assessed that day

By not making that distinction, the Guide risks communicating that student feedback will be heard but not meaningfully used.

## Reusable Coaching Move
Use the Notice → Diagnose → Adjust → Recheck process.

### Notice the mismatch signal

Pause when the student says that something is:
  - confusing
  - unhelpful
  - uncomfortable
  - too distracting
  - excessively stressful
  - not addressing the real problem.

Do not treat the signal as resistance until the mismatch has been investigated.

### Diagnose what is mismatched

Determine whether the issue is primarily:
  - the strategy
  - the explanation
  - the environment
  - the pace
  - the person providing support
  - the technology
  - the schedule
  - a temporary physical or emotional condition.

The Guide should identify what part is not working before changing the entire plan.

### Adjust one meaningful variable

Change the route while preserving the goal.

Possible adjustments include:
  - changing the workspace
  - changing the coach
  - reducing frequency while maintaining practice
  - replacing a verbal explanation with demonstration
  - separating a system problem from a skill problem
  - postponing an assessment when a temporary condition makes the result unreliable.

The adjustment should respond directly to the mismatch the student identified.

### Recheck the fit

Return to the student after the adjusted approach has been tried.

Check whether:
  - the revised support helped
  - the original barrier is still present
  - a different adjustment is needed
  - the student is now ready for greater independence.

The coaching standard is not merely to ask for feedback. The standard is to let credible feedback change the support.
