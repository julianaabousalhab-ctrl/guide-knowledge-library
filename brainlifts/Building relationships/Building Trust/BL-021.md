# Make the Purpose and Decision Rules Explicit Before Asking for Buy-In

## Pattern
Guides build trust by explaining why an activity, expectation, checkpoint, or decision process exists before asking the student to participate, commit, or make a judgment.

The Guide makes four elements clear when they apply:
  - Purpose: Why are we doing this?
  - Decision rule: How will the decision be made?
  - Boundaries: What is fixed and what can the student influence?
  - Success condition: What must happen before the student can move forward?

The recurring sequence is:

Explain the purpose → clarify the rules and boundaries → check understanding → ask for participation or commitment

This pattern prevents students from experiencing expectations as arbitrary. It also helps them make informed choices because they understand what the process is designed to accomplish and how their input will be used.

## Evidence

*1- Explain the reason for a required checkpoint before asking the student to follow it*

The Guide shows the student a required checkpoint in a budgeting assignment.

> Guide: “Do you see where it says guide signature for completion. You see this, yeah, you have to do the document from the top all the way until here, and then you have to show us that spot before you get to the next section. Does that make sense?”

The student asks why the checkpoint is necessary.

> Student: “Why do you need to show it to us?”

The Guide explains the purpose behind the rule rather than relying only on authority.

> Guide: “Because we want to make sure that you did it correctly before you move on to the next so we don't waste time.”

The Guide then makes the consequence concrete.

> Guide: “Imagine if you did it all wrong and then you moved on, and you did all wrong, and then you moved on. You did it wrong, and you got to the end of the assignment, and then you showed us. And we're like, hey, you know the 10 hours work. You just did it's all wrong. Go back and do it again. Would that be frustrating?”

> Student: “Yeah.”

The Guide clarifies both the rule and its protective purpose:
  - the student must stop at the marked section
  - a Guide checks the work before the student continues
  - the checkpoint exists to prevent the student from investing hours in an incorrect process.

The student is not asked to follow an unexplained requirement. The rationale makes the requirement credible.

*2- Clarify the decision criteria before asking the student to evaluate proposals*

The Guide prepares the student to decide which proposals should reach Town Hall.

> Guide: “Do you remember what we said tells us if topics will be accepted or not?”

The Guide identifies the standard.

> Guide: “Does it make your—does it make the person next to you better, right? And your class overall, right?”

The Guide then separates two decisions that could easily be confused: whether the student personally agrees with a proposal and whether the proposal qualifies for discussion.

> Guide: “You don't have to say whether or not you agree with what they said. You just have to say, is this worth coming up in town?”

The Guide restates the decision rule in more specific terms.

> Guide: “Does this tie to what classroom standards is, classroom culture and classroom procedures?”

The Guide also explains how disagreement should be handled.

> Guide: “So you might completely disagree with the proposal. You can vote that way, but this is just, is it on topic? Does that make sense?”

The student is being asked to exercise judgment, but only after the Guide clarifies:
  - what makes a proposal eligible
  - what the student is deciding at this stage
  - what the student is not deciding yet
  - when personal agreement becomes relevant

This protects the student from making arbitrary or preference-based decisions.

*3- Explain the purpose of Town Hall before asking the student to risk submitting an unpopular idea*

The student is hesitant to submit an idea because it might not pass.

> Student: “I don't think it's gonna pass, though, that's why I'm scared to put it in town hall submission.”

The student explains that public rejection would feel embarrassing.

> Student: “I do want to, but I don't feel like it's gonna pass, and I rather not submit it with—and then, instead of being embarrassed.”

The Guide clarifies that passing is not the only purpose of submitting a proposal.

> Guide: “You don't have to be embarrassed if it doesn't pass.”

> Guide: “The point of town hall is not only for it to pass, it's to see where does the class fall on this.”

After the purpose is clarified, the Guide preserves the student’s choice.

> Guide: “If you would like to submit it, feel free to.”

The student then chooses to participate.

> Student: “Okay, I'll submit it.”

The sequence matters. The Guide does not pressure the student to submit first and explain the process afterward. The Guide clarifies that Town Hall is also a way to gather the class’s perspective, which makes a failed vote less personally threatening.

The student’s buy-in becomes informed rather than compliant.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is asking students to participate in an activity before they understand its purpose, boundaries, or decision rules.

The Guide may eventually explain the activity well, but a late explanation means the student has already been expected to engage without knowing what the work is intended to accomplish.

*The student has already encountered Mini Alpha X but still does not understand what it is for*

The student asks:

> Student: “What exactly do we do in mini Alpha X again?”

The Guide recognizes that the purpose has not been explained clearly enough.

> Guide: “Okay, so this is a thank you for telling me this because I've been thinking about this and I think that [another Guide] and I could definitely do a better job explaining the purpose of mini Alpha X.”

Before explaining, the Guide checks the student’s current understanding.

> Guide: “So, before I tell you, what do you currently understand as like the purpose of the mini Alpha X?”

The student offers an incomplete interpretation.

> Student: “I think the purpose for the mini Alpha X right now is to like find a job that suits you.”

The Guide then clarifies that the activity is broader than choosing a career.

> Guide: “It's a lot of it is discovering things that you're passionate about.”

The Guide’s repair is constructive: they acknowledge the gap, check the student’s understanding, and provide a clearer explanation. However, the student’s question shows that the purpose was not established before the student was expected to participate.

The anti-pattern is therefore the earlier sequence:

Activity introduced → participation expected → student remains unclear → purpose explained later

A stronger sequence would establish the purpose, available choices, and expected outcome before the student begins Mini Alpha X.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Why → Rules → Choice → Check process.

### Explain why

Before introducing the task or expectation, state the problem it is designed to solve or the skill it is designed to build.

The explanation should help the student understand the benefit, not merely repeat that the task is required.

### Clarify the rules

Identify the criteria that will govern the process.

Clarify:
  - what must happen
  - what qualifies
  - what does not qualify
  - who makes the final decision
  - what evidence will be used

### Separate fixed elements from student choice

Tell the student which parts are required and where they retain influence.

For example:
  - the checkpoint may be fixed, while the student can choose how to complete the work
  - the Town Hall eligibility criteria may be fixed, while the student can vote however they choose
  - the learning goal may be fixed, while the project topic remains open

### Check understanding before asking for buy-in

Ask the student to explain the purpose or rule in their own words before requesting commitment.

Do not treat a quick “yes” as sufficient when the process is complex.

### Ask for informed participation

Only after the student understands the purpose and boundaries should the Guide ask them to:
  - commit to the expectation
  - choose an option
  - submit an idea
  - evaluate a proposal
  - begin the activity
    
The standard is not simply that the Guide explained the process. The standard is that the student understood the process before being asked to participate in it.
