# Use Student Knowledge to Personalize Later Coaching

## Pattern
Guides strengthen relationships when they remember what a student has previously shared and use that knowledge to shape later coaching.

Useful student knowledge can include:

  - interests
  - aspirations
  - preferred ways of learning
  - strategies that have worked before
  - experiences that carry personal meaning
  - sources of motivation
  - situations that make the student uncomfortable or disengaged

The pattern is not simply remembering a fact. The remembered information must influence what the Guide does next.

The recurring sequence is:

Learn something meaningful about the student → retain it → bring it into a later conversation → adapt the support, opportunity, or strategy

Strong personalization communicates:

“I listened to you, I remember what matters to you, and I am using that knowledge to support your growth.”

## Evidence

*1- The Guide uses a valued object, a previous poem, and an earlier practice routine to personalize a recording challenge*

The Guide connects the student’s recording challenge to a personally meaningful object.

> Guide: “One thing I want to encourage you to do when we do our recording today, and it's going to be about little T, right? Your T-Rex?”

The Guide then brings forward a poem the student created earlier.

> Guide: “You remember that that poem that you wrote two days ago about little T? I typed it up for you so that it would be easy to read.”

The Guide also recalls a previous practice activity and uses it to structure the new challenge.

> Guide: “We're gonna practice it today with our handwriting and I'm gonna help you practice what to say just like when we did Betty Botter. Do you remember when we did Betty Botter yesterday?”

> Student: “Yeah.”

> Guide: “We'll do it just like that, but with a recording.”

The Guide uses three pieces of prior knowledge:

  - the student’s attachment to the T-Rex
  - the student’s earlier poem
  - a familiar rehearsal routine

The recording task is therefore not presented as a generic performance assignment. It is built around content and a practice method already meaningful to the student.

*2-The Guide transfers a strategy that previously helped the student in piano into typing coaching*

The Guide reminds the student of strategies previously used during piano practice.

> Guide: “What is one of the practice strategies that we've talked about on piano?”

The student does not immediately remember, so the Guide retrieves the prior strategy.

> Guide: “You remember looping it?”

The Guide names several familiar strategies and identifies the one that had been especially important for this student.

> Guide: “Looping it, chunking it, slowing it down.”

> Student: “I remember slowing it down.”

> Guide: “Slowing it down was an important one for you.”

The Guide then applies that known strategy to a different learning context.

> Guide: “That's what we're doing on the typing test. Is a typing test is gonna have you just work on these two keys again and again, till the typing test knows that you're perfect at those keys, and then it's gonna move you on to next key.”

The personalization is not based only on a general principle such as “practice more.” The Guide uses a strategy the student has already experienced in piano to make the typing process more understandable.

The student’s previous learning history becomes a bridge into new coaching.
Source artifact: [Limitless Meeting](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1hC3EtIjIIFgntvGEjaXtMYIIH1nTX0JTeJvpBx1yR34/edit?usp=sharing)

*3- The Guide remembers the student’s career aspiration and connects it to an upcoming learning experience*

The Guide asks whether the student is looking forward to science.

> Guide: “Are you looking forward to science this session?”

The student connects science to a future aspiration.

> Student: “Yes, because I want to be a scientist when I grow up.”

The Guide explicitly shows that this is remembered knowledge rather than new information.

> Guide: “I remember talking about that, that's awesome.”

The Guide then connects the aspiration to specific upcoming experiences.

> Guide: “We're going to be doing different experiments.”

The Guide describes an experiment involving a candle, carbon dioxide, and a small cup acting like a hot-air balloon.

The Guide’s response communicates continuity: the student’s aspiration has not disappeared between meetings. It is being used to make the upcoming science experience more personally relevant.

## Anti-Pattern
The anti-pattern is collecting information about a student but failing to use it accurately when later coaching decisions are made.

This can appear when a Guide:
  - forgets a previously stated preference
  - applies a generic coaching frame that conflicts with what the student has already shared
  - repeatedly asks for information without building on prior answers
  - uses remembered information in a way the student says does not fit
    
*The student has already identified a strong dislike, but the later coaching frame does not fit what the student meant*

The student explains that they had already communicated that escape rooms were not a strength.

> Student: “I already told everyone that the escape room was not my strength. It's literally going to make me scream, get out of my way.”

The Guide responds by placing the experience inside a previously taught “uncomfortable zone” frame.

> Guide: “So it sounds like escape rooms might be a little bit of an uncomfortable zone.”

The student rejects that interpretation.

> Student: “It's just.”

> Guide: “No?”

The student clarifies that the term “uncomfortable” carries a different meaning for them.

> Student: “Also didn't really like that one, because in my opinion, uncomfortable means when somebody's creeping you out.”

Later, the student states a preferred framing.

> Student: “I just like grit. I don't prefer saying the uncomfortable thing.”

The Guide eventually adapts:

> Guide: “You like just calling it having grit through the situation? That still works for sure.”

The repair is useful, but the exchange shows the anti-pattern: prior knowledge that the student strongly disliked the escape-room experience was not initially used to personalize the coaching language.

Instead, the Guide applied a general framework and required the student to correct the fit.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Remember → Connect → Confirm → Apply process.

### Remember

Retain meaningful information from previous conversations.

Prioritize information that could influence future coaching, including:

  - what the student cares about
  - what they want to become
  - what has worked before
  - what has made them disengage
  - how they prefer to receive support
    
### Connect

Bring the remembered information into the later coaching moment.

Make the connection explicit enough that the student can recognize continuity.

Examples of the structure:

> “You mentioned before that this matters to you.”

> “This reminds me of the strategy that worked for you in another subject.”

> “You told me that this type of environment helps you focus.”

### Confirm

Do not assume remembered information is still accurate.

Check whether it still fits.

A student’s interests, goals, and preferences can change.

The Guide should verify the connection before building the full plan around it.

### Apply

Let the student knowledge change something meaningful.

Use it to personalize:
  - the strategy
  - the example
  - the project
  - the resource
  - the level of support
  - the environment
  - the opportunity offered

The standard is not:

> “I remember facts about this student.”

The standard is:

> “What I remember helps me coach this student more effectively today.”
