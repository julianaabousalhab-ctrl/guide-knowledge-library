# Explore the Student's Interests Until They Reveal a Usable Coaching Lever

## Pattern
Guides get to know students by exploring an expressed interest beyond its surface label until they discover something that can improve the student’s learning, motivation, ownership, or growth.

Knowing that a student likes sports, music, reading, technology, animals, or games is not yet a coaching lever. The interest becomes useful when the Guide learns enough to connect it to a real opportunity, such as:
  - choosing work the student is more willing to engage with
  - creating an authentic leadership responsibility
  - identifying a relevant resource
  - making a difficult goal feel personally meaningful
  - designing a next step around an existing strength

The recurring sequence is:

Notice the interest → ask specific follow-up questions → identify what the student values or already knows → connect it to a meaningful action

The Guide does not force every interest into an academic task. The standard is that, when an interest has useful coaching potential, the Guide continues exploring until that potential becomes visible.

## Evidence

*1-The Guide narrows a broad reading preference until it produces a book the student wants to read*

The Guide begins with an open question about the student’s reading preferences.

> Guide: “Have you chosen a book yet? What do you like reading?”

The student identifies two interests.

> Student: “Mystery books.”

> Student: “Sports.”

The Guide combines those interests with the requirements of the student’s reading check.

> Guide: “Mystery books. Sports, sports, okay, would you like to do a realistic fiction sports book?”

The Guide does not stop after identifying the categories. The Guide searches for options and asks the student to evaluate them.

> Guide: “Read this and tell me if you like it.”

The student responds to a basketball-related option.

> Student: “Oh, basketball, yeah... I think I want to do that.”

The Guide then moves toward obtaining the selected book.

> Guide: “Do you prefer paperback or hardcover?”

The interest becomes a usable coaching lever because it directly shapes the student’s next reading task. The Guide moves from a broad preference—sports and mystery—to a specific book the student is willing to read for an academic requirement.

*2- The Guide explores a technology interest until it becomes a structured leadership opportunity*

The Guide does not treat the student’s interest in 3D printing as a general hobby. The Guide asks what an actual student-led club would look like.

> Guide: “So it’s a 3D printing club. You’re the president. Yeah. Okay, you’re the president of the 3D printing club. What does it look like, Do you teach kids about 3D printing? Do you guys print things out together?”

The student starts defining the first session.

> Student: “The first class, I’m gonna like make something.”

The Guide continues probing.

> Guide: “What’s it going to be?”

> Student: “I don’t know, like a basic robot or something.”

The Guide then uncovers relevant prior experience.

> Guide: “That’s cool. You’ve done this before?”

> Student: “Yeah, I have a 3D printer in my house.”

The Guide continues until the interest produces a practical structure:

> Guide: “How many days a week?”

> Student: “Two, Thursday and Tuesday.”

Later, the student distinguishes planning from production.

> Student: “Tuesdays, like, we won’t 3D print anything. We’ll just, like, sculpt or just talk about what we want to do. Thursdays are the days that we’re going to 3D print something.”

The Guide recognizes the emerging structure.

> Guide: “Tuesday could be like a planning day... and then we’re gonna plan out what we’re gonna print on Thursday.”

The student’s interest becomes a coaching lever for planning, communication, leadership, scheduling, and teaching others. The Guide discovers that leverage by asking increasingly specific questions about the student’s knowledge and intended club design.

*3-The Guide explores a music interest until it reveals a resource the student can use independently*

The Guide asks about the student’s recent music experience.

> Guide: “Did you do music this week yet?”

> Student: “Yeah.”

> Guide: “How was it?”

> Student: “It was good. I like my teacher.”

The Guide follows up rather than moving on after learning that the student likes the teacher.

> Guide: “Can you tell me what you guys did together?”

> Guide: “And what’d you work on with him?”

The student identifies the songs and the specific technique being practiced.

> Student: “Working on zombie and the Beatles song.”

> Student: “He said, like, if you have time, practice, like, practice going up and down the strings. Learn how to do it faster.”

The Guide then connects the student’s interest and home practice to a resource that can support independent learning.

> Guide: “Okay, since you’re practicing at home, can you log in and I’m gonna show you something.”

The Guide opens the relevant song inside the music platform.

> Guide: “Now your song was zombie, right? So zombies down here, and you can practice along with it.”

The Guide’s follow-up questions reveal the exact song, instrument practice, and home routine. That information allows the Guide to provide a resource matched to what the student is already motivated to practice.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is allowing an interest conversation to remain at the level of friendly chat when it contains potential coaching value.

Rapport is valuable, and not every personal conversation must become a lesson. The weakness appears when the Guide spends meaningful time on an interest but does not ask what the student enjoys about it, what skills it reveals, or whether it could support a current goal.

*The Guide engages enthusiastically with an interest but then shifts away without uncovering a coaching use*

The Guide asks about the student’s break.

> Guide: “What’d you do?”

The student describes playing outside with friends.

> Student: “We, like, play like football and catch lizards.”

The student then shares detailed enthusiasm about catching geckos, lizards, and discovering many frogs.

The Guide responds positively.

> Guide: “I love that.”

> Guide: “That’s awesome.”

The conversation then shifts directly to session goals.

> Guide: “Um, I didn’t get to do this with you last week, or, sorry, two weeks ago, so I have a question, and that is, what do you want out of session two?”

The interaction builds rapport, but the Guide does not explore:
  - what the student enjoys about catching and observing animals
  - whether the interest reflects curiosity, patience, confidence, or scientific observation
  - whether it could connect to a project, reading choice, workshop, or personal goal

The anti-pattern is not discussing the student’s break. The anti-pattern is stopping at enthusiasm when the conversation offered a possible route into deeper knowledge of the student.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Interest → Meaning → Strength → Application process.

### Identify the specific interest

Move beyond broad labels.

Instead of stopping at “sports,” “music,” or “animals,” determine the specific activity, topic, role, or experience that attracts the student.

### Explore what the student values about it

Find out whether the student is drawn to:
  - competition
  - creativity
  - mastery
  - independence
  - collecting
  - teaching
  - teamwork
  - problem-solving
  - performance
  - learning how something works.

The same stated interest can reveal very different coaching levers for different students.

### Identify the strength or motivation inside the interest

Listen for evidence of:
  - persistence
  - prior knowledge
  - self-directed practice
  - leadership
  - curiosity
  - planning
  - willingness to take risks
  - sustained attention.

This is the point where an interest begins becoming useful for coaching.

### Connect the interest to a real next step

Use the interest only when the connection is authentic.

Possible applications include:
  - selecting a relevant book or project
  - creating a student-led club or responsibility
  - matching the student with a resource
  - connecting a difficult practice to a valued aspiration
  - using an existing strength in a new context

### Keep the student’s ownership intact

The Guide should offer the connection, not impose it.

The student should have space to confirm that the proposed book, project, resource, or responsibility genuinely fits what they care about.

The coaching standard is not simply, “I know what this student likes.”

The standard is:

“I understand this interest well enough to use it in a way that helps this student grow.”
