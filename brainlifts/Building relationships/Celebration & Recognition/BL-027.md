# Connect Recognition to Growth Over Time

## Pattern

Guides deepen recognition when they help the student compare their current performance, behavior, or capability with an earlier starting point.

The Guide does not recognize only what the student achieved today. The Guide makes the change visible by identifying:
  - where the student began
  - what improved
  - how the student progressed
  - what the trajectory suggests about future growth

The recurring sequence is:

Name the earlier starting point → identify the current state → make the change visible → help the student understand what the progress means

This matters because students often focus on the distance remaining rather than the distance already traveled. Connecting recognition to growth over time gives the student evidence that their abilities and habits can develop.

## Evidence

*1- The Guide traces the student’s independence from red to yellow to green*

The Guide begins by identifying the student’s current level.

> Guide: “What I want to tell you is, you're starting this session at like a green plus on independence.”

The Guide then compares that current level with the student’s earlier starting point.

> Guide: “You did a really good job with it. And by the way, I want you to know you got so much better.”

> Guide: “You started the year at like a red on independence, and then you became a yellow, and now you're a green.”

The Guide explicitly celebrates the progress.

> Guide: “And I'm so proud of you for that.”

The Guide then identifies behaviors that contributed to the change.

> Guide: “What you did really well was you took notes, you watched all the videos, you were focused, and when you asked a guide for help... it was literally like, Hey, I actually need support on this. I don't understand this.”

The recognition is powerful because the student is shown a clear progression:

red → yellow → green

The student can see that independence was developed over time rather than treated as a fixed trait.

*2-The Guide uses the student’s movement across reading levels as evidence of growing capability*

The Guide asks the student to identify an area of significant improvement.

> Guide: “What do you think your biggest success was academically this past session? What do you think you had the most improved?”

The student describes the progression.

> Student: “I feel like reading because I used to be in first grade reading but then I passed the test and then I passed the second grade test with a perfect score and then now I'm in third grade reading.”

The Guide helps the student interpret what that change means.

> Guide: “And how quickly that happens, what does that teach you about just the that you're capable of?”

The Guide then connects the demonstrated growth to confidence in further progress.

> Guide: “Does it give you confidence that you can go up more than one grade level by the end of this year?”

The recognition does not stop at “you passed.” It compares the earlier level with the current level and asks the student to see the progression as evidence of capability.

Source artifact: [Limitless Meeting](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1jh_1XwLDFJvOlhPaC4qmLmXaDJB1tXMTgMnDdabDPBs/edit?usp=sharing)

*3-The Guide uses the student’s performance history to make the overall upward trend visible*

The Guide reviews the student’s typing data and points to the earlier starting point.

> Guide: “You started at five, and you see this, you start at two, and you just consistently did that, which is like exactly what we hope for, by the way.”

The Guide acknowledges that individual results may sometimes decrease.

> Guide: “But you're gonna go down. You're gonna have times when you go down.”

The Guide then asks the student to focus on the longer-term trajectory.

> Guide: “But what is the overall trend doing?”

> Guide: “Is it overall going up or going down?”

The student identifies the pattern.

> Student: “It's going up.”

The Guide helps the student distinguish between one disappointing result and the full record of progress.

This recognition is connected to growth over time because the Guide uses multiple data points rather than defining the student by the most recent score.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is celebrating only the current result without helping the student see how it compares with an earlier point.

Current-result praise can feel positive, but it may not help the student understand:
  - how much they have improved
  - whether the result represents growth
  - what the progress proves about their ability to develop
    
*The Guide celebrates current XP and check performance without establishing a growth comparison*

The Guide summarizes the student’s current performance.

> Guide: “So we talked about checks, we talked about XP, you're crushing both of them.”

The Guide adds:

> Guide: “You're so close to getting three checks.”

The recognition is encouraging, but it remains focused on the student’s current position.

The Guide does not compare the current performance with:
  - the student’s previous XP pattern
  - the number of checks completed earlier
  - a past difficulty
  - an earlier level of independence

As a result, the student hears that the current result is strong but is not helped to see a developmental trajectory.

A stronger recognition move would preserve the celebration and add a factual comparison showing what has changed over time.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Then → Now → Change → Meaning process.

### Name the earlier point

Identify a real baseline from prior meetings, student work, or performance data.

Examples include:
  - an earlier skill level
  - a previous behavior rating
  - an old score
  - the student’s former level of independence
  - how the student responded to a similar challenge before

Do not invent a comparison or rely on vague memory.

### Name the current point

Describe the student’s present performance or behavior using specific evidence.

The current point should be directly comparable with the earlier point.

### Make the change visible

State the progression clearly.

Useful structures include:

> “You started at ___, and now you are at ___.”

> “Earlier this year, you needed ___; now you are doing ___ independently.”

> “Your scores still move up and down, but the overall trend has moved from ___ to ___.”

### Explain what the growth means

Help the student interpret the evidence.

The Guide can ask:

> “What does this progress show you about what you are capable of?”

> “What helped you move from the earlier point to where you are now?”

> “Where else could you use the same approach?”

### Keep the comparison factual

Recognition should be grounded in evidence from:
  - prior meetings
  - observed behavior
  - student work
  - recorded data

The standard is not simply:

> “You are doing well now.”

The standard is:

> “You can see how far you have come, and you understand what that growth proves.”
