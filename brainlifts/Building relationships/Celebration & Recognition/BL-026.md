# Recognize the Specific Behavior or Strategy Behind Progress

## Pattern
Guides strengthen celebration and recognition when they name the specific behavior, choice, habit, or strategy that contributed to the student’s progress.

The recognition goes beyond:
  - “Good job.”
  - “You are doing great.”
  - “I am proud of you.”
  - simply repeating a score, XP total, or completed task.

Instead, the Guide helps the student understand:
  - what they did
  - why it worked
  - what they can repeat in the future

The recurring sequence is:

Identify the progress → name the behavior or strategy behind it → connect the behavior to the result → reinforce its continued use

This form of recognition builds a growth mindset because progress is linked to actions the student can control rather than to fixed talent or general praise.

## Evidence

*1-The Guide names the exact study and help-seeking behaviors behind increased independence*

The Guide first identifies the area of progress.

> Guide: “You did a really good job with it. And by the way, I want you to know you got so much better.”

The Guide then explains exactly what the student did well.

> Guide: “Because here's the thing, what you did really well was you took notes, you watched all the videos, you were focused, and when you asked a guide for help, it wasn't like, I'm lazy, so I want you to answer the question. It was literally like, Hey, I actually need support on this. I don't understand this.”

The Guide concludes by connecting those behaviors to the broader area of growth.

> Guide: “I'm so proud of you for the way you're managing your core skills right now.”

The recognition is specific because the student is told which repeatable behaviors demonstrated independence:
  - taking notes
  - watching the instructional videos
  - maintaining focus
  - requesting help when support was genuinely needed

The student is not left to guess why the Guide is proud.

*2-The Guide identifies focused speed—not rushing—as the strategy behind stronger performance*

The student describes working quickly while still thinking carefully.

> Student: “I go fast, but I think about it so I can get a lot.”

The Guide identifies the useful distinction inside the student’s strategy.

> Guide: “That was a really cool thing. You said at the end, you're trying to go fast so you're making sure you're not wasting time, yeah, but you're not saying I'm going fast, as in, I'm not caring about it. You're thinking about it and being focused while you go fast.”

The Guide then links that strategy to the student’s performance.

> Guide: “Well, let me just tell you, it's paying off.”

Later, the Guide connects the student’s increased focus to higher accuracy.

> Guide: “Do you think that's the changes that you just focused on a little bit harder.”

> Guide: “Well, let me tell you, it's working because look at that big 100% you had yesterday, yeah, and look at this 89% from today. So it's working.”

The recognition is useful because the Guide does not praise speed by itself. The Guide identifies the effective strategy as moving efficiently while remaining thoughtful and focused.

That distinction gives the student something specific to repeat.

*3-The Guide names the reasoning strategy used to solve a measurement problem*

The student selects the correct answer to a measurement question.

Instead of stopping at “good job,” the Guide asks the student to explain the reasoning.

> Guide: “You think a finger is between 4 and centimeters? Good job. So yeah, what are you using? Walk me through it. What are you using to kind of deduce?”

The student explains:

> Student: “Well like, inches are like this.”

The Guide then names the strategy.

> Guide: “So you're using like relative measures. You're basically like, okay. And yeah, and you're, and you're comparing the things that you already know.”

The Guide reinforces the specific reasoning process.

> Guide: “That's a really good strategy. That's a very good strategy.”

The Guide recognizes more than the correct answer. The student learns that comparing an unfamiliar measurement with a familiar reference point is an effective strategy.

This recognition makes the successful thinking process visible and reusable.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is recognizing only the outcome or offering broad praise without identifying what the student did to produce the progress.

Outcome recognition can feel encouraging, but it does not teach the student which behavior or strategy should be repeated.

*The Guide celebrates XP and check progress without naming the actions behind it*

The Guide recognizes the student’s XP progress.

> Guide: “You're at 350 out of 600 XP already.”

> Guide: “You're doing great. You're at 59 out of 120 already today.”

Later, the Guide summarizes the student’s progress.

> Guide: “So we talked about checks, we talked about XP, you're crushing both of them.”

The recognition is positive, but it remains focused on the outcomes:
  - XP earned
  - checks completed
  - overall progress

Within these statements, the Guide does not identify whether the progress came from:
  - stronger focus
  - improved accuracy
  - consistent use of resources
  - better time management
  - persistence
  - another repeatable behavior

The student hears that the result is strong but receives limited information about how to reproduce it.

A stronger recognition move would preserve the celebration while naming the observed action that produced the outcome.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Progress → Action → Impact → Repeat process.

### Identify the progress

Name the concrete improvement, success, or completed challenge.

This may include:
  - higher accuracy
  - stronger independence
  - greater persistence
  - improved communication
  - more consistent effort
  - successful completion of a difficult task

### Name the action or strategy

Describe what the student actually did.

Use observable details such as:
  - took notes
  - reread the question
  - slowed down
  - asked for help after trying independently
  - compared the problem with something familiar
  - practiced consistently
  - stayed focused during transitions.

Avoid attributing progress only to being smart, talented, gifted, or naturally good at something.

### Connect the action to the impact

Make the cause-and-effect relationship clear.

Use this structure:

When you [specific behavior], it helped you [specific result].

The connection should be based on evidence from the meeting or observed work.

### Reinforce repeatability

Ask the student to carry the strategy into the next relevant situation.

The student should leave knowing:
  - which behavior worked
  - where else it could help
  - that progress came from something within their control

The standard is not simply:

> “The student knows the Guide is proud.”

The standard is:

> “The student knows exactly what they did well and how to do it again.”
