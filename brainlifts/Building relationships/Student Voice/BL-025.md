# Preserve Student Agency When Advocating or Escalating

## Pattern
Guides preserve student agency when they help students communicate concerns, request changes, set boundaries, or escalate unresolved problems without automatically taking over the conversation.

The Guide may provide:
  - rehearsal
  - wording
  - relevant context
  - an introduction
  - a second voice
  - adult follow-up when the student’s first attempt does not resolve the issue.

The defining behavior is that the student remains meaningfully involved whenever the situation is appropriate for student participation.

The recurring sequence is:

Clarify what the student needs → prepare the student to communicate it → let the student lead → add Guide support only where needed

Preserving agency does not mean leaving the student unsupported. It means using the least amount of adult takeover necessary.

## Evidence

*1-The Guide rehearses the request, but the student remains responsible for communicating it*

The student is organizing a 3D-printing club and needs to coordinate use of a shared printer with other levels.

The Guide makes clear that communicating with the relevant adults is part of the student’s leadership responsibility.

> Guide: “You personally as the president, need to talk to each one of their guides.”

The Guide explains why the conversation is necessary.

> Guide: “You need to find out what their schedule is so that you're not conflicting with their 3d printing schedule because that's but that's that's your responsibility.”

Rather than having the conversation for the student, the Guide rehearses it.

> Guide: “Pretend that I'm [another Guide].”

The student practices the request.

> Student: “I'll be doing a 3D printing club every Tuesday and Thursday and the second half of lunch.”

> Student: “And I want to ask if it's fine with your schedule for 3D printing.”

When the Guide introduces a possible scheduling conflict, the student responds with a compromise.

> Student: “Tuesdays, like, we won't 3D print anything. We'll just, like, sculpt or just talk about what we want to do. Thursdays are the days that we're going to 3D print something.”

The Guide recognizes that the student handled the problem.

> Guide: “Awesome that's what a great answer way to think on your feet.”

The Guide provides rehearsal and access, but the student remains the person responsible for making the request and negotiating the solution.

*2-The Guide positions themself as a second voice only after the student has advocated independently*

The Guide acknowledges that the student already knows how to raise system problems.

> Guide: “I know you know how to ticket and advocate for yourself.”

The Guide does not replace the student as the first point of communication. Instead, the Guide offers additional support if the student’s own advocacy does not receive a response.

> Guide: “But happy to be a second voice if you're ever not hearing back.”

The Guide explains why adult follow-up may sometimes help.

> Guide: “Sometimes they are quicker to respond to guides than kids, so just let me know if I can ever follow back up.”

The student remains the initial advocate. The Guide becomes an escalation partner only when the first route is not working.

This is a strong example of preserving agency because support is available without making the student dependent on the Guide to communicate every concern.

*3-The Guide helps the student prepare language for setting a peer boundary rather than intervening for them*

The student describes a shared arrangement with two peers for pooling earned currency and purchasing items. The student now wants to stop participating and focus on earning independently.

The Guide recommends that the student address the peers directly.

> Guide: “I think the most important thing is from what I can tell you is that you have a conversation with them.”

The Guide helps the student frame the boundary without presenting it as a personal attack.

> Guide: “It's not a personal thing. It's just that you decided to do that. I kind of want to get stuff on my own.”

The Guide also leaves open the possibility of a future arrangement.

> Guide: “So we can talk about it in the future about doing it together again. But I think I want to just focus on getting my own Alpha is my own XP.”

The Guide supplies possible wording, but does not say that they will speak to the peers on the student’s behalf.

The student remains responsible for communicating the decision and maintaining the peer relationship.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is making the Guide the sole advocate for a routine problem before giving the student a meaningful role in documenting, communicating, or following up on it.

Some issues require direct adult intervention. Safety concerns, confidential matters, access permissions, and problems outside the student’s authority may need the Guide to lead.

The weakness appears when the student could appropriately participate, but the Guide immediately takes full ownership of the escalation.

*The Guide takes over a routine system escalation without involving the student in the process*

The student reports that a math application is not awarding XP despite strong accuracy.

> Student: “But it's not giving me any XP when I'm getting 100% accuracy.”

The Guide reviews the available metrics and decides to contact a system representative.

> Guide: “I think that might be, I'll ask [system contact]. I can't tell.”

The Guide later confirms:

> Guide: “I'll talk to [system contact] about it, okay?”

The escalation itself may be necessary because the Guide says the system representative has access to more detailed metrics.

However, within the available exchange, the student is not asked to:
  - document the affected lesson
  - submit or contribute to a ticket
  - identify what evidence should be shared
  - follow up if the issue continues

The Guide becomes the sole owner of the escalation.

A more agency-preserving approach would keep the necessary adult contact while giving the student an appropriate role in preparing or tracking the request.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Clarify → Prepare → Student Leads → Guide Backs Up process.

### Clarify the student’s request

Before escalating, determine:
  - what happened
  - what the student wants changed
  - who has the authority to address it
  - what evidence supports the request

The student should be able to explain the concern in their own words.

### Prepare the student

Help the student decide how to communicate the issue.

Support may include:
  - rehearsing the conversation
  - drafting a message
  - organizing evidence
  - identifying the correct person
  - practicing a respectful boundary statement

The Guide may suggest wording, but the wording should still reflect the student’s actual position.

### Let the student lead when appropriate

The student can:
  - ask the question
  - send the message
  - submit the ticket
  - present the proposal
  - state the boundary

The Guide can remain present without becoming the primary speaker.

### Add support when the student’s authority is insufficient

The Guide should step in when:
  - the student has already tried and received no response
  - an adult decision-maker is required
  - there is a power or safety concern
  - the matter cannot reasonably be resolved by the student alone

When escalating, keep the student informed about what is being communicated and what will happen next.

### Return ownership after the escalation

After the Guide assists, identify the student’s next role.

That might include:
  - checking for a response
  - testing whether the issue was fixed
  - updating the Guide
  - communicating the final outcome to others.

The standard is not that students must handle every problem alone.

The standard is:

The Guide adds power to the student’s voice without unnecessarily replacing it.
