# Ask for the Student's Solution Before Supplying One

## Pattern

Guides strengthen student voice by giving the student the first opportunity to generate a solution before offering advice.

The Guide can still provide structure, challenge weak ideas, or add options. The defining behavior is that the student thinks first.

The recurring sequence is:

Name the challenge → ask the student what they could do → explore the student’s idea → add Guide support only where needed

This pattern matters because the person who generates the plan is more likely to understand it, remember it, and feel ownership over carrying it out.

A student’s first solution does not need to be complete. The Guide’s role is to help refine the student’s thinking rather than replace it.


## Evidence

*1-The Guide asks the student to solve a transition problem before suggesting an environmental adjustment*

The Guide identifies that setup and transition time are reducing the student’s productive learning time.

Instead of immediately assigning a new routine, the Guide asks:

> Guide: “So what can you do to make sure your transition time for setting up in between apps takes less time?”

The student generates an initial solution:

> Student: “Get here early.”

The student then develops the idea further:

> Student: “And then when I like, get to school, if I get here before time, I can just start getting ready.”

The Guide affirms and builds from the student’s solution:

> Guide: “Okay, so before you play ping pong, maybe you set up your space and then you go play ping pong in the morning, or, like, do whatever else. Okay, that’s great.”

The Guide then asks for another student-generated solution:

> Guide: “What will help you transition between your apps quicker?”

The student responds:

> Student: “I have to pause my time.”

Only after the student has proposed solutions does the Guide begin exploring an additional workspace option.

This is strong evidence because the Guide does not begin with, “Here is what you need to do.” The student first identifies actions they can own.

*2-The Guide asks the student to interpret peer feedback before offering a behavioral experiment*

The Guide reviews peer feedback suggesting that teamwork may be an area for growth.

Before offering advice, the Guide asks the student to interpret the feedback:

> Guide: “Do you have any guesses as to why?”

The student responds:

> Student: “No.”

The Guide continues giving the student opportunities to generate their own analysis.

> Guide: “Is there any aspects of teamwork? You feel like you—What do you think you’re doing good in teamwork?”

The student identifies one strength:

> Student: “Well, sometimes I cheer them on, like, when we’re doing like, the bench workouts.”

The Guide then asks about possible improvement:

> Guide: “Is there anything you’d think you could do better in teamwork or in just being a part of your class and community?”

The student again says:

> Student: “You know, no.”

Only after the student has had multiple opportunities to interpret the feedback does the Guide ask permission to contribute an idea:

> Guide: “Can I give you an idea?”

The Guide then proposes creating one positive interaction with a classmate outside the student’s closest friendship group.

This is strong evidence because the Guide does not treat the peer data as permission to prescribe immediately. The student is invited to interpret the evidence, identify strengths, and propose an improvement before the Guide fills the remaining gap.

*3- The Guide asks the student to design the club structure before supplying operational support*

The student wants to lead a 3D-printing club.

Rather than defining the club for the student, the Guide asks:

> Guide: “So it’s a 3D printing club. You’re the president. Yeah. Okay, you’re the president of the 3D printing club. What does it look like, Do you teach kids about 3D printing? Do you guys print things out together?”

The student begins constructing the plan:

> Student: “The first class, I’m gonna like make something.”

The Guide asks for greater specificity:

> Guide: “What’s it going to be?”

> Student: “I don’t know, like a basic robot or something.”

The Guide then asks about frequency:

> Guide: “How many days a week?”

> Student: “Two, Thursday and Tuesday.”

Later, when a scheduling conflict is introduced, the student develops a solution:

> Student: “Tuesdays, like, we won’t 3D print anything. We’ll just, like, sculpt or just talk about what we want to do. Thursdays are the days that we’re going to 3D print something.”

The Guide recognizes the student’s thinking:

> Guide: “Awesome. That’s what—a great answer. Way to think on your feet.”

The Guide later asks:

> Guide: “What do you need from me? How can I help out?”

The student remains the designer of the club while the Guide becomes a source of support.

This evidence aligns with the pattern because the Guide uses questions to draw out the student’s solution before adding logistical guidance.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is answering the student’s problem immediately instead of first asking the student to consider their own options.

The Guide’s answer may be reasonable, but the student loses an opportunity to practice judgment and ownership.
   
*The Guide selects the student’s next check without first exploring the student’s preferences*

The student explains that several checks are nearly complete and asks:

Student: “What should my last one be?”

The Guide immediately answers:

Guide: “The Mount Everest one, I think.”

The Guide then explains that the activity is a Mount Everest simulation and that it will be completed as a Launch.

The recommendation may be practical, but the Guide does not first ask questions such as:
  - which remaining checks interest the student
  - what skill the student wants to develop
  - which check fits the student’s current timeline
  - what option the student is already considering

The missed coaching opportunity is not that the Guide provided advice. It is that the advice arrived before the student was asked to exercise judgment.

A stronger sequence would allow the student to compare the remaining options first, then ask the Guide for feedback.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Ask → Explore → Refine → Support process.

### Ask

Before giving a solution, ask the student what they think they could do.

The question should require an actual decision or idea, not a yes-or-no response.

### Explore

Ask the student to explain how the idea would work.

Clarify:
  - what the student would do
  - why they believe it might help
  - when they would use it
  - what could make it difficult

### Refine

Help the student strengthen the idea without taking ownership away.

The Guide may:
  - ask for more specificity
  - surface a missing constraint
  - compare options
  - ask the student to anticipate what could go wrong

### Support

After the student has generated and examined a solution, the Guide can add:

  - relevant information
  - another option
  - a resource
  - a boundary
  - direct support

When the student genuinely does not know, the Guide can offer a small set of options and return the choice to the student.

The standard is not that Guides never give advice.

The standard is:

The student gets the first meaningful opportunity to think.
