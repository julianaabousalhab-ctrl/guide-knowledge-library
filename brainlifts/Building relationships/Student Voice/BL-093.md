# Close the Loop on Student Voice — Act, Attribute, or Explain

## Pattern

Students stop using their voice when using it visibly changes nothing. Collecting input — through Limitless Meetings, surveys, or Townhall — is only the first half of the practice; the half that sustains voice is what the Guide does next:

1. **Act and attribute** — when student input shapes a decision (a workshop chosen from surveys, a rule adopted from Townhall), make the origin visible: students should see that *their* input produced *this* change.
2. **Explain when you can't act** — infeasible suggestions get a visible answer with the constraint and, where real, a timeline: heard, considered, and here is why not (or not yet).
3. **Honor what was delegated** — a decision genuinely given to students is not revoked after the fact; if a standard constrains their choices, the standard is stated before the decision, not enforced by reversal afterward.

The mechanism: voice is an investment students make only while it pays. Acknowledgment without visible consequence teaches them the channel is decorative; post-hoc reversals of delegated decisions teach them the delegation was decorative.

## Evidence

*1- The loop design: heard → acted → explained, in a visible place*

> "When students encountered barriers to loving school, many did not feel that the existing feedback channels led to meaningful change. ... Feedback shared during Limitless meetings was acknowledged but did not consistently result in visible action. ... As a result, some students became less motivated to voice concerns." → "1) Close the feedback loop. Regularly communicate what feedback was heard, what actions are being taken, and why certain suggestions may not be feasible in a visual place such as a bulletin board... 2) Increase transparency for students. When larger issues can't be solved immediately, share timelines, constraints, and progress updates..."

Source artifact:[Feathers and Fix](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1hmY7E8YIgffFMLlXJtupiWz9CDuxU7rF/view?usp=drive_link)

*2- Ownership valued above the guide's own better idea*

> "When students - particularly this group of students - felt something was being done TO them vs. being created by them, they reacted very strongly with their feedback, despite being bought in." → "For middle school, workshops should be deeply informed by beginning-of-the-year surveys. The benefit we get of 'ownership' / status outweighs any marginal benefit we might get from implementing what we believe to be a world-class workshop."

Source artifact:[Feathers and Fix](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1U2jqp5UFd-B0joVT8za4bWHWIWeehbPB/view?usp=drive_link)

*3- Input made substantive, not consultative*

> "When students were not fully bought into the workshops, their love of school dropped..." → "Allow for more student input into workshop ideas - make the process as collaborative as possible. Allow for students to grapple with their workshop ideas over the course of a few weeks to allow for meaningful reflection and thought processes."

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is granting the decision and then taking it back — or acknowledging input and letting it disappear — so students learn the channel doesn't pay.

*1- Delegated Townhall decisions repeatedly reversed, and engagement died*

The feather's own title records the outcome: "Disinterest in L2 Town Hall due to poor structure and constant guide intervention post student decision."

> "Per instruction to leave Town Hall to the students, guides let the kids come up with their own rules and let them run Town Hall. ... This caused inconsistency for the students and unclear expectations. Some decisions the students made were not up to Alpha standard. As a guide team, we informed the students on several occasions that we would have to reve[rse them]."

The guide's fix confirms the rule: set the standard and decision boundaries in Session 1, then hold them stable — "If the students want to change standards, they can vote on it."

Source artifact:[Feathers and Fix](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1FRVT5PZLSaO1pVY-v_Cn-dwHDZ3gcx8V/view?usp=drive_link)

*2- A guide-made call on something students should have owned*

> "Students need to drive campus-wide initiatives more, not just Mobile Squad choice and TownHall laws but group motivational models and activities. For example, lead guide chose to have the Level Up trophies for session 5 when it should have been brought to TownHall or another discussion format first."

Source artifact:[Feathers and Fix](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1lgPn4H2OHau8RB4So-pHGoDzy4TCIGUi/view?usp=drive_link)

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Collect → Answer → Attribute → Protect process.

### 1. Collect with intent to respond

Only open channels you will answer. Every piece of input gets one of three outcomes: acted on, scheduled with a timeline, or declined with the constraint named.

### 2. Answer visibly, on a cadence

Publish the loop in a consistent place and rhythm (a board, a Monday launch, a Townhall slot): what we heard, what's changing, what isn't and why.

### 3. Attribute the change to the voice

When something students asked for happens, say so explicitly — the connection between their input and the change is the payoff that keeps the channel alive.

### 4. Protect delegated decisions

Before delegating, state the fixed constraints (see BL-021). After delegating, the decision stands; if it produced a real problem, bring it back through the students' own process rather than overriding it. Reversal is the most expensive move in the system — each one taxes every future use of voice.

The coaching standard is not:

> "Students have channels to share feedback and we listen."

The standard is:

> "Every student can point to something that changed because they said it — and no student can point to a decision they were given that was quietly taken back."
