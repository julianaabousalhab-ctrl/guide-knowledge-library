# Guides design the Launch environment — they are not the center of it

## Pattern
Across the strongest Limitless Launches, the Guide's biggest contribution wwas designing the conditions for learning before students started working. Once the activity began, students - not the Guide - drove the learning.

## Evidence
In the House of Cards Launch, after briefly introducing the challenge and establishing the constraints:
> "Our objective is to build the tallest house of cards. You're gonna have five minutes."

The Guide largely stepped back while students drove the learning. Students continuously experimented:
> "Let's just keep on doing the normal base."

Students collaborated:
> "Listen, we have to make two so we can do more stable in the middle."

The Guide's role was limited to brief reinforcements such as:
> "Great idea." and "I see a lot of good communication over here."

This allowed the challenge itself to generate the learning. During the debrief, students — not the Guide — articulated the lesson.

Source artifact: [House of Cards Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1dY1kBuN2iQZF6CN6e-tYMlpJS21YtuDz/view?usp=sharing)

In the Rainbow String Drawing Launch, the Guide designed a collaborative constraint — students could only control the marker by pulling strings — and then let teams discover how to succeed. After explaining the rules, students spent most of the Launch testing strategies:
> "We should make the cloud first."

Observing other groups:
> "Guys, look what they're doing."

The Guide intervened only briefly with comments like:
> "That's a strategy in itself." and "Thank you for being a team player."

before students resumed experimenting. The learning emerged from the constraint itself, culminating in students identifying teamwork, communication, and strategy during the reflection.

Source artifact: [Rainbow String Drawing Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1_M6psUaQCIvxErm5qIpS76ZtUHi0r2gw/view?usp=sharing)

## Anti-Pattern
In the Thanksgiving Launch, the Guide creates a creative scenario (design a product for a bored Thanksgiving family), but does not step back. Instead, the Guide continuously scaffolds, prompts, and narrows the student's thinking throughout the activity.

Before independent work, the Guide repeatedly explains the problem and reminds the student of the constraints instead of letting the student discover them:
> "I need you to design something that's going to keep them engaged.", "They really like music and animals.", "Make sure they're really going to love whatever you design."

During the work period, the Guide frequently interrupts with additional prompts that direct the student's design:
> "Do you have a theme for your product?", "Could you sell this in a store?", "Did you write how much it's going to cost or its name?", "The dad wanted to know, is it for every one of them?"

During the presentation, rather than allowing the student's explanation to stand on its own, the Guide continues to lead the reasoning through a series of guided questions:
> "Tell me about this chicken. How do you think that'll keep them entertained?", "How did you know my family was going to like it?", "How's it helpful?"

Source artifact: [Thanksgiving Launch (Google Drive)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1PPoU-z9KNanyh-N9d-h9z6AWdhtBbnmZ/view?usp=sharing)

## Reusable Coaching Move
Never compete with your activity. If students are looking at you more than they're looking at the challenge, you're teaching too much.
Build the stage. Leave the spotlight.
