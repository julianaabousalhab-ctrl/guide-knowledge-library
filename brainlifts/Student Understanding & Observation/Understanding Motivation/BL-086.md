# Identify the Student’s Motivational Driver Before Selecting a Lever

## Pattern

The Guide identifies what makes effort compelling to the student before choosing a motivational intervention.

An interest answers:

> “What does the student like?”

A motivational driver answers:

> “Why does this student choose to act, persist, or improve?”

Common drivers may include:

- mastery;
- autonomy;
- competition;
- visible progress;
- belonging;
- recognition;
- contribution;
- challenge;
- or external reward.

The same student may respond to different drivers in different contexts.

A reward that once worked may no longer feel motivating. A student who enjoys a particular object may still care more deeply about mastery, choice, or proving personal growth.

This BrainLift focuses on diagnosing the driver. It is distinct from exploring interests and from designing the final incentive system.

Related: This pattern identifies the driver in conversation; BL-070 goes further, decoding the mechanism behind a specific interest and engineering it into the work. Where an interest is already named, prefer BL-070's sequence.

## Evidence

*1- The student’s unusually high performance reveals a need for greater challenge*

The student reacts to a high vocabulary result by saying:

> **Student:** “Vocabulary is too easy.”

The Guide checks whether the student wants greater difficulty:

> **Guide:** “It's too easy? Yeah. Okay, you want to level up in vocab?”

The student responds:

> **Student:** “Yeah, I want you guys to make me fourth grade.”

The Guide probes further:

> **Guide:** “Why? What makes it easy?”

The conversation reveals that more points are not the primary lever.

The student is asking for greater challenge and a level that better reflects their capability. The relevant driver is mastery through appropriately difficult work.

*2- The student selects visible progress but anchors the earning rule in mastery*

The student chooses a calendar and sticker chart:

> **Student:** “A calendar and then maybe a sticker chart.”

When asked how a sticker should be earned, the student answers:

> **Student:** “If I master one of these.”

The Guide checks whether the student prefers mastery or simply a higher performance number:

> **Guide:** “Do you want it to be mastery or do you want it to be that your words per minute increases?”

The student maintains the mastery criterion and explains its purpose:

> **Student:** “Because that also makes your... words per minute go up.”

This aligns with the pattern because the visible tracker is not treated as the motivational driver by itself.

The student reveals that mastering component skills and seeing those gains accumulate is what makes the system meaningful.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is defending an assumed motivator after the student says it feels wrong.

*The Guide relies on prior knowledge instead of the student’s present response*

The Guide proposes a recurring gift card:

> **Guide:** “So if I were giving you Amazon gift card every week, you wouldn't love school.”

The student responds:

> **Student:** “It sounds like bribery.”

Instead of immediately investigating that response, the Guide initially defends the assumption:

> **Guide:** “To me. It sounds like motivation for you. I know what motivates you.”

> **Guide:** “I've always known that either the soccer stuff or money are the things that normally drive you.”

The Guide relies on prior knowledge instead of treating the student’s present reaction as new motivational data.

Knowing what the student likes is not the same as knowing what will currently produce committed action.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use **Behavior → Energy → Why → Match → Confirm**.

### 1. Behavior

Start with a concrete example:

> “Tell me about a time you worked harder than anyone expected.”

Avoid asking only:

> “What motivates you?”

Students may not yet have language for the answer.

### 2. Energy

Ask:

> “What part made you want to keep going?”

Listen for clues such as:

- beating a personal record;
- choosing the method;
- helping someone;
- being publicly recognized;
- mastering something difficult;
- or earning a desired privilege.

### 3. Why

Probe below the first answer:

> “Why did that matter to you?”

A student may initially name a prize but reveal that independence, status, mastery, or connection was the deeper driver.

### 4. Match

Select a lever that corresponds to the driver:

- mastery → levels, demonstrations, or harder challenges;
- autonomy → meaningful choices;
- competition → personal bests or fair contests;
- belonging → team contribution;
- visible progress → trackers and milestones;
- recognition → specific acknowledgment;
- external reward → a clear, proportionate incentive.

### 5. Confirm

Ask:

> “Does this make you want to act, or does it just sound like something adults think should motivate you?”

Treat hesitation, indifference, or discomfort as evidence that the motivational hypothesis needs revision.
