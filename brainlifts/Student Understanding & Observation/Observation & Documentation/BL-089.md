# Verify Off-Task-Looking Behavior Against the Student's Baseline Before Correcting

## Pattern

Behaviors that read as disengagement by default — fidgeting, chatting, flat affect, looking away during feedback — often mean something different for a specific student. Guides who document each student's baseline learn what these signals actually indicate for that student, and check evidence (progress data, known signals, a quick probe) before issuing a correction.

The recurring sequence is:

Notice the behavior → check it against what is known about this student (data or documented baseline) → interpret → only then decide whether to intervene

Two kinds of verification appear in the corpus:

- **Objective data:** checking the student's actual progress (Timeback pace, accuracy) before treating "looks off task" as "is off task."
- **Documented signal maps:** knowing from accumulated observation that, for this student, fidgeting is a self-managed mental break, a flat face can hide excitement, or looking away during feedback is processing rather than dismissal.

The payoff is twofold: unnecessary corrections (which can break trust and backfire) are avoided, and real disengagement is caught more accurately because the Guide knows what genuine off-task looks like for that student.

## Evidence

*1- Progress data checked before correcting apparent goofing off*

> "Oftentimes, Brady will look like he is off task and goofing around with friends, but then I'll check his Timeback and he'll be ahead of schedule for an epic day. ... I believe this is because he knows mentally how much time he can afford to 'waste' not being fully engaged in academics while still getting his normal XP count which is about 160."

The Guide's default action on seeing the behavior is a data check, not a correction.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is correcting the surface behavior immediately, at face value, without checking what it means for this student — which in the observed case produced shutdown rather than refocus.

*An immediate refocus command backfires; a baseline-informed response works*

> "Wyatt likes to play around and pretend that he is confused sometimes when he really isn't. It's tricky. If I immediately try to get him to refocus, he puts his head down and wants to not try. If I play along with his game for too long, he won't think it's important to do core skills at all. I have to find that perfect middle ground where I will play along with his silliness for a second and then encourage him to refocus, and sometimes when I act like I'm going to walk away and help someone else, he will lock in."

The correction that ignores the documented meaning of the behavior (a connection-seeking game) produces disengagement; the response calibrated to the baseline restores focus and, per the Guide, "strengthens our relationship."

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Notice → Check → Interpret → Respond process.

### 1. Notice without acting

Treat an off-task-looking behavior as an observation to log, not yet a violation to correct.

### 2. Check against evidence

Before intervening, consult what exists: the student's live progress data (is the work actually on pace?), and the documented baseline in the student's 360 profile (what has this behavior meant for this student before?). If neither exists, run a low-stakes probe (a light check-in question) instead of a command.

### 3. Interpret for this student

Classify the behavior: self-regulation (mental break, sensory reset), processing (pause during feedback), masked engagement (flat affect), connection-seeking (playful stalling), or genuine disengagement. Document the interpretation in the student's profile so it compounds.

### 4. Respond to the meaning, not the surface

Self-regulation → allow it; processing → give the pause; masked engagement → use known buy-in signals or check-ins; connection-seeking → brief connection, then a calibrated redirect; genuine disengagement → coach it directly.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The Guide redirected the student as soon as they looked off task."

The standard is:

> "The Guide knew — from data or the student's documented baseline — what the behavior meant before deciding whether and how to intervene."

## Confidence: High — 4 instances + 1 anti-pattern contrast (immediate correction backfires; baseline-informed response works).
