# Put Struggling on a Clock — Fixed-Cadence Data Review, Numeric Flags, and a Timed Intervention Cycle

## Pattern

Students who end the year under 2x rarely collapse suddenly — they drift there while their daily data quietly signals risk. Where intervention timing depends on a Guide happening to notice, or on the student asking for help, support arrives late. Five guides across four campuses converged on the same replacement: make detection and response run on a clock instead of on attention.

1. **A fixed review cadence** — the Guide reviews the group's data (XP, accuracy, attendance, mastery results, avoidance patterns) on a set schedule, starting early (Week 3, not mid-session).
2. **Numeric flag triggers** — pre-defined thresholds convert data into action automatically: two failed mastery tests; a subject below pace two consecutive weeks; projected <2x growth; avoidance persisting past an agreed threshold. No judgment call required to open the case.
3. **A timed intervention cycle** — each flagged student gets one specific intervention with a review date; at review, the Guide continues, modifies, or stops it based on results — and escalates (to leadership or academics) when the pattern doesn't change.

This is the individual, leading-indicator counterpart to BL-090's cohort trigger: BL-090 fires when many students fail one assessment; BL-096 fires when one student's daily data drifts, weeks before any assessment records the damage.

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is attention-based detection — noticing when you notice — with the cost recorded in the same guides' own retrospectives.

*The guide's own accounting of late detection*

> "Logan did not really try on his last MAP tests once he knew he was not returning to Alpha, Ginny was present for only about a third of the school year, and Zarielle did not start pushing hard toward short-term goals until later in the year, and I did not always catch or respond to those patterns early enough to give them a better shot at 2x."

The same failure mode with its downstream damage: "When Liv's academic output began falling below expectations, the intervention cycle was not fast or targeted enough, so low math growth and negative science growth continued." And its behavioral cost: "Repeated experiences of challenge without timely academic intervention or opportunities for success led students to develop academic avoidance behaviors."

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Cadence → Threshold → Cycle → Escalate process.

### 1. Set the cadence before the session starts

Put the group data review on the calendar (weekly, same day), with the first risk pass no later than Week 3. The review happens whether or not anything feels wrong — that's the point.

### 2. Write the thresholds down

Define in advance what opens a case: e.g., two failed mastery tests; one subject below pace two consecutive weeks; attendance below X; avoidance recurring in one content area. A flag is a trigger, not an accusation.

### 3. Run one intervention per flag, with a review date

Diagnose the barrier (BL-041), assign one specific intervention, and set the date it will be judged. At review: continue, modify, or stop — never let an intervention run unexamined.

### 4. Escalate on unchanged patterns

If two cycles pass without movement, the case leaves the Guide's sole ownership: loop in the lead guide or academics with the documented history (then track that commitment per BL-043).

The coaching standard is not:

> "The Guide keeps a close eye on the students and steps in when someone is struggling."

The standard is:

> "Every student's data gets reviewed on a schedule, defined thresholds open cases automatically, and every open case has an intervention with a date it will be judged."
