# Write the Student's Feedback Protocol Into the Profile — Tested Delivery, Not Just Description

## Pattern

A profile that only describes the student ("perfectionist," "sensitive to feedback") leaves every adult to rediscover, by trial and error, how coaching actually lands for this kid. The stronger practice treats feedback delivery as a per-student protocol: tested in real corrections, written into the profile as operating instructions, and specific enough that any adult could follow it.

A complete feedback protocol records three things:

1. **The delivery that works** — tone, privacy, dosage, timing ("Feedback style: Private, specific, and evidence-based... Correction approach: Gentle, concrete, future-focused, never shaming"), and the mentor style the student responds to ("energetic, structured, performance-focused").
2. **The tested sequence** — moves discovered through real corrections, not inferred from personality: letting a student test her own approach first and revisiting the feedback immediately after it fails; running a feelings check before any feedback conversation so the student can hear it.
3. **What backfires** — the default deliveries this student rejects (long explanations, public correction, feedback delivered mid-frustration), stated as plainly as what works.

The mechanism: how feedback is delivered determines whether it is received at all — and delivery knowledge is exactly the kind that dies with one guide unless the profile carries it.

## Evidence

*1- A calibration discovered through repeated corrections, written as a protocol*

The observed behavior: "When Payton receives feedback, she often questions it and continues using the approach she believes is correct. It typically takes consistent coaching over time before she accepts and applies the feedback." The tested protocol that emerged:

> "Payton responds best to feedback that is clear, concise, and consistent. When feedback includes too much information, it can become overwhelming and lose its effectiveness. One strategy that has been particularly successful is allowing her to test her own approach first. When she is able to see through experience that her strategy is not producing the desired outcome, she becomes more open to considering an alternative. Immediately following that experience, it is important to revisit the same f[eedback]."

## Anti-Pattern

The anti-pattern is delivering feedback in the adult's default style — full explanation, in the moment, however the student is feeling — and reading the rejection as the student's receiving-feedback deficit rather than a delivery mismatch.

*Default delivery, repeated rejection, eroding relationships*

Payton's record (Evidence 1) shows the cost of the default: corrections "often question[ed]" and re-litigated, acceptance taking "consistent coaching over time" — until the delivery changed, not the student. Elliott's record shows the higher-stakes version:

> "because feedback about it overwhelmed him emotionally, he kept avoiding the conversations that could have repaired those relationships, so he stayed on the outside of the community instead of rebuilding trust session after session."

In both cases the breakthrough was a delivery protocol, discovered late; nothing in either record shows it written anywhere another adult would find it before this pattern was extracted.

## Reusable Coaching Move

Use the Test → Codify → Share → Update process.

### 1. Test deliveries deliberately

When corrections keep bouncing off a student, vary the delivery as the experiment: dosage (one sentence vs. full reasoning), timing (immediately vs. after regulation vs. after their approach fails), privacy, and framing. Note what changed reception.

### 2. Codify the protocol in the profile

Write the working delivery into a standing profile section in executable terms: tone, timing, privacy, praise approach, correction approach, the pre-step if one exists (regulation check, let-them-test-first) — and what never works.

### 3. Share it with every adult

The protocol is for the whole staff, not the author: workshop leaders, substitutes, and next year's guide should correct this student the way that works on day one, not month three (see BL-095 for keeping the standard itself consistent).

### 4. Update it when reception shifts

Reread the protocol when a correction unexpectedly bounces; students outgrow their protocols the way they outgrow their motivators.

The coaching standard is not:

> "The Guide knows this student is sensitive to feedback and is careful with them."

The standard is:

> "The profile states, in executable steps, how feedback lands for this student and what backfires — and an adult who has never met the kid could deliver a correction the right way on the first try."
